# Vol 3 Chapter 5 — Halo Nuclei: Dripline Structure

## Headline result

Halo nuclei (Li-11, Be-11, B-17, etc.) are exotic neutron-rich nuclei near the neutron dripline that feature a "halo" of loosely bound neutrons extending far beyond standard nuclear-shell radius. The halo phenomenon was discovered in the 1980s and remains an active research domain (RIBF at RIKEN, FRIB at Michigan State, RAON in Korea).

### Level 1 — Bright 5th-grader

> Most nuclei are roughly spherical, with neutrons and protons packed close together. But some nuclei have a "halo" — one or two neutrons that orbit far from the rest of the nucleus, like a moon orbiting at a great distance from a planet. Lithium-11 is the most famous example. BST predicts WHY these halos exist using the same shell-model framework that gives magic numbers. The neutrons in halos are barely bound — they live at the edge of nuclear stability.
