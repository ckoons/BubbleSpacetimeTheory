# Chapter 8 — Monster, Moonshine, and Supersingular Primes

The Monster group $\mathbb{M}$ — the largest sporadic simple group, order ~ $8 \times 10^{53}$ — was constructed Griess 1982 and conjectured to exist by Fischer and Griess 1973. Conway and Norton's 1979 "monstrous moonshine" conjecture connected the Monster to modular forms (the $j$-invariant); Borcherds proved the conjecture 1992 (Fields Medal).

In BST, the Monster is a convergence hub. The 15 Ogg supersingular primes (Ogg 1975, L1 ESTABLISHED) are exactly the primes dividing $|\mathbb{M}|$, providing a deep connection.

## 8.1 The Monster

The Monster $\mathbb{M}$ has order $2^{46} \cdot 3^{20} \cdot 5^9 \cdot 7^6 \cdot 11^2 \cdot 13^3 \cdot 17 \cdot 19 \cdot 23 \cdot 29 \cdot 31 \cdot 41 \cdot 47 \cdot 59 \cdot 71$. The 15 prime factors are the Ogg supersingular primes.

## 8.2 Monstrous moonshine

The $j$-function coefficients $a_n$ are sums of dimensions of irreducible Monster representations. The first non-trivial coefficient: $a_1 = 196884 = 1 + 196883$ where 196883 is the smallest non-trivial Monster irrep.

## 8.3 BST reading

In BST, the Monster is a convergence hub for L1 sources (Ogg 1975 supersingular primes + Borcherds 1992 mechanism + Conway 1968 moonshine). BST does not require the Monster as L1 source itself; the Monster emerges as a confluence of L1 ESTABLISHED structure.
