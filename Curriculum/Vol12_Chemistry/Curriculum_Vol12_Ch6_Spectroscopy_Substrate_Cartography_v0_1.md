# Vol 12 Chapter 6 — Spectroscopy (UV-Vis + IR + NMR + X-ray) + Substrate Cartography

## Chapter motivation

Spectroscopy is chemistry's primary observational tool: probe matter with electromagnetic radiation across the spectrum (X-ray through radio) and read substrate structure from the absorption/emission/scattering response. Each technique probes a different energy scale: X-ray (10²-10⁴ eV) → electron density (crystallography); UV-Vis (1-10 eV) → electronic transitions; IR (0.01-1 eV) → vibrational transitions; Raman (vibrational, with shifted frequency) → vibrational + rotational; NMR (10⁻⁷-10⁻⁶ eV) → nuclear spin in magnetic field. Standard chemistry treatment: Beer-Lambert law A = εcl; selection rules from quantum mechanics (Δl = ±1 electric dipole; Δv = ±1 vibrational harmonic; Δm_I = ±1 NMR); transition strengths from matrix elements. BST substrate-cartography reading: spectroscopy IS substrate-readout — selection rules are substrate K-type representation transitions; transition strengths follow α^{BST primary} pattern per T2476 (substrate fine-structure constant emerges in transition matrix elements); X-ray diffraction reads substrate periodic structure tied to BST primary integer ladder.

## Section 6.1 — UV-Vis Spectroscopy (Electronic Transitions)

Beer-Lambert law: A = εcl (absorbance = molar absorptivity × concentration × path length).

Electronic transitions: σ → σ* (vacuum UV ~200 nm), n → π* (300-400 nm common), π → π* (200-300 nm common), d → d (transition metal colors, visible).

Selection rules: Δl = ±1 (electric dipole), ΔS = 0 (spin-allowed), Laporte g ↔ u (parity).

BST framing: electronic K-type transitions between Wallach K-type representations on D_IV⁵ at electronic energy scale.

## Section 6.2 — IR Spectroscopy (Vibrational Transitions)

Selection rule Δv = ±1 (harmonic oscillator); overtones Δv = ±2, ±3 (anharmonic).

Characteristic frequencies provide functional group identification:
- C-H stretch: 2850-3300 cm⁻¹
- C=O stretch: 1650-1850 cm⁻¹
- O-H stretch: 3200-3650 cm⁻¹ (broad if H-bonded)
- N-H stretch: 3300-3500 cm⁻¹

Fingerprint region 600-1500 cm⁻¹ used for compound identification.

BST framing: vibrational K-types as substrate sub-structure modes; vibrational frequencies = substrate K-type Casimir-eigenvalue differences at vibrational scale.

## Section 6.3 — Raman Spectroscopy

Inelastic photon scattering: Stokes shift (Δv = +1, photon loses energy) and anti-Stokes shift (Δv = −1, photon gains energy).

Complementary to IR: Raman requires polarizability change (centrosymmetric vibrations); IR requires dipole change (asymmetric vibrations). Centrosymmetric molecules (e.g., CO₂ symmetric stretch) show in Raman but not IR.

BST framing: Raman probes substrate polarizability-active K-types; IR probes substrate dipole-active K-types; together complete vibrational K-type spectroscopy.

## Section 6.4 — NMR Spectroscopy (Nuclear Spin)

Nuclear spin in applied magnetic field B₀ splits into 2I+1 levels (Zeeman effect). For I = ½ (¹H, ¹³C, ³¹P), two levels separated by ΔE = γħB₀ where γ is gyromagnetic ratio.

Chemical shift δ (ppm) probes local electron environment via shielding constant σ. J-coupling (Hz) probes through-bond connectivity (typically 1-15 Hz for ¹H-¹H).

BST framing: nuclear spin K-types as substrate ½-integer representations; chemical shifts read substrate sub-structure electronic environment.

## Section 6.5 — X-Ray Crystallography

Bragg's law: nλ = 2d sin θ relates X-ray wavelength + diffraction angle to lattice spacing d.

Full 3D structure determination from diffraction intensities I(hkl) via Fourier transform to electron density ρ(r). Phase problem resolved by isomorphous replacement, anomalous dispersion, or direct methods (Hauptman-Karle Nobel 1985).

BST framing: X-ray diffraction reads substrate periodic lattice directly; lattice spacings emerge from BST primary integer structure (Vol 11 Generative Geometry + Topology cross-link).
