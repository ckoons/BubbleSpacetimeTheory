# Chapter 10 — Electromagnetism in Matter

Electromagnetism in matter — dielectrics, magnetics, conductors, semiconductors, plasmas — is the working physicist's bread and butter. The constitutive relations $\vec{D} = \epsilon \vec{E}$, $\vec{B} = \mu \vec{H}$, $\vec{J} = \sigma \vec{E}$ summarize material response.

In BST, EM in matter is the substrate's $U(1)_{em}$ bundle response in atomic-scale K-type configurations corresponding to specific material compositions.

## 10.1 Dielectric response

Dielectric materials polarize under applied $\vec{E}$, producing displacement $\vec{D} = \epsilon_0 \vec{E} + \vec{P}$. The dielectric constant $\epsilon_r$ characterizes the material's response.

Substrate-mechanism: atomic K-types polarize under substrate $U(1)_{em}$ field, with $\epsilon_r$ set by the atomic structure (Volume 3 Chapter 7 orbital sequence).

## 10.2 Magnetic response

Magnetic materials magnetize under applied $\vec{H}$, producing $\vec{B} = \mu_0(\vec{H} + \vec{M})$. The permeability $\mu_r$ characterizes the magnetic response.

Substrate-mechanism: atomic spin K-types align in response to substrate magnetic field, with $\mu_r$ set by the substrate's natural spin structure at the relevant atomic K-types.

## 10.3 Conductors and Ohm's law

For ohmic materials, $\vec{J} = \sigma \vec{E}$ with $\sigma$ the conductivity. Substrate-mechanism: in conductors, electron K-types are delocalized across the substrate's atomic lattice; the conductivity follows from substrate-mediated electron transport.

## 10.4 Optical properties

Refractive index $n = \sqrt{\epsilon_r \mu_r}$ governs light propagation in matter. Dispersion ($n$ varying with frequency), absorption, birefringence — all follow from substrate K-type response at the relevant frequencies.

## 10.5 Frequency-dependent response

Materials respond differently at different frequencies. The Lorentz oscillator model gives the standard frequency-dependent permittivity; substrate-mechanism: substrate K-type resonances drive the standard Lorentz response.
