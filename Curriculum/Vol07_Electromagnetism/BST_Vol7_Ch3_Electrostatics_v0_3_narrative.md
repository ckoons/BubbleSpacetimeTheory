# Chapter 3 — Electrostatics

Electrostatics — the physics of static electric charges and the fields they produce — is the simplest sector of electromagnetism. Coulomb's law, Gauss's law, the electric potential, conductors and dielectrics, boundary-value problems — the standard apparatus that physics undergraduates learn in their first electromagnetism course.

In BST, electrostatics is the substrate's static-charge configuration in the $U(1)_{em}$ sector, with the standard apparatus following directly.

## 3.1 Coulomb's law and the electric potential

Two static point charges $q_1, q_2$ separated by distance $r$ exert force $F = k q_1 q_2 / r^2$ on each other, with $k = 1/(4\pi\epsilon_0)$. The electric potential is $\phi(r) = k q / r$.

Substrate-mechanism: the electric force is the substrate's $U(1)_{em}$ Bergman-kernel propagator evaluated between two charged states. The $1/r$ falloff is the substrate's Bergman-kernel asymptotic structure on the substrate's coset geometry.

## 3.2 Gauss's law and field calculations

The integral form of Gauss's law $\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = Q_{enc}/\epsilon_0$ enables field calculations for symmetric charge distributions. Standard apparatus — spherical, cylindrical, planar — applies on the substrate without modification.

## 3.3 Conductors

A perfect conductor has zero electric field inside; charges redistribute on the surface to cancel any imposed field. Substrate-mechanism: conductor surfaces are substrate Shilov-boundary configurations where the charged K-types redistribute to zero internal field.

## 3.4 Dielectrics

Dielectric materials have bound charges that polarize under applied fields. The dielectric constant $\epsilon_r$ relates the substrate's substrate-vacuum permittivity to the material's effective permittivity.
