## Level 2 — graduate-physicist precision

### 7.1 The 4-current and 4-potential

In 4-vector notation:

- **Position**: $x^\mu = (ct, \vec r)$
- **4-current**: $J^\mu = (c\rho, \vec J)$ — conservation $\partial_\mu J^\mu = 0$
- **4-potential**: $A^\mu = (\phi/c, \vec A)$
- **Field tensor**: $F^{\mu\nu} = \partial^\mu A^\nu - \partial^\nu A^\mu$

Maxwell's equations (Chapter 2 Section 2.2):

$$\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 J^\nu, \quad \partial_{[\mu} F_{\nu\rho]} = 0$$

These are *manifestly* covariant (each side transforms the same way under Lorentz). Coulomb gauge fixing: $\partial_\mu A^\mu = 0$.

### 7.2 The field tensor under Lorentz boosts

For a boost with velocity $v$ along $\hat x$:

$$E'_x = E_x, \quad E'_y = \gamma(E_y - v B_z), \quad E'_z = \gamma(E_z + v B_y)$$

$$B'_x = B_x, \quad B'_y = \gamma(B_y + (v/c^2) E_z), \quad B'_z = \gamma(B_z - (v/c^2) E_y)$$

So $\vec E$ and $\vec B$ mix under boost. There's no Lorentz-invariant separation between "electric" and "magnetic" — they're components of the unified field tensor $F^{\mu\nu}$.

Lorentz invariants:
- $F^{\mu\nu} F_{\mu\nu} = 2(B^2 - E^2/c^2)$
- $F^{\mu\nu} \tilde F_{\mu\nu} = -4 \vec E \cdot \vec B / c$

Both are unchanged by Lorentz transformations.

### 7.4 Relativistic dynamics: charged particle in EM

Lagrangian for charged particle in EM:

$$L = -mc^2/\gamma - q\phi + q\vec v \cdot \vec A$$

(non-relativistic limit: $L = (1/2)mv^2 - q\phi + q\vec v \cdot \vec A$).

Relativistic equation of motion:

$$\frac{dp^\mu}{d\tau} = qF^{\mu\nu} u_\nu$$

with $u^\mu = dx^\mu/d\tau$ the 4-velocity. Reduces to Lorentz force law in 3-vector form.

### 7.5 Doppler effect and aberration

For a source emitting at frequency $\omega_0$ moving with velocity $\vec v$ relative to observer:

**Longitudinal Doppler**: $\omega = \omega_0 \sqrt{(1-\beta)/(1+\beta)}$ for recession; reciprocal for approach.

**Transverse Doppler** (purely relativistic): even at 90° relative motion, $\omega = \omega_0 / \gamma$. No classical analog.

**Aberration**: light from a star displaces by angle $\alpha = v/c$ for stellar aberration (used by Bradley 1729 to first measure $c$).

Substrate reading: Doppler is the substrate's K-type frequency shift under boost; aberration is the substrate's K-type angular shift. Both inherit from $SO(4,2)$ symmetry of the substrate $U(1)$ gauge sector.

### 7.7 Worked example: Lorentz contraction of a charged rod

A rod of rest length $L_0$ with linear charge density $\lambda_0$ in its rest frame. Total charge $Q = \lambda_0 L_0$.

In a frame moving with velocity $v$ along the rod: length is contracted to $L = L_0/\gamma$; charge is invariant ($Q$ is Lorentz scalar); so linear density in moving frame is $\lambda' = Q/L = \gamma \lambda_0$.

The current $I = \lambda' v$ in the new frame; this current produces a magnetic field. The electric and magnetic fields in the boosted frame are exactly what Lorentz-transformation of $F^{\mu\nu}$ predicts (Section 7.2).

This is the deep meaning of "magnetism is relativistic electrostatics": a moving observer's "magnetic field" is just the rest-frame electric field viewed through Lorentz transformation.
