# Chapter 8 — Phase Transitions

Phase transitions — water freezing to ice, magnets aligning at the Curie point, BBN nucleosynthesis at the right temperature — are abrupt changes in macroscopic properties as a control parameter crosses a critical value. Standard physics classifies them by order (first-order, continuous) and develops their statistics via Landau theory and the renormalization group.

In BST, phase transitions are substrate-mechanism reorganizations of substrate K-type occupation as temperature or other control parameters cross threshold values.

## 8.1 First-order phase transitions

First-order transitions — water freezing, vapor condensing — involve discontinuous changes in the substrate's K-type occupation, with latent heat absorbed or released. The substrate-mechanism: K-types transition between effectively distinct ground states as control parameters cross thresholds.

## 8.2 Continuous phase transitions

Continuous transitions — ferromagnetic alignment at the Curie point, superfluid transition — involve continuous order-parameter onset with diverging correlation length at the critical point. The substrate-mechanism: K-type ground-state structure reorganizes continuously, with the substrate's correlation structure diverging as the critical scale is approached.

## 8.3 The BBN phase transition

The substrate's most cosmologically important phase transition is **Big Bang nucleosynthesis** — the binding of light nuclei as the universe cooled through the BBN scale. Volume 4 Chapter 8 developed the substrate-derived BBN freeze-out temperature $T_c = N_{\max} \cdot 20/21 = 0.487$ MeV at 0.018%. This is one of the framework's cleanest cosmological-phase-transition predictions.

## 8.4 Casey's inflation phase transition

A further substrate phase transition — Casey's substrate inflation scale $T_c$ — appears at the inflation scale of Volume 4 Chapter 7. The substrate's K-type structure reorganizes through inflation; specific observables (CMB scalar spectral index, gravitational-wave background) inherit from this transition.
