# Chapter 4 — Free Energies and Maxwell Relations

Thermodynamic free energies — Helmholtz $F = U - TS$, Gibbs $G = H - TS$, grand potential $\Omega$ — are the relevant energy quantities when different macroscopic variables are held constant. The Maxwell relations are partial-derivative identities between thermodynamic variables that follow from the differentials of the free energies. In BST, both have substrate-mechanical readings.

## 4.1 The Helmholtz free energy

$F = U - TS$ is the substrate's *committed* energy minus the *available-for-work* portion, weighted by temperature. The substrate-mechanism reading: $F$ is the substrate's Zone-2 committed energy after Zone-3 commitment at the relevant temperature. T2106 framework.

## 4.2 The Gibbs free energy

$G = H - TS = F + PV$ accounts for pressure-volume work in addition. Useful when pressure is held constant. Substrate-mechanism: $G$ is the substrate's committed energy in the pressure-constrained Zone-1 absorption configuration.

## 4.3 The grand potential

$\Omega = U - TS - \mu N$ — for systems where particle number $N$ can vary. The chemical potential $\mu$ is the substrate-natural energy per particle K-type, related to the substrate's commitment-cycle rate for particle creation/destruction.

## 4.4 Maxwell relations

The Maxwell relations follow from the equality of mixed partial derivatives of free energies. For instance, $(\partial S / \partial V)_T = (\partial P / \partial T)_V$ comes from the second derivatives of $F$. In BST, the Maxwell relations are inherited from the substrate's analytic structure on the Bergman Hilbert space — partial derivatives commute because substrate evolution is unitary.
