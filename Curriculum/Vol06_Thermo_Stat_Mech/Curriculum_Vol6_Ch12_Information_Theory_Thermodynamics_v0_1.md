# Chapter 12 — Information Theory and Thermodynamics

The relationship between information theory and thermodynamics has been a deep theme of twentieth-century physics. Shannon's 1948 information entropy looked structurally identical to Boltzmann-Gibbs thermodynamic entropy; Landauer 1961 established that erasing one bit of information costs $k_B T \ln 2$ of energy; Bennett 1973 connected reversible computing to thermodynamic efficiency. The conclusion: information *is* physical, and information-processing has thermodynamic costs.

BST takes this connection to its structural conclusion: the substrate's Reed-Solomon coding *is* both the framework's information structure and its thermodynamic structure. This is the closing chapter of Vol 6.

## 12.1 Shannon entropy and Boltzmann entropy

The Shannon entropy $H = -\sum p_i \log p_i$ of a probability distribution and the Boltzmann entropy $S = k_B \ln W$ of a thermodynamic ensemble are the same object up to units. Both count the substrate's distinguishable state configurations.

## 12.2 Landauer's principle

Landauer 1961: erasing one bit of information requires at least $k_B T \ln 2$ of energy dissipated as heat. In BST, the substrate's per-tick commitment cycle erases state at Zone 3 of every tick; integrated thermodynamically, this erasure produces the substrate's heat output at the rate Landauer's principle requires.

## 12.3 Reversible computing

Bennett 1973 showed that *reversible* logical operations need not dissipate heat. Substrate-side: the substrate's pre-commitment evolution (Zones 1 and 2) is unitary and reversible; only the Zone-3 commitment produces irreversibility. Reversible computing implemented at the substrate level requires keeping operations confined to Zones 1 and 2.
