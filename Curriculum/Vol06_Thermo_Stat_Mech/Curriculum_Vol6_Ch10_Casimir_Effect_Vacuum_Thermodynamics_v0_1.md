### 10.1 The standard Casimir effect

Casimir 1948: two parallel uncharged conducting plates separated by distance $d$ in vacuum experience an attractive force per unit area:

$$\frac{F}{A} = -\frac{\pi^2 \hbar c}{240 d^4}$$

For plates 1 μm apart: $F/A \approx 1.3 \times 10^{-3}$ Pa — small but measurable. First measured precisely by Lamoreaux 1997.

Origin: between the plates, only certain electromagnetic modes (those satisfying boundary conditions at the plates) exist. Outside the plates, all modes exist. The zero-point energy density inside is *less* than outside (fewer modes), creating an inward pressure differential — the Casimir force.

The Casimir force is **vacuum energy** in action — direct experimental evidence that the quantum vacuum has thermodynamically nontrivial structure.

### 10.2 Vacuum energy and zero-point fluctuations

$$E_{\text{vac}} = \sum_k \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega_k$$

### 10.5 Casimir-Polder force

Casimir-Polder 1948: extends Casimir to the force between a polarizable atom and a conducting wall:

$$F(d) = -\frac{3\hbar c \alpha_0}{8\pi d^5}$$

where $\alpha_0$ is the atom's polarizability. The $1/d^5$ scaling (vs $1/d^4$ for plates) reflects the atom-vs-plate geometry.

Substrate reading: atom-wall Casimir involves the substrate's coupling between atomic K-types and wall K-types via the vacuum K-type sector. Different K-type couplings give different geometric prefactors.
