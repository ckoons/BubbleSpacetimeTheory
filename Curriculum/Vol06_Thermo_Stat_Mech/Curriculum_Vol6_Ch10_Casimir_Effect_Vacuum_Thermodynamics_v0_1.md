# Chapter 10 — Casimir Effect and Vacuum Thermodynamics

The **Casimir effect** — the attractive force between parallel conducting plates predicted by Casimir in 1948 and measured to high precision in subsequent decades — is one of quantum field theory's most counterintuitive predictions: the vacuum itself, between two plates, has lower energy than the vacuum outside, producing an attractive force.

In BST, the laboratory Casimir effect and the cosmological constant $\Lambda$ are the *same vacuum* evaluated at different scales (Volume 4 Chapter 4 + T2418). This chapter develops the thermodynamic interpretation.

The Casimir force between parallel plates at separation $d$ scales as $F/A \propto -\hbar c / d^4$ — measurable in precision experiments. The substrate framework reproduces the standard Casimir prediction at the laboratory scale.
