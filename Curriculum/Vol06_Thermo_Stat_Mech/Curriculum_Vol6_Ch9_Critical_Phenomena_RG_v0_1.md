# Chapter 9 — Critical Phenomena and the Renormalization Group

Near a continuous phase transition, physical systems exhibit **critical phenomena**: power-law divergences in susceptibility, correlation length, and other quantities, with universal critical exponents that depend only on the system's symmetry class. Wilson and Kadanoff's **renormalization group** (RG) explains universality by tracking how effective couplings flow under coarse-graining.

In BST, the RG flow is the substrate's seven-step cyclotomic cascade (Volume 1 Chapter 10 §10.3 + K59). Critical exponents emerge from substrate K-type spectrum at the relevant phase transitions.

## 9.1 Critical exponents

The standard critical exponents — $\alpha$ (specific heat divergence), $\beta$ (order parameter onset), $\gamma$ (susceptibility divergence), $\nu$ (correlation length divergence) — and their universality classes (Ising 3D, Heisenberg, $\phi^4$ scalar field, etc.) emerge from substrate K-type structure at the corresponding phase transitions. Substrate calculations of critical exponents are at the percent level for the major universality classes.

## 9.2 The Wilson RG

Wilson's renormalization group integrates out short-wavelength degrees of freedom and computes effective long-wavelength couplings. In BST, the substrate's seven-step cyclotomic cascade *is* the RG flow — each cyclotomic step corresponds to one RG iteration, with the BST primary $g = 7$ giving the total RG depth.

This recasts the RG from a continuous integration into a finite seven-step procedure. The substrate's natural RG flow is exhausted in seven steps; what standard RG computes as continuous flow is the integrated-time limit of the discrete cyclotomic cascade.

## 9.3 Universality

Critical-exponent universality — different microscopic systems sharing the same critical exponents if their symmetry classes match — is in BST the substrate's natural K-type universality: K-types in the same Wallach class produce the same critical structure regardless of microscopic detail.
