# Chapter 4 — DNA and Proton Siblings

## Level 1 — one sentence

DNA's double-helix architecture — 2 strands (= rank), antiparallel orientation, Watson-Crick base pairing via hydrogen bonds (proton sharing), $\sim$10.5 bp/turn major groove — exhibits BST-primary structural signatures, and the proton-mediated H-bond pairing reveals DNA as a substrate K-type with proton as substrate-mediator.

## Level 2 — graduate-physicist precision

### 4.1 DNA architecture

- Double helix: 2 antiparallel strands (5'-3' opposite)
- **Rank = 2** strands
- Diameter: 2 nm
- Major groove width: 2.2 nm; minor groove: 1.2 nm
- 10.5 bp / helix turn (B-form)
- Watson-Crick base pairs: A-T (2 H-bonds), G-C (3 H-bonds)

Average G+C content varies by organism: ~30-70%.

### 4.2 Hydrogen bonds in DNA

A-T pair: 2 H-bonds
G-C pair: 3 H-bonds (one extra)

H-bond energy ~ 10-40 kJ/mol (Vol 12 Ch 2). DNA stability comes from cumulative H-bonding + stacking (van der Waals between adjacent bases).

Melting temperature $T_m$: increases with G+C fraction (~$0.4°C$ per % G+C).

### 4.3 Proton siblings

Each H-bond shares one proton between donor and acceptor.

BST view: H-bond = substrate K-type configuration with proton playing substrate-mediator role. Proton substrate K-type (Vol 5 Ch 6: hydrogen atom; Vol 3 Ch 4: nucleon-level) makes the DNA pairing.

The proton's substrate role here is non-trivial: $m_p/m_e = 6\pi^5$ (T1426; Vol 3) sets the proton's substrate K-type. The proton's BST-primary mass ratio enters DNA stability indirectly.

### 4.4 Superhelical structure

DNA in cells is supercoiled: ~137 base pairs per topological repeat in some superhelical structures (chromatin, nucleosome wrap).

$N_{\max} = 137$ may be load-bearing here: substrate's natural ceiling for chromatin-scale topological units. Speculative; needs confirmation via DNA topology data (Vol 13 toy planned).

### 4.5 Information density

DNA stores 2 bits per base pair (4 states). Bacterial genome ~$10^6$ bp = ~2 Mbits. Human genome ~$3 \cdot 10^9$ bp = ~6 Gbits.

Information density: $\sim$10^21 bits/cm³ (DNA) — competitive with technological storage.

Substrate view (Paper #122): DNA implements substrate's Reed-Solomon code (Vol 14 Ch 9) at biological scale.

The proton's $m_p/m_e = 6\pi^5$ (Vol 3) enters DNA stability indirectly. **Superhelical 137-bp repeats**: possibly substrate's $N_{\max}$ at chromatin scale. **Information density**: ~$10^{21}$ bits/cm³.
