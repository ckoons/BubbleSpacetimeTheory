# Chapter 8 — Oscillations

Oscillations — simple harmonic motion, damped oscillators, driven oscillators, coupled oscillators, normal modes — are the working physicist's tool for analyzing perturbations near stable equilibria. The harmonic oscillator is one of the most-used models in physics, appearing in quantum mechanics, field theory, condensed matter, and engineering.

In BST, oscillations are the substrate's K-type response to small perturbations near substrate equilibrium configurations.

## 8.1 Simple harmonic motion

For a particle with potential $V(x) = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$, the equation of motion is $\ddot x + \omega^2 x = 0$ with $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$. Solution: $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$. Standard.

## 8.2 Damped and driven oscillators

Damping adds $\gamma\dot x$ to the equation; driving adds a forcing term. The standard underdamped, critically damped, overdamped distinctions apply.

## 8.3 Normal modes

For coupled oscillators, diagonalization of the coupling matrix gives normal modes — uncoupled oscillator modes that diagonalize the system. Each normal mode has a characteristic frequency.

Substrate-mechanism: normal modes are the substrate's natural K-type basis at the relevant equilibrium configuration.
