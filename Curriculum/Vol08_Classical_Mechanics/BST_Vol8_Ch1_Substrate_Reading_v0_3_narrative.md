# Chapter 1 — Classical Mechanics as Substrate Scale 2

Classical mechanics — the physics of macroscopic particles, rigid bodies, fluids — is the apparatus Newton built between 1665 and 1687 and that the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries refined into Lagrangian and Hamiltonian forms. Standard physics treats it as the macroscopic limit of quantum mechanics, with classical behavior emerging when quantum effects are negligible.

In BST, classical mechanics is the substrate's **Scale-2 behavior** (Volume 0 Chapter 3 §3.4) — many substrate ticks averaged within a small spatial region, producing effective classical dynamics. This volume develops the apparatus.

## 1.1 What classical mechanics is in BST

The substrate's per-tick commitment cycle (Volume 0 Chapter 3) operates at Scale 1 with full quantum-mechanical character. At Scale 2 — many ticks averaged within a small region — the substrate's commitments average out, producing deterministic dynamics that match Newton's laws to extraordinary precision.

Classical mechanics is therefore not a separate theory. It is the substrate's coarse-grained behavior at scales above thermal averaging and below cosmological scales. Newton's laws, Lagrangian mechanics, Hamiltonian mechanics, and continuum mechanics all emerge from the substrate's Scale-2 effective dynamics.
