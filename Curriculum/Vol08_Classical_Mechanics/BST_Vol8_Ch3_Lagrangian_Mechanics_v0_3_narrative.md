# Chapter 3 — Lagrangian Mechanics

Lagrange's 1788 reformulation of mechanics — the Lagrangian $L = T - V$ and the Euler-Lagrange equations — provides a coordinate-free framework for classical mechanics. It also generalizes naturally to relativistic mechanics, field theory, and quantum mechanics via the path integral.

In BST, the Lagrangian formulation is the substrate's natural action-principle formulation at Scale 2.

## 3.1 The Euler-Lagrange equations

For generalized coordinates $q_i$ and a Lagrangian $L(q, \dot q, t)$:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q_i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i} = 0.$$

This is the Euler-Lagrange equation. Substrate-mechanism: the substrate's action functional at Scale 2 has these equations as its stationary-action conditions.

## 3.2 The action principle

The classical trajectory minimizes (or extremizes) the action $S = \int L \, dt$. Substrate-mechanism: at Scale 2, the substrate's many-tick path-integral concentrates on the classical-stationary path via constructive interference of nearby paths. This is the substrate's natural way of recovering Lagrangian mechanics from the path-integral formulation of Volume 1 Chapter 7.
