# Chapter 11 — Chaos and Nonlinear Dynamics

Chaos — sensitive dependence on initial conditions in deterministic systems — was first studied by Poincaré in the 1890s and rediscovered by Lorenz in the 1960s. The double pendulum, weather, three-body gravitational systems, turbulent fluid flow are all chaotic. Chaos theory's central insight: deterministic equations can produce unpredictable behavior because tiny differences in initial conditions amplify exponentially.

In BST, chaos is the substrate's natural amplification of substrate-tick perturbations across many ticks. The substrate's discrete tick structure naturally bounds chaotic divergence at the per-tick level.

## 11.1 Sensitive dependence

The Lyapunov exponent $\lambda$ characterizes the exponential separation rate of nearby trajectories. Positive $\lambda$ → chaos; trajectories separate as $\delta x(t) \sim \delta x(0) e^{\lambda t}$.

## 11.2 Attractors

Strange attractors — fractal structures in phase space that nearby trajectories asymptotically approach — characterize many chaotic systems (Lorenz, Rössler, etc.).

## 11.3 The three-body problem

Newton's gravitational three-body problem is generically chaotic. Specific resonances permit stable orbits; most do not.
