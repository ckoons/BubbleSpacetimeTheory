# Chapter 6 — Central Forces and the Kepler Problem

Central forces — forces depending only on radial distance — admit the most-studied integrable systems in classical mechanics. Kepler's two-body problem (gravity, $V \propto -1/r$) and the Coulomb problem are exact solvable cases. The conserved angular momentum and Runge-Lenz vector ensure closed orbits for both.

In BST, the central-force structure follows from the substrate's $SO(3)$ rotational invariance.

## 6.1 The reduction to one dimension

A central force problem reduces to one-dimensional radial motion in the effective potential $V_{eff}(r) = V(r) + L^2/(2mr^2)$. Substrate-mechanism: the angular momentum $L$ is conserved by $SO(3)$ symmetry; the radial degree of freedom is what remains.

## 6.2 Kepler's three laws

Kepler 1609, 1619: planets move in ellipses with the Sun at one focus; equal areas swept in equal times; period squared proportional to semi-major axis cubed.

For inverse-square central force ($V = -k/r$), all three laws follow analytically. The substrate-derivation: gravity is the substrate's residual curvature (Volume 4 Chapter 1, Chapter 2); at large distances and slow speeds, the substrate produces the inverse-square Newtonian force, and Kepler's laws follow.

## 6.3 The Runge-Lenz vector

The Runge-Lenz vector $\vec{A} = \vec{p} \times \vec{L} - mk\hat{r}$ is conserved for the Kepler problem (only). It is what makes Kepler orbits close exactly — for non-Coulombic central forces, orbits precess, but for inverse-square the orbit returns to itself exactly.

The Runge-Lenz vector has a deep symmetry origin: the Kepler problem has $SO(4)$ dynamical symmetry larger than the obvious $SO(3)$. The extra symmetries generate $\vec{A}$.
