# Chapter 2 — Newton's Laws

Newton's three laws — inertia, $\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$, action-reaction — are the foundation of classical mechanics. They emerge in BST as the substrate's Scale-2 effective dynamics for slow, massive bodies.

## 2.1 The three laws

- **First law (inertia)**: a body at rest stays at rest; a body in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by a force. Substrate-mechanism: at Scale 2, the substrate's natural state for non-interacting matter is uniform motion (no commitment-cycle perturbation).
- **Second law**: $\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$. The substrate-mechanism for force is the substrate's commitment-cycle perturbation rate; for mass, the substrate's energy-eigenvalue scale on the relevant K-type; the relationship between them produces standard Newtonian acceleration.
- **Third law (action-reaction)**: every action has an equal and opposite reaction. Substrate-mechanism: substrate commitment cycles between interacting K-types are symmetric, producing balanced momentum transfer.

## 2.2 Inertia and mass

The inertial mass of a classical object is the substrate's natural energy scale on its constituent K-types. Different particles have different masses because they correspond to different substrate K-types with different Casimir eigenvalues. This connects to Volume 2's particle-mass derivations.

## 2.3 Galilean invariance

Newton's laws are Galilean-invariant — they take the same form in all inertial frames. The substrate framework recovers Galilean invariance as the low-velocity limit of substrate Lorentz invariance (Volume 0 Chapter 4 §4.5).
