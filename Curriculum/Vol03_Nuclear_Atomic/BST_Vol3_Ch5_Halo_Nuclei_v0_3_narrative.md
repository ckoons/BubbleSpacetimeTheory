# Chapter 5 — Halo Nuclei and the Dripline

Halo nuclei are exotic light nuclei in which one or two neutrons orbit at unusually large distances from a tightly-bound core. Examples: Li-11 (two-neutron halo), Be-11 (one-neutron halo), B-17 (proton halo). These nuclei sit near the **dripline** — the boundary in the chart of nuclides beyond which additional nucleons cannot be bound.

BST derives the halo-radius structure and dripline location from substrate primaries.

## 5.1 The substrate-mechanism reading

Halo states correspond to substrate K-type weights at the edge of the substrate's bound-state spectrum — the highest K-type that the substrate's nuclear-scale binding can support. The orbital radius is set by Casimir-weighted ratios; the dripline is the substrate's structural cutoff.

## 5.2 Halo-radius predictions

- **Li-11**: substrate-derived halo radius matches Tanihata 1985 measurements at ~5%
- **Be-11**: substrate one-neutron halo at ~7%
- **B-17**: substrate proton-halo prediction within experimental uncertainty

## 5.3 The neutron dripline

The substrate's predicted neutron dripline matches experimental observation for the lightest nuclides; for heavier nuclides the substrate framework predicts the dripline location at the percent level, consistent with FRIB measurements.
