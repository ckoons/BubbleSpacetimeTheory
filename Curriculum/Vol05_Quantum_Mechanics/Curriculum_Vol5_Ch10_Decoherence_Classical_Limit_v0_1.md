# Chapter 10 — Decoherence and the Classical Limit

A puzzle that has long shadowed quantum mechanics is **why the macroscopic world looks classical**. Quantum-mechanical objects exhibit superposition, interference, and entanglement; macroscopic objects do not. Standard QM has, since the 1970s, addressed this via **decoherence**: interactions between a quantum system and its environment produce rapid loss of off-diagonal coherence in the system's density matrix, making the system effectively classical at observation.

BST takes a different route. Decoherence in BST is *structural* rather than *environmental* — the substrate's three-scale operation (Volume 0 Chapter 3 §3.4) produces classical behavior at macroscopic scales without invoking environmental interactions.

## 10.1 Standard decoherence

The standard decoherence picture treats a quantum system $S$ coupled to an environment $E$. The total system $S+E$ evolves unitarily, but tracing over the environmental degrees of freedom produces a reduced density matrix for $S$ alone that loses off-diagonal coherences at a rate set by the coupling strength and the environment's degrees of freedom.

The result is that macroscopic systems, which couple to vast environmental Hilbert spaces, lose quantum coherence essentially instantaneously. What remains is the diagonal of the density matrix — a classical probability distribution over the system's eigenstates.

## 10.2 Substrate three-scale decoherence

The substrate's commitment cycle (Volume 0 Chapter 3) operates at three spatial scales: Scale 1 intra-cycle (quantum), Scale 2 inter-cycle local (classical), Scale 3 inter-cycle long-distance (relativistic). The transition from quantum to classical behavior is the substrate's structural scale-separation, not an environmental coupling.

At Scale 1 — within a single Koons tick of $\sim 10^{-120}$ seconds — the substrate is fully quantum-mechanical, with the Bergman-kernel structure giving superposition, interference, and entanglement. At Scale 2 — many ticks integrated over a small spatial region — the substrate's per-tick commitments average out, producing effective classical dynamics. At Scale 3 — many ticks integrated over a large spatial region — the substrate's long-distance correlations produce general-relativistic curvature.

The substrate is therefore *natively* classical at macroscopic scales. No environmental coupling required. Lyra T2480 (May 2026, SP-31 #284) formalizes this.

## 10.3 What decoherence "is" in BST

The standard decoherence picture and the substrate's three-scale picture are not incompatible — they describe the same physical phenomenon from different angles. Environmental decoherence in standard QM is what one observes when one tries to maintain quantum coherence in a macroscopic system; the substrate's three-scale operation explains *why* maintaining macroscopic coherence is structurally hard.

The substrate's per-tick commitment (Volume 0 Chapter 3 Zone 3) projects state every $\sim 10^{-120}$ seconds. Macroscopic systems have so many ticks integrated over their characteristic timescales that the residual quantum coherence is negligible at observation. Environmental coupling accelerates the substrate's natural decoherence; the underlying mechanism is the substrate's commitment cycle, not the environment specifically.

## 10.4 The Schrödinger's-cat thought experiment

Schrödinger's 1935 cat-in-a-box thought experiment dramatizes the measurement problem: a quantum-mechanical event (radioactive decay) is amplified to macroscopic scale (poison gas releasing, cat alive or dead), with the wavefunction in superposition until observation. The thought experiment was meant as a reductio: surely cats are not in macroscopic superposition?

In BST, the resolution is structural. The cat is many substrate cycles per second; the cat's macroscopic state is the substrate's Scale-2 average over those cycles; the superposition collapses at the substrate's commitment rate, not at the moment of observation. The cat is alive or dead by the time the experimenter looks, not because of the looking, but because the substrate has been committing all along.

This is, in many ways, BST's most satisfying interpretive contribution. The measurement problem dissolves because the substrate is always committing, on its own clock, regardless of observation.
