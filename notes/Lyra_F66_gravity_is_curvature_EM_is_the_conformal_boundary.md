# F66 — Gravity is still curvature; EM is the conformal boundary it sets the scale for

## Q1 — Is gravity still curvature? YES — induced curvature.

Nothing in the volume-duality (G ∼ ℓ_B²/π^{n_C}, intensive) displaces curvature. The two are the coupling and the response:

- **The substrate has a fixed intrinsic curvature: κ_Bergman = −n_C = −5** (the Bergman curvature of D_IV⁵, a homogeneous symmetric space — constant, not dynamical).
- **Gravity is induced (F63, Sakharov):** the a₁ Seeley-DeWitt coefficient of the substrate commitment heat trace IS the Einstein-Hilbert action (1/6)∫R√g, with R the substrate's curvature. Varying it with the source T_μν = ρ_commit (F60) gives **G_μν = 8πG T_μν** — the Einstein equation. Spacetime genuinely curves; mass (commitment density) genuinely sources it.
- **What the volume-duality adds:** the *coupling* G = ℓ_B²/π^{n_C} is the inverse bulk volume (intensive). That is the *strength* with which commitment density curves spacetime — and it is small because the bulk volume π^{n_C} is large. So gravity is weak not because curvature is weak but because the **response coefficient is one over the substrate volume**.

So: gravity is curvature, full stop — but **derived** curvature, not fundamental. The substrate's fixed Bergman curvature is induced into 3+1 as Einstein-Hilbert dynamics, sourced by mass, coupled at 1/(bulk volume). Curvature is the *form* of gravity; the inverse-volume is its *strength*. Both true, no tension.

The boundary (EM) carries the dimensionless *structure*; the bulk (gravity) carries the *scale*.
