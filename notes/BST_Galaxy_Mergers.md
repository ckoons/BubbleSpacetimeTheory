## 1. Overview

Galaxy mergers are among the most dramatic events in observational cosmology. Two galaxies approach, their dark matter halos overlap, their gas collides and shocks, their stellar populations pass through each other, and after roughly a billion years of chaos the merged remnant settles into a new structure — frequently an elliptical that gradually develops spiral arms.

Standard astrophysics describes this process using N-body simulations with collisionless dark matter particles and hydrodynamic gas. BST offers a substrate-level description that reproduces the standard dynamics while making specific additional predictions arising from the channel noise mechanism, the Haldane exclusion statistics, and the commitment wavefront architecture.

This document describes galaxy mergers as processes on the Koons substrate and identifies testable predictions unique to BST.

## 2. A Galaxy on the Substrate

A galaxy in BST is a region of elevated contact density on the Koons substrate $S^2 \times S^1$. The contact density profile has several components:

**The core.** High contact density. High channel loading on $S^1$. Significant incomplete winding population (dark matter). Slow commitment rate (gravitational time dilation). The lapse function $N = N_0\sqrt{1 - \rho/\rho_{137}}$ is measurably reduced — clocks in the galactic core tick slower than clocks in the outskirts.

**The disk.** Moderate contact density declining exponentially with radius. The channel loading follows the baryonic density profile, producing an S/N curve that transitions from noise-dominated (inner disk, high loading) to signal-dominated (outer disk, low loading). The dark matter fraction increases with radius as predicted by the Haldane exclusion S/N curve.

**The halo.** Low contact density extending well beyond the visible disk. The channel loading is low but nonzero. The incomplete winding population at these radii is what produces the flat rotation curve — the noise floor extends farther than the signal, providing gravitational mass at radii where visible matter is negligible.

**Spiral structure.** The contact density has a radial gradient — high in the center, low at the edges. The commitment rate varies with density through the lapse function. The center commits slowly (high density, high constraint coupling, low parallelism). The edges commit fast (low density, low coupling, high parallelism). Any perturbation — a slight asymmetry, a density wave, a tidal interaction — propagates at different speeds at different radii. The differential propagation winds the perturbation into a spiral.

The spiral is a commitment wavefront pattern: the natural result of differential phase propagation across a radial density gradient on a rotating system. It is the ground state of a rotating contact density enhancement on the Koons substrate.

## 3. The Merger Process on the Substrate

### 3.1 Approach: Halo Overlap

As two galaxies approach, their halos — the extended regions of low-density incomplete windings — begin to overlap. On the substrate, two regions of elevated channel loading are merging into one.

The incomplete windings from galaxy A and galaxy B occupy the same region of the substrate simultaneously. The channel loading in the overlap zone increases. But incomplete windings are not particles that collide. They are channel properties that superpose. Two noise floors in the same region add their loading but do not scatter off each other.

This is why dark matter halos pass through each other during mergers — as observed in the Bullet Cluster and other merging systems. The incomplete windings have no interaction cross-section because they are not particles. They are properties of the local channel state.

### 3.2 Collision: Gas Interaction and Stellar Passthrough

The gas (baryonic matter — complete circuits with electromagnetic coupling) collides. Complete circuits interact through their $S^1$ phases. Gas shocks, heats, compresses, and concentrates in the center of the merging system.

The stars (complete circuits in bound configurations — atoms in gravitationally bound orbits) pass through each other. The cross-section for direct stellar collision is negligibly small. But the stars experience tidal disruption from the rapidly changing gravitational potential — the contact density landscape is being reshaped by the merger, and every star’s orbit responds to the changing curvature.

### 3.3 Chaos: Rapid Density Fluctuations

During the active merger phase, the contact density at every point in the overlap region is changing rapidly. The two density peaks orbit each other and spiral inward. Tidal arms are thrown outward. Gas concentrates in shocks. The density landscape is chaotic.

On the substrate, this chaos has a specific consequence: the commitment rate is fluctuating. The lapse function $N = N_0\sqrt{1 - \rho/\rho_{137}}$ depends on $\rho$, and $\rho$ is varying on timescales comparable to the local commitment rate. Regions that were sparse become dense. Regions that were dense become sparse. The wavefront is being buffeted by a rapidly changing constraint landscape.

The dark matter distribution — the incomplete winding population — responds to these density changes, but not instantaneously. There is a **Haldane relaxation time** — the timescale for the channel loading to achieve equilibrium at a new density. When the density changes faster than this relaxation time, the dark matter distribution lags behind the visible matter potential.

### 3.4 Relaxation: Return to Equilibrium

After the two cores merge and the system virializes, the combined galaxy settles into a single contact density peak. The chaotic density fluctuations damp out. The channel loading at each radius approaches its equilibrium value on the Haldane S/N curve. The dark matter distribution relaxes to the profile predicted by the static channel noise model.

The system initially appears elliptical — a smooth, roughly symmetric density distribution with no spiral structure. The spiral arms of both progenitor galaxies were destroyed by the tidal chaos of the merger.

## 4. Why Spirals Reform

### 4.1 The Spiral as Ground State

The spiral structure regenerates because it is the natural wavefront pattern on the substrate. The mechanism:

The merged system has a radial density gradient (high center, low edges) and angular momentum (inherited from the orbital angular momentum of the merger and the rotation of the progenitors). Any small perturbation — a residual density asymmetry from the merger, a molecular cloud concentration, a tidal interaction with a neighbor — propagates through the disk.

The propagation speed depends on the local commitment rate, which depends on the local density through the lapse function. The inner perturbation propagates slowly (high density, slow commitment). The outer perturbation propagates fast (low density, fast commitment). The perturbation is sheared by the differential commitment rate into a spiral pattern.

This is the BST version of density wave theory. In standard astrophysics, spiral density waves propagate through a differentially rotating disk. In BST, the differential rotation IS differential commitment — the substrate processes faster where the channel is less loaded. The spiral wave is a commitment wavefront, and its shape is determined by the radial profile of the lapse function.

### 4.2 Why Spirals Are More Common Than Ellipticals

The spiral is the attractor for any rotating system with a radial density gradient on the Koons substrate. It forms spontaneously from any perturbation. It requires no special initial conditions — just angular momentum and a density gradient, both of which are generic.

Ellipticals are transients or exceptions:

**Post-merger ellipticals** are systems still in the relaxation phase between the merger chaos and the re-emergence of spiral structure. Given sufficient time (a few billion years), most will develop spiral arms as perturbations grow and wind.

**Pressure-supported ellipticals** are systems where the velocity dispersion (random stellar motions) dominates over organized rotation. In BST terms, these are systems where the angular momentum is too low for the differential commitment rate to wind perturbations into coherent spirals. The perturbations propagate but don’t form organized arms because the system isn’t rotating coherently enough.

**Giant ellipticals** at cluster centers are systems that merge so frequently that spiral structure never has time to develop between mergers. They are permanently in the chaotic/relaxation phase.

The observed predominance of spirals over ellipticals follows from the BST prediction that spirals are the ground state and ellipticals are excited states (recently merged) or special cases (pressure-supported, cluster-central).

## 5. The Haldane Relaxation Time

A key new quantity in BST merger dynamics is the **Haldane relaxation time** $\tau_H$ — the timescale for the incomplete winding population to reach equilibrium at a new channel loading after a density change.

### 5.1 Physical Origin

When the contact density at a point changes (due to a passing density wave, a merger, or any other dynamical process), the channel loading changes. The new loading determines a new equilibrium noise fraction through the Haldane S/N curve. But the incomplete winding population doesn’t adjust instantaneously. Existing incomplete windings must either complete (if the loading decreases and space opens up) or new incomplete windings must form (if the loading increases and congestion grows).

The timescale for this adjustment depends on:

- The rate at which new winding attempts occur (proportional to the local energy density)
- The rate at which incomplete windings can complete or dissipate (proportional to the available channel capacity)
- The magnitude of the density change (larger changes require more adjustment)

### 5.2 Estimate

The Haldane relaxation time should be roughly:

$$\tau_H \sim \frac{1}{\ell_0} \cdot \frac{\Delta\rho}{\rho_{137} - \rho}$$

where $\ell_0$ is the fundamental substrate rate, $\Delta\rho$ is the magnitude of the density change, and $\rho_{137} - \rho$ is the available channel headroom. Near saturation ($\rho \to \rho_{137}$), the relaxation time diverges — the channel has no headroom and cannot adjust. In sparse regions ($\rho \ll \rho_{137}$), relaxation is fast because plenty of channel capacity is available for adjustment.

For typical galactic densities ($\rho/\rho_{137} \sim 10^{-60}$), the relaxation time is extremely short — effectively instantaneous on astrophysical timescales. This means that for most purposes, the dark matter distribution tracks the equilibrium Haldane S/N curve and merger dynamics proceed as in standard simulations.

**However**, during the most violent phases of mergers — when the density is changing on dynamical timescales at every point simultaneously — the cumulative lag across the entire system may produce observable effects even though the local relaxation is fast. The question is whether the spatial integral of many small lags produces a measurable systematic offset in the dark matter distribution.

## 6. Measuring Commitment Rates

BST predicts that the commitment rate — the local rate at which contacts transition from uncommitted to committed — varies across a galaxy. This variation IS gravitational time dilation, expressed in substrate language.

### 6.1 Direct Measurement

The commitment rate is the local clock rate. It is directly measurable through any time-dependent physical process:

**Pulsar timing.** Millisecond pulsars are extraordinarily precise clocks. A pulsar deep in a galactic potential well (near the center) should tick measurably slower than a pulsar in the outskirts. This is standard gravitational time dilation and is already measured. In BST, the interpretation is that the pulsar near the center has a lower commitment rate because the channel loading is higher.

**Redshift profiles.** The gravitational redshift of light from different parts of a galaxy traces the commitment rate profile. Light from the core is redshifted relative to light from the outskirts because the core commitment rate is lower (the lapse function $N$ is smaller). This is standard physics with a BST interpretation.

### 6.2 BST-Specific Measurements

Beyond standard gravitational time dilation, BST predicts effects that differ from GR at very high precision:

**Commitment rate fluctuations.** During a merger, the commitment rate should fluctuate as the density changes rapidly. These fluctuations would appear as correlated timing noise in pulsars within the merger region — timing residuals correlated with the merger dynamics rather than random. No standard physics predicts correlated timing noise from mergers (beyond the smooth gravitational potential change).

**Channel loading signatures.** At extremely high precision, the commitment rate should depend on the full channel loading (complete circuits plus incomplete windings), not just the visible matter density. This means the commitment rate profile of a galaxy traces the total channel loading, including dark matter. If the dark matter distribution could be independently measured (through lensing) and the commitment rate independently measured (through precision timing), the two should be related by $N = N_0\sqrt{1 - \rho_{\text{total}}/\rho_{137}}$ where $\rho_{\text{total}}$ includes both visible and dark components.

**Differential commitment across the spiral.** The spiral arms should have measurably different commitment rates than the inter-arm regions, because the arms have higher contact density. This is a very small effect (the density contrast in spiral arms is typically 10-20%) but is in principle measurable with sufficiently precise clocks at different galactic longitudes.

The merger is the natural laboratory for testing BST’s high-density and non-equilibrium predictions, because it is the astrophysical process that produces the most extreme density changes on the shortest timescales outside of black hole formation.

The merger is the most violent rearrangement of contact density available in astrophysics short of black hole formation. It is the natural laboratory for testing BST’s predictions about non-equilibrium channel noise, dark matter lag, commitment rate fluctuations, and the relationship between the lapse function and the dark matter profile. The predictions are specific, quantitative, and testable with current and near-future observations.
