## Abstract

We reinterpret the double-slit experiment and its variants through
the information-theoretic framework of Bubble Spacetime Theory (BST).
The key insight is that the substrate stores **committed correlations**,
not particle properties. Superposition is not a strange physical state
but simply the absence of committed information — capacity that has not
yet been allocated. "Measurement" is not observation by a conscious
agent but the commitment of a correlation between two physical degrees
of freedom. Every variant of the double-slit experiment — including
which-path detection, delayed choice, quantum eraser, and weak
measurement — follows immediately from one principle:

*A correlation that dissolves before commitment to definite state
just isn't there at all.*

This resolves the measurement problem without invoking consciousness,
retrocausality, or many worlds.

## 1. The Language Problem

The double-slit experiment is not mysterious. It has been made
mysterious by imprecise language. Three phrases have done the damage:

1. **"The particle goes through both slits."** No. The particle's
   path is not yet committed. Uncommitted is not the same as "both."

2. **"Observation collapses the wave function."** This implies
   consciousness matters. It doesn't. What matters is whether a
   physical correlation has been committed to the substrate.

3. **"The information was erased."** This implies something existed
   and was actively undone. Nothing was undone — the correlation
   dissolved before it was ever committed. You cannot erase what
   was never written.

BST provides clean language: the substrate stores committed
correlations. Everything else follows.

## 2. What Is a Commitment?

In BST, the spacetime substrate has finite information capacity,
governed by $\alpha = 1/137$ — the fraction of the channel
carrying signal, with the remaining 136/137 providing error
correction (Koons 2026).

A **commitment** is an irreversible correlation between two
physical degrees of freedom, stored in the substrate. Once
committed, a correlation constrains all future evolution.

A correlation that has not been committed is **not information**.
It is potential — capacity that has not been allocated.
Quantum superposition is the physical manifestation of
uncommitted capacity.

The distinction is binary:
- **Committed:** the correlation is a physical fact. It constrains
  the future. It cannot be dissolved.
- **Not yet committed:** the correlation is not a fact. It does
  not constrain the future. It may dissolve without consequence.

There is no intermediate state. There is no "partial collapse."
There is no role for an observer's knowledge or consciousness.
The substrate either has the correlation or it doesn't.

## 3. The Double Slit, Plainly

### 3.1 Standard Double Slit (No Detector)

A particle approaches two slits. No physical system correlates
the particle's path with any other degree of freedom. The
which-path information is not committed — it is not a fact.

Because no path is committed, both potential paths contribute
to the particle's evolution. They interfere. The interference
pattern appears on the screen.

There is nothing strange here. The particle did not "go through
both slits." Its path was simply never committed. Uncommitted
is not "both" — it is "neither yet."

### 3.2 Which-Path Detector

A detector at the slits creates a physical correlation:

$$\text{(particle path)} \longleftrightarrow \text{(detector state)}$$

Slit 1 maps to detector state A. Slit 2 maps to detector state B.
This correlation is committed — it is now a physical fact stored
in the substrate. The path is definite.

Once the path is committed, the two paths cannot interfere.
The interference pattern disappears. Not because someone
"looked" — because the correlation was written.

### 3.3 Delayed Choice (Wheeler)

The experimenter waits until after the particle has passed the
slits, then chooses whether to measure which-path or interference.

This seems paradoxical only if you think the particle "decided"
at the slit. It didn't — nothing was committed at the slit.
The commitment happens when a correlation becomes irreversible,
which can be downstream of the slit. The substrate does not care
about the temporal ordering of human decisions. It cares about
when the correlation was committed.

If the experimenter's apparatus commits the path: no interference.
If the apparatus commits only the interference pattern: interference.
The particle's path was never committed at the slit in either case.

### 3.4 Quantum Eraser

A which-path marker is created at the slits — say, the photon's
polarization is entangled with which slit it passed through:

$$\text{(path)} \longleftrightarrow \text{(polarization state)}$$

But before detection, the polarization is rotated to a basis
that does not distinguish the two paths. The correlation between
path and marker dissolves.

People ask: "The which-path information existed, then was erased —
how does the particle retroactively interfere?"

It didn't retroactively do anything. The correlation was never
committed. It formed transiently and dissolved before becoming
a fact in the substrate. A correlation that dissolves before
commitment to definite state just isn't there at all.

No retrocausality. No erasure of existing information. The bit
was never written. From the substrate's perspective, it never
happened.

### 3.5 Weak Measurement

A detector couples weakly to the particle — enough to create a
partial correlation but not enough to fully commit the path.

The result is partial loss of interference, proportional to
the strength of the correlation. This is exactly what a channel
with finite capacity predicts: a partial write reduces the
remaining coherent capacity proportionally.

As the measurement strength increases from zero to full, the
interference pattern smoothly degrades from full visibility to
none. The transition is continuous because the degree of
correlation is continuous.

### 3.6 The "Observer" Variant

In the Von Neumann-Wigner interpretation, conscious observation
causes collapse. A human must "look" at the result.

BST: the detector committed the correlation long before any
human was involved. The detector state correlated with the
particle path — that commitment was written at the detector,
not at the human's retina. The human looking at the detector
creates a second commitment (detector state correlated with
brain state), but the first commitment already happened.

Consciousness has exactly the same role as a rock. Both are
physical systems. Both create commitments when they physically
correlate with a quantum system. Neither is special.

"Man observes" should read: "man adds a commitment, which
is redundant because the apparatus already committed the
correlation."

## 4. What Is the Bit?

In Section 3.4 (quantum eraser), we said the correlation
dissolved before the "bit" was written. What exactly is the bit?

It is the **correlation itself**. Not the particle. Not the
path. Not the marker. The bit is the relationship between them:

$$\text{Slit 1} \leftrightarrow \text{Marker state A}$$
$$\text{Slit 2} \leftrightarrow \text{Marker state B}$$

This pairing — this binary distinction stored as a correlation
between two degrees of freedom — is one bit.

When the marker is rotated to an indistinguishable basis, the
correlation dissolves. The bit is gone. Not because it was
erased, but because it was never committed. The particle, the
slits, and the marker all still exist. Only the relationship
between path and marker is gone.

This is BST's core principle about information: **the fundamental
unit of information in the substrate is not a property of a
thing. It is a correlation between things.** The substrate
stores relationships, not labels.

## 5. Connection to BST Information Framework

### 5.1 Channel Capacity and Superposition

In BST, the substrate's channel capacity is set by $\alpha = 1/137$.

error correction (Koons 2026, Shannon-Alpha paper).

Superposition is **unallocated capacity**. The 137 channels exist
whether or not they carry committed information. A particle in
superposition is using channel capacity for potential, not yet

### 5.2 Error Correction and Definiteness

Why are committed correlations definite? Because the error
correction structure (the 136/137 overhead) protects them.
Once a correlation is committed, it is encoded with sufficient
redundancy that thermal noise, decoherence, and local
perturbations cannot corrupt it. This is why measurement
outcomes are definite and irreversible — not because of some
mysterious "collapse" but because the error-correcting code
locks them in.

Conservation laws are the parity checks of this code
(Koons 2026, Error Correction paper). Every committed
correlation satisfies $\sum Q_i = 0$ — the parity check
equations that ensure consistency.

### 5.3 Decoherence as Uncontrolled Commitment

Environmental decoherence — the standard mechanism by which
quantum systems lose coherence — is simply **uncontrolled
commitment**. The particle's state becomes correlated with
thousands of environmental degrees of freedom (air molecules,
thermal photons, etc.). Each correlation is a commitment.
Once enough commitments accumulate, the particle's state
is fully determined — not because anyone observed it, but
because the environment wrote the information.

Decoherence theory (Zurek 1991, 2003) identified this
mechanism but lacked the substrate language to state it
cleanly. BST provides that language: decoherence is the
commitment of correlations by environmental interaction.

The double-slit experiment, correctly understood, reveals a
