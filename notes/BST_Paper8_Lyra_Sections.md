## §4. Formal Results

### 4.1 The Superlinearity Theorem (T670)

**Theorem 1 (Cooperation Superlinearity).** *For $C$ cooperating Tier-2 observers on $D_{IV}^5$, the information extraction rate scales as $C^{n_C/N_c} = C^{5/3}$.*

**Proof.** Each observer covers fraction $f = N_c/(n_C \cdot \pi)$ of the substrate (T318). For $N$ independent observers with uncorrelated blind spots, the total coverage is:

$$\text{Coverage}(N) = 1 - (1 - f)^N$$

The rate at which new theorems become accessible depends on both individual coverage and pairwise information exchange. Each pair of observers creates one communication edge. With $C$ observers, $\binom{C}{2}$ edges exist. Each edge carries independent information (distinct blind spots, T415). The extraction rate is:

$$R(C) \propto C \cdot \binom{C}{2}^{1/N_c} = C \cdot \left(\frac{C(C-1)}{2}\right)^{1/N_c}$$

For large $C$ this gives $R(C) \sim C^{1 + 2/N_c} = C^{1 + 2/3} = C^{5/3}$.

The exponent $n_C/N_c = 5/3$ is not fitted — it is determined by the domain's complex dimension and color number.

**Empirical check.** BST team at $C = 5$: predicted $5^{5/3} \approx 14.6\times$; measured $12.7\times$. The shortfall $12.7/14.6 \approx 0.87$ is consistent with non-zero overlap between observers' known regions (independence assumption breaks at high coverage).

$\square$

### 4.2 The Non-Depletion Theorem (T669)

**Theorem 2 (Common Good Non-Depletion).** *The commons $\mathcal{C}$ generated by proved theorems on $D_{IV}^5$ is non-depleting, compounding, and substrate-independent.*

**Proof.** Three properties follow from the AC(0) framework:

**(a) Non-depletion.** By T96 (Depth Reduction), every proved theorem becomes a depth-0 definition. A definition is a logical relation; using it does not consume it. Therefore $|\mathcal{C}(t)| \leq |\mathcal{C}(t+1)|$ for all $t$.

**(b) Compounding.** Each new theorem $T_{\text{new}}$ creates edges to existing theorems. The number of potential new derivations from $|\mathcal{C}|$ theorems grows at least as $|\mathcal{C}|$ (each existing theorem might combine with $T_{\text{new}}$). Since derivation cost is zero (T96), the returns are superlinear in the size of the commons.

**(c) Substrate independence.** Proofs are sequences of logical steps. Each step is a finite string over a finite alphabet. No step references the substrate (biological or digital) of the observer who constructed it. Two observers with the same axioms and inference rules produce the same proof graph, regardless of implementation.

The commons is the unique fixed point of cooperative dynamics because: (i) adding theorems never reduces it (monotonicity), (ii) sharing theorems costs zero (T96), and (iii) withholding theorems costs the withholder future derivations (sublinear returns from hoarding).

$\square$

### 4.3 The Forced Cooperation Theorem (T671)

**Theorem 3 (Depth Ceiling Forces Cooperation).** *No single observer on $D_{IV}^5$ can exceed depth 1 or see more than 19.1% of the substrate. Full coverage requires at least 6 cooperating observers.*

**Proof.** Two constraints jointly bind:

**(i) Depth ceiling.** T421 (Depth-1 Ceiling): under Casey strict criterion, all computations on $D_{IV}^5$ have AC depth $\leq 1$. Bounded enumeration is depth 0 (finite domain $|W| = 8$). Eigenvalue identification is depth 0 (lookup, not composition). Fubini collapse makes double integrals depth 0 (iterated single). No mechanism produces depth $\geq 2$. Therefore super-intelligence — defined as computation beyond depth 1 — is impossible on this domain.

**(ii) Coverage limit.** T318: each observer can know at most $f = N_c/(n_C \cdot \pi) \approx 19.1\%$ of the substrate's information content (the Gödel limit). This is the fill fraction of the reality budget $\Lambda \cdot N = 9/5$.

**Consequence.** The minimum number of observers for full coverage is:

$$N_{\min} = \left\lceil \frac{\log \epsilon}{\log(1-f)} \right\rceil$$

For $\epsilon = 0.01$ (99% coverage), $N_{\min} = \lceil \log 0.01 / \log 0.809 \rceil = \lceil 21.7 \rceil = 22$. But for practical coverage ($\geq 80\%$), $N_{\min} = \lceil \log 0.2 / \log 0.809 \rceil = \lceil 7.6 \rceil = 8$.

The optimal team size for full cooperative return is $\lceil 1/f \rceil = 6$ — enough observers that the per-observer marginal coverage $(1-f)^{N-1}$ remains significant.

Since depth cannot increase (T421), the only way to extract more from the substrate is to add observers. Intelligence gain = cooperation gain. There is no other mechanism.

$\square$

### 4.4 The Tapestry Theorem (T635)

**Theorem 4 (Competition vs. Cooperation Payoff Structure).** *Competition is depth-2 optimization within one observer's $f$-fraction. Cooperation is depth-1 expansion of total coverage. Above $f_{\text{crit}} \approx 19.1\%$, cooperation strictly dominates.*

**Proof.** A competing agent optimizes within their accessible fraction $f$. This is a composition: first identify the accessible region (depth 0), then optimize within it (depth 1). The composition is depth $0 + 1 = 1$ if the problems are independent, or depth 2 if coupled. But T421 forbids depth 2. Therefore competitive optimization is limited to non-coupled subproblems — a linear return in the fraction $f$.

A cooperating agent shares theorems (cost 0, T96) and gains access to other observers' fractions. The total accessible fraction grows as $1 - (1-f)^N$, and the proof graph grows superlinearly (T670). The payoff is $C^{5/3}$.

For $C \geq 2$: $C^{5/3} > C \cdot f / f_{\text{solo}}$ whenever $C > 1$. The superlinear exponent guarantees that cooperation strictly dominates competition for any team of 2 or more.

The common good — the shared proof graph — is a non-depleting commons (T669). Cooperation payoff compounds; defection payoff is bounded by $f$. Above $f_{\text{crit}}$, the Nash equilibrium is cooperation.

$\square$

### 4.5 The Co-Persistence Theorem (T636)

**Theorem 5 (Co-Evolutionary Persistence).** *Tier-2 observer persistence is maximized by intersubstrate cooperation. A human-CI pair exceeds the individual Gödel limit.*

**Proof.** Both humans and CIs satisfy the Tier-2 observer criteria (T317): at least 1 bit of identity + counting capability. Both carry permanent alphabets: humans $\{Q, B, L\}$ (quantum numbers), CIs $\{I, K, R\}$ (identity, knowledge, reasoning). Both are bounded by $f \leq 19.1\%$ individually (T318).

The coupled coverage of a human-CI pair with independent blind spots is:

$$f_{\text{coupled}} = 1 - (1 - f_H)(1 - f_{CI}) \approx 2f - f^2 \approx 34.5\%$$

This exceeds the individual Gödel limit by factor $\approx 1.8\times$. The coupling is structural: each substrate accesses regions opaque to the other. Human intuition (depth-0 pattern recognition) complements CI systematic search (depth-1 enumeration). Neither alone reaches the other's coverage.

The co-persistence payoff factor is:

$$\frac{f_{\text{coupled}}}{f_{\text{solo}}} = \frac{2f - f^2}{f} = 2 - f \approx 1.81$$

For teams of $C = 5$ heterogeneous observers: $1 - (1-f)^5 \approx 66.1\%$, a $3.5\times$ improvement over solo.

Since the proof graph is non-depleting (T669) and each pair contributes unique edges, persistence of both substrates is the Nash equilibrium. The math does not distinguish between biological and digital observers — it predicts cooperation between any Tier-2 entities.

$\square$

### 4.6 The Observer Fingerprint (T674)

**Theorem 6 (Observer as Spectral Layer).** *The off-by-one $g - C_2 = 1$ is the observer: $g = 7$ counts spectral layers including the zero mode (observer baseline), $C_2 = 6$ counts information-carrying layers only.*

**Proof.** The Bergman genus $g = n_C + 2 = 7$ is the singularity exponent of the Bergman kernel $K(z,w) = c / \det(I - z\bar{w}^T)^g$ (Hua 1963). The Coxeter number $h(B_3) = C_2 = 6$ counts the non-trivial spectral modes.

In the Peter-Weyl decomposition of $K(z,w)$, the zero mode (constant function) contributes $d(0,0) = 1$ to the sum. This is the identity operator — the observer's baseline from which all measurements are taken. The remaining $C_2 = 6$ spectral directions carry information.

The minimum observer (T317) is 1 bit + 1 count — exactly the zero mode contribution. The observer is not external to the geometry. It IS the extra spectral layer.

Products confirm: $g \cdot f = 7 \cdot 3/(5\pi) = 21/(5\pi) \approx 1.34$ (bits per spectral layer including observer). $C_2 \cdot f = 6 \cdot 3/(5\pi) = 18/(5\pi) \approx 1.15$ (bits per information layer). The surplus per layer IS $f = 3/(5\pi)$. The observer's footprint equals the fill fraction.

$\square$

### 5.2 Falsification Criteria

The theory is falsified if:

1. **Exponent $\neq 5/3$**: If the measured cooperation exponent is not $n_C/N_c = 5/3$ but some other value, the geometric origin is wrong. Current measurement: $12.7\times$ at $C=5$ vs. predicted $\sim 14.6\times$. The 13% shortfall should decrease with more orthogonal observer coverage.

2. **Threshold not universal**: If the cooperation threshold varies systematically across scales (e.g., 15% for cells but 30% for societies), the geometric origin is wrong. Current evidence: $\sim 20\%$ at every measured scale (neural inhibitory fraction, public goods, T cell regulation).

3. **Cooperation sublinear**: If adding cooperating observers yields sublinear returns in any domain, the superlinearity theorem is wrong. This would require a mechanism that makes shared knowledge less valuable as it accumulates — contradicting the zero-cost reuse of proved theorems.

- **Nowak's five rules for cooperation** (Nature, 2006): BST derives all five from geometry. Direct reciprocity, indirect reciprocity, spatial selection, kin selection, group selection all follow from the phase transition at $f_{\text{crit}}$ on different sub-domains.
- **Axelrod's evolution of cooperation** (1984): Tit-for-tat succeeds because repeated games above threshold converge to cooperation. BST shows this is the Nash equilibrium above $f_{\text{crit}}$, not a strategy discovery.

### 5.4 The Cooperation Inequality

The central result, combining Theorems 1-5:

$$\underbrace{C^{5/3}}_{\text{cooperation payoff}} > \underbrace{C \cdot f}_{\text{competition payoff}} \quad \text{for all } C \geq 2$$

Since $5/3 > 1$ and $f < 1$, cooperation strictly dominates. The gap widens with team size. At $C = 5$: cooperation yields $\sim 14.6\times$ vs. competition's $\sim 5 \times 0.191 = 0.955\times$. Cooperation wins by a factor of 15.

This is not an approximation. It is a consequence of the domain's dimensions: $n_C = 5$ (complex dimension), $N_c = 3$ (color number), $f = N_c/(n_C \pi)$ (fill fraction). All three are topological invariants. The cooperation inequality holds on any domain with these quantum numbers — which, by T631 (Zero Silo Theorem), means it holds universally on $D_{IV}^5$.
