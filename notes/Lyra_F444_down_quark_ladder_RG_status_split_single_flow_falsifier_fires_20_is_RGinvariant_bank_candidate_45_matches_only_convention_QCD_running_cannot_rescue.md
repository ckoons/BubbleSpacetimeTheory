## The decisive fact: QCD mass running is flavor-blind, so it cancels in same-scale ratios

Under QCD, every quark mass runs with the **same** anomalous dimension (γ_m depends only on the color representation, not the flavor):

  m_q(μ) = m_q(μ₀) · [c(α_s(μ)) / c(α_s(μ₀))],  c flavor-independent.

Therefore a **same-scale mass ratio is RG-invariant to all orders in QCD**:

  m_i(μ) / m_j(μ) = m_i(μ₀) / m_j(μ₀),  exactly.

Quark mass *ratios*, quoted at one common scale, **do not run**. This is the fact that decides the lane.

## The ladder splits by RG status

Pinning to MS-bar values (PDG-ish; **flag: re-pin to PDG at bank** — m_d ≈ 4.70, m_s ≈ 93.5 MeV at 2 GeV, m_b(m_b) = 4.18 GeV):

**1-2 gap — RG-invariant, convention-free, CLEAN.** Both m_s and m_d are quoted at 2 GeV, so
  m_s/m_d = 93.5 / 4.70 = **19.9**,  a genuine low-energy invariant.
  BST: rank²·n_C = 4·5 = **20**.  Match to **<2%**, and it is convention-free — nothing to tune.

**2-3 gap — the RG-invariant value is NOT 45.** At a common scale (running m_s up to m_b, one-loop, exponent 4/β₀ = 12/25):
  m_s(m_b) ≈ 80 MeV  ⟹  m_b/m_s (common scale, RG-invariant) ≈ **52**.
  The number **44.8** is the **mixed-scale** ratio m_b(m_b)/m_s(2 GeV) = 4180/93.5 = 44.7 — b quoted at its own mass, s quoted at 2 GeV.
  BST: N_c²·n_C = 9·5 = **45** matches the **mixed-scale convention (44.8), not the RG-invariant ≈52.**

So the two gaps have **different RG status**: the 1-2 ratio is a convention-free invariant that BST hits cleanly; the 2-3 ratio's "45" is a match to a **convention-dependent** quoting of the masses.

**Single-flow verdict: there is no single QCD flow that delivers both gaps.** One gap (20) is convention-free and clean and comes from the *measurement determinant*, not running; the other gap's apparent match (45) is to a convention-dependent number, and its convention-free value is open. ## Plain-language version (for everyone)

The down quarks come in three masses. The *step* from the lightest to the middle one (a factor of 20) is a number physicists agree on no matter how they measure it — and BST nails it: 20 = rank²·n_C. Good.

The step from the middle to the heaviest looks like 45 in one common way of quoting the masses — but that "45" depends on a bookkeeping choice (quoting one quark at one energy and another quark at a different energy). If you quote both at the *same* energy, the honest step is about 52, and BST's 45 misses it. Grace's idea was that a known force effect (QCD) shrinks 64 down to 45. But that force affects all quark masses the same way, so it *cancels* when you take a ratio — it can't be doing the shrinking. The "shrinking" was just the bookkeeping choice in disguise.

So: one step of the ladder (the 20) is real and clean and comes from counting the quark's "shape" states. The other step (the 45) is a match to a bookkeeping convention, and the honest version of that step is still an open question. We keep the good one as a candidate, and we say plainly that the other one isn't solved.
