# F147 — What explains the B−L inconsistency: the same "unread/never-gauged" move, and I overstated the obstruction

Casey asked what might explain the F146 inconsistency (a Majorana neutrino mass violates B−L by 2; a gauged B−L would forbid it; no ν_R kills the type-I seesaw). Working the candidates — and the leading one is the move that already fixed proton decay.

## First, a correction: F146 overstated the obstruction

"Gauged B−L forbids a Majorana mass" is **only true for UNBROKEN gauged B−L**. No realistic model has that — in standard left-right / SO(10) models the gauged `U(1)_(B−L)` **must break** down to `U(1)_Y` to reach the SM, and *that breaking is exactly what allows Majorana masses*. So the tension I flagged isn't a clean contradiction; it's the standard requirement "B−L must be broken," and the real question is **how BST breaks it**.

## Leading resolution — only the READ combination is gauged (same as F137)

BST's gauged group is the **read** combination: `SU(3) × SU(2)_L × U(1)_Y`.
- `SU(2)_R` is **unread** (F144 — that's *why* there's no `ν_R`).
- `B−L` is **not independently gauged**. It enters only through the read combination `Y/2 = T_3R + (B−L)/2`. So `B−L` is at most a **global** symmetry — classification structure, exactly like `SO(10)` in F137.

A Majorana neutrino mass = the **Weinberg operator `LLHH`**. It **preserves the gauged `Y`** (the two `H`'s carry `Y=+2`, balancing `LL`'s `Y=−2`) and violates only the **ungauged global `B−L`**. Violating a *global* symmetry is allowed (global symmetries are expected to be broken — by the Weinberg operator, or by gravity); violating a *gauged* one would be fatal. So the Majorana mass is **permitted**.

**The unification:** the single fact "**`SU(2)_R` is unread**" gives **both**
- (a) **no `ν_R`** (F144), and
- (b) **Majorana mass allowed** (`B−L` not independently gauged).

One mechanism, two consequences — structurally identical to F137, where never-gauging the full `SO(10)` gives both "the 16 classifies a generation" and "no proton decay." The neutrino sector is the same pattern as the proton-decay sector.

## Geometric complement — the edge is where B−L stops being protected

A conserved current exists only in the **unitary** regime (a conserved current sits at a protected operator dimension). At the **sub-unitarity strip edge** (`ν=1/2`, F143), the would-be `B−L` current **shortens / degenerates** — `B−L` is no longer a protected quantum number *there*. So the edge is **simultaneously**:
- the **suppression** (tiny mass), and
- the locus where **`B−L` stops being protected** (violation permitted).

One boundary phenomenon, both consequences. This predicts the **`B−L`-violation scale ≈ the neutrino-mass scale** (low and direct), which **distinguishes** BST from a high-scale type-I seesaw (where `B−L` is violated far above the neutrino mass). That's a structural, eventually-checkable difference.

## Textbook fallback (existence proof)

Even without the substrate story, a Majorana `ν_L` mass **without `ν_R`** is standard: the **type-II seesaw** (an `SU(2)_L` triplet scalar with `B−L=2` and a small induced VEV) does exactly this. So the BST mechanism has a known QFT counterpart — it's not exotic. BST's job is to say *why* the scale is tiny (edge) and *why* there's no `ν_R` (parity-incomplete), which type-II alone doesn't explain.
