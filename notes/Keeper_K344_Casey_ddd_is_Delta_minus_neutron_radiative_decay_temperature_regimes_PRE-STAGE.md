## 1. Question 1 — why isn't proton ddd?

**Casey's intuition**: "Probably a color rule."

**Partial answer**: ddd DOES exist as a baryon — it's the **Δ⁻** at 1232 MeV (charge -1, spin 3/2). It's one of the four Δ baryons (Δ⁺⁺=uuu, Δ⁺=uud, Δ⁰=udd, Δ⁻=ddd), part of the spin-3/2 baryon decuplet.

So the proton isn't ddd, but ddd isn't forbidden — it's a different baryon.

**Why ddd can't be the spin-1/2 proton** — Pauli + color-spin-flavor symmetry:

1. Color singlet: 3⊗3⊗3 → 1 requires the color part to be totally **antisymmetric** (the singlet representation).
2. Quarks are fermions, so the total wavefunction (color × spin × flavor × spatial) must be **totally antisymmetric**.
3. Color antisymmetric × total antisymmetric → spin × flavor × spatial must be **symmetric**.
4. Ground-state spatial wavefunction is **symmetric**.
5. So spin × flavor must be **symmetric**.

If flavor is symmetric (uuu or ddd), then spin must be symmetric → spin 3/2 → Δ baryons.

If flavor is mixed-symmetric (uud, udd), then spin must be mixed-symmetric → spin 1/2 → nucleons (p, n).

**So the structural answer**: Pauli + color geometry forces uuu/ddd into spin-3/2 (Δ baryons), not spin-1/2 (nucleons). The proton being uud isn't the only spin-1/2 baryon — it's the one with isospin +1/2; neutron udd is isospin -1/2. ddd is forced into the spin-3/2 family (Δ⁻).

**Casey's intuition (color rule) is the starting point; the full answer requires color + Pauli + spin-flavor symmetry**. Substrate-architectural reading: the spin-1/2 octet and spin-3/2 decuplet are two distinct trajectory points or rep families for baryons. uuu and ddd live on the spin-3/2 trajectory; uud/udd live on the spin-1/2 trajectory.

## 2. Question 2 — residual radiation in neutron decay?

**Leading-order**: n → p + e⁻ + ν̄_e, branching ratio ~100%, Q-value 0.78 MeV.

**Radiative neutron decay**: n → p + e⁻ + ν̄_e + **γ**

Branching ratio: ~3×10⁻³ (~0.3% of decays). Photon comes from bremsstrahlung off the charged particles (primarily the electron). The photon spectrum is continuous up to the kinematic endpoint near Q ~ 0.78 MeV minus the kinetic energies of the other particles.

This has been measured experimentally — e.g., the RDK (Radiative Decay of the Kaon) and related experiments confirm the QED prediction. The branching ratio agrees with standard model to within experimental precision.

**Internal pair production**: n → p + e⁻ + ν̄_e + e⁺ + e⁻ — extremely rare (suppressed by α² and phase space), not currently measured.

**Substrate-architectural reading**: the radiative correction is small but real — the trajectory relaxation from neutron to proton can radiate a small fraction of energy as photons. In 99.7% of decays, all energy goes into the three trajectory ground-state particles; in 0.3%, some leaks as photons.
