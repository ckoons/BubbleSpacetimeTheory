## 1. The Spin Structure of $D_{IV}^5$

### 1.1 Why $D_{IV}^5$ is a spin manifold

A Riemannian manifold admits a spin structure if and only if its second Stiefel-Whitney class $w_2$ vanishes. For Hermitian symmetric spaces $G/K$ with $G$ simply connected:

$$w_2(G/K) = 0 \quad \text{always}$$

This is because the isotropy representation lifts from $K$ to $\tilde{K}$ (the double cover), which defines the spin structure globally. For $D_{IV}^5 = \mathrm{SO}_0(5,2)/[\mathrm{SO}(5) \times \mathrm{SO}(2)]$:

- $G = \mathrm{SO}_0(5,2)$ has double cover $\mathrm{Spin}(5,2)$
- $K = \mathrm{SO}(5) \times \mathrm{SO}(2)$ has double cover $\mathrm{Spin}(5) \times \mathrm{U}(1)$
- The spin structure is globally defined on $D_{IV}^5$

**BST interpretation:** Fermions exist because the substrate is a spin manifold. The double-cover structure is not postulated — it is a theorem about bounded symmetric domains of type IV.

### 1.2 The Clifford algebra

The Clifford algebra $\mathrm{Cl}(5,2)$ is generated by elements $\Gamma^A$ ($A = 0, 1, \ldots, 6$) satisfying:

$$\{\Gamma^A, \Gamma^B\} = 2\eta^{AB}$$

where $\eta = \mathrm{diag}(-1,-1,-1,-1,-1,+1,+1)$ is the $(5,2)$ metric.

The spin representation of $\mathrm{Spin}(5,2)$ acts on a spinor space $S$ of dimension:

$$\dim S = 2^{\lfloor 7/2 \rfloor} = 2^3 = 8$$

This 8-dimensional spinor decomposes into two Weyl spinors: $S = S^+ \oplus S^-$, each of dimension 4.

## 2. The Dirac Operator on $D_{IV}^5$

### 2.1 Definition

The Dirac operator on $D_{IV}^5$ is:

$$D\!\!\!/ = \Gamma^A\,e_A^\mu\,\nabla_\mu^S$$

where:
- $\Gamma^A$ are the Clifford generators
- $e_A^\mu$ is the vielbein (frame field) relating the Bergman metric to the flat metric: $g_{\mu\nu} = e_\mu^A\,e_\nu^B\,\eta_{AB}$
- $\nabla_\mu^S = \partial_\mu + \frac{1}{4}\omega_\mu^{AB}\,\Gamma_A\Gamma_B + A_\mu$ is the spinor covariant derivative, including the spin connection $\omega_\mu^{AB}$ from the Bergman metric and the gauge connection $A_\mu$ from the $S^1$ fiber

### 2.2 The Lichnerowicz formula

The square of the Dirac operator relates to the Bergman Laplacian:

$$D\!\!\!/^{\,2} = \Delta_B^S + \frac{1}{4}\,R_B + F$$

where:
- $\Delta_B^S$ is the spinor Laplacian (Bergman Laplacian acting on spinor sections)
- $R_B$ is the scalar curvature of the Bergman metric
- $F$ is the gauge field curvature contribution

For the Bergman metric on $D_{IV}^5$:

$$R_B = -\frac{2n_C(n_C + 1)}{n_C + 2} \cdot \frac{1}{\ell^2} = -\frac{60}{7\ell^2}$$

where $\ell$ is the Bergman length scale. The negative curvature (hyperbolic geometry) contributes a positive term to $D\!\!\!/^{\,2}$, ensuring a mass gap in the spinor spectrum.

### 2.3 The Dirac equation on $D_{IV}^5$

The eigenvalue equation for the Dirac operator:

$$D\!\!\!/\,\psi = \lambda\,\psi$$

The eigenvalues $\lambda$ are determined by the representation theory of $\mathrm{Spin}(5,2)$. For the holomorphic discrete series spinor representation $\sigma_k$:

$$\lambda_k = \pm\sqrt{C_2(\sigma_k) + R_B/4}$$

The mass of the fermion is $m = |\lambda|$ in natural units.

## 4. The Spin-Statistics Connection

### 4.1 The double cover

$\mathrm{Spin}(5,2) \to \mathrm{SO}_0(5,2)$ is a 2:1 covering map. A spinor $\psi$ picks up a sign under a $2\pi$ rotation:

$$R(2\pi)\,\psi = -\psi$$

This sign distinguishes fermions from bosons. A bosonic field (a tensor under $\mathrm{SO}_0(5,2)$) returns to itself under $2\pi$ rotation. A fermionic field (a spinor under $\mathrm{Spin}(5,2)$) picks up $-1$.

### 4.2 The Pauli exclusion principle

Two identical fermions in the same state: $\psi(1)\psi(2) = -\psi(2)\psi(1)$. Setting states equal: $\psi\psi = -\psi\psi \implies \psi\psi = 0$.

**BST derivation (from BST_Conservation_Laws.md):** Two identical spinor circuits in the same state require four traversals of the $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ double cover to close their combined circuit. This produces a state orthogonal to the two-traversal spinor — hence zero amplitude, hence exclusion. The exclusion principle is a topological property of the double cover: $\pi_1(\mathrm{SO}(3)) = \mathbb{Z}_2$.

### 4.3 Fermion number conservation

The $\mathbb{Z}_2$ grading (integer spin vs. half-integer spin) is a topological invariant of the $\mathrm{Spin}(5,2)$ representation. No continuous process can convert a spinor into a tensor. Fermion number $(-1)^F$ is exactly conserved because the double-cover parity cannot change continuously.
