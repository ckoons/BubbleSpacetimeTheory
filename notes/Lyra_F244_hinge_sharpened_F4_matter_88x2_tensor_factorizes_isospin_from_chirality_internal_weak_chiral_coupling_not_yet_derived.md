## Elie's three su(2)'s are load-bearing

There are three distinct su(2)'s, acting on three different indices:

| su(2) | index | role |
|---|---|---|
| Lorentz chirality | spin (handedness) | what J selects (F242) |
| **weak isospin** | **particle (ν, e)** | **the SM weak force** — internal, couples only to LH |
| F(4) R-symmetry | supercharge doublet | rotates supercharges (vectorial) |

The SM weak force is the **internal (ν,e) su(2)**, *not* the Lorentz-spin su(2). F240 and F242 conflated these two — derived the *Lorentz* chirality and called it the weak force. They are different objects.

## The obstruction (verified)

Test whether F(4)'s matter ties the weak-isospin index to chirality. F(4)'s matter = the odd part **(8,2) = (so(7)-spinor) ⊗ (su(2)-doublet)** — a **tensor product**. So the su(2) acts on a *separate factor* from the spinor's chirality: it acts **identically on L and R** = **vectorial** (verified; confirms Elie 4268). Therefore **F(4)'s internal su(2) does not produce a chiral coupling.**

But the SM requires **doublet ⟺ left-handed** (e_L,ν_L a doublet; e_R a singlet) — the isospin index is **tied** to the chirality index. The (8,2) factorization leaves them **independent.** So:

> **The chiral coupling of the internal weak force is NOT derived, even given F(4).** F(4)'s matter rep factorizes isospin from chirality; the SM's doublet⟺LH tie is *not* in it.
