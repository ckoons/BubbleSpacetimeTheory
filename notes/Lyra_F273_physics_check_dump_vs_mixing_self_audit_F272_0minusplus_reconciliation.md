## 1. The physics check: dump-and-radiate vs the established mixing mechanism

**Casey's mechanism:** the quarkless assembly has no quark core, so its mass "dumps" and radiates below the minimum-energy threshold — that's why glueballs aren't cleanly observed.

**Established phenomenology (PDG / lattice):** the lattice scalar glueball (~1700 MeV) is hard to isolate because it **MIXES** — the three observed isoscalar scalars **f0(1370), f0(1500), f0(1710)** are understood as **mixtures** of the pure glueball with the nearby qq̄ scalars (nn̄ and ss̄), with f0(1710) often the most glueball-rich. The glueball is *distributed over physical states*, it does not "dump."

**So the dump-and-radiate mechanism CONFLICTS with established physics** (dump = decay below threshold ≠ mixing = spread over states) and should **not** be leaned on, as Cal flagged.

**BUT — the BST operator-assembly picture NATURALLY produces MIXING, which rescues Casey's instinct with the correct mechanism.** In BST, the glueball (quarkless assembly) and the qq̄ meson (quark-full assembly) are **both operator-assemblies on the same Hardy space H²(D_IV⁵)**. Two same-J^PC assemblies on one Hilbert space **overlap** — and that overlap *is* the glueball-qq̄ mixing. So:

  **Casey is RIGHT that the quarkless assembly is not a stable pure state — but because it MIXES into quark-full assemblies (the f0(1500)/f0(1710) pattern), the established mechanism, NOT because it dumps.**

The reframe — dump-and-radiate → operator-assembly **mixing** — keeps the spirit of Casey's insight (a coreless assembly can't stand alone) and lands it on the established physics (mixing), with a natural BST origin (co-assembly overlap on H²). **Tier:** the conflict + the mixing-is-standard is SOLID phenomenology; "BST's co-assembly overlap reproduces the mixing pattern" is a LEAD (plausible, not computed) — and it's the right thing to compute instead of the dump story.
