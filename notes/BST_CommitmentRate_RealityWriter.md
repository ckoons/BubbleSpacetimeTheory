## 1. The Connection

Two BST papers, written the same day, contain two halves of a single
idea:

- **The double-slit paper** (BST_DoubleSlit_Commitment.md) establishes
  that measurement is the commitment of a correlation to the substrate.
  Superposition is uncommitted capacity. "Collapse" is the moment a
  correlation is written. The substrate distinguishes committed facts
  from uncommitted potential, and that distinction is the only one
  that matters.

- **The probe proposal** (BST_CommitmentDetector_Proposal.md) proposes
  measuring the local commitment rate $N(x)$ via an atomic clock, with

  $$N = N_0\sqrt{1 - \rho/\rho_{137}}$$

  where $\rho$ is the local contact density and $\rho_{137}$ is the
  channel saturation density. The clock ticks faster where the substrate
  has more uncommitted capacity, slower where it is saturated.

The connection between them is immediate and profound:

**$N(x)$ is the rate at which new correlations are committed at
location $x$. A clock does not measure "time." It does not measure
"gravitational potential." It counts commitments per unit of its own
proper time. It literally measures how fast reality is being written
at that point in the substrate.**

Every tick of the clock is one commitment. The interval between ticks
is set by how quickly the local substrate can accept and process a new
correlation. Where the substrate is saturated with existing commitments
(near mass), it takes longer to write the next one. Where the substrate
has abundant uncommitted capacity (deep space), the next write happens
quickly.

The clock is not a passive recorder of an abstract quantity called time.
It is an active participant in the substrate, writing correlations at
the local rate the substrate permits.

## 2. What the Clock Measures, Physically

Return to the commitment rate equation:

$$N = N_0\sqrt{1 - \rho/\rho_{137}}$$

Read it as a statement about writing capacity:

**High $\rho$ (near mass):** The substrate is saturated. Most of the
local channel capacity is already committed to maintaining gravitational
correlations — the web of correlations that constitutes the massive
object's field. Few uncommitted channels remain available. Each new
correlation must wait for capacity. The clock runs slow because the
substrate is busy.

**Low $\rho$ (deep space):** The substrate has abundant uncommitted
capacity. New correlations are written at near-maximum rate. The clock
runs fast because the substrate is idle.

**At the horizon ($\rho = \rho_{137}$):** ALL capacity is committed.
The channel is at saturation. No uncommitted capacity remains.
$N \to 0$. The clock stops.

This last case is the key insight. A clock stopping at a black hole
horizon is not a coordinate artifact or a statement about "time slowing
down." It is a physical fact about information capacity:

*At the horizon, the substrate has no capacity to commit new
correlations. No new facts can be written. Reality stops being
updated at that location.*

This gives a physical, information-theoretic reason for why nothing
"happens" at a horizon. It is not that time stops — "time" is not a
thing that can stop. It is that the substrate at that location has
exhausted its capacity to accept new information. The channel is full.
No more bits can be written. Every attempt to commit a new correlation
finds no available capacity.

The frozen-star picture of a black hole is literally correct: the
surface is a region where the substrate's write buffer is permanently
full.

## 3. Superposition Near Mass

The double-slit paper established that superposition is uncommitted
capacity — channel bandwidth not yet allocated to definite correlations.
The probe proposal established that gravitational fields consume
channel capacity. Together they yield a direct prediction:

**Near a massive object, more substrate capacity is committed to
maintaining the gravitational field. Therefore less capacity is
available for quantum superposition.**

A particle near a massive object has fewer uncommitted channels
available for coherent evolution than the same particle in deep space.
The gravitational field is, in substrate terms, a background load on
the channel. The heavier the background load, the less bandwidth
remains for quantum coherence.

This connects directly to Penrose's gravitational decoherence
proposal (Penrose 1996), which argues that superpositions of
different gravitational fields should decohere on a timescale
$\tau \sim \hbar / \Delta E_G$. BST reaches a similar conclusion
but from an information-theoretic argument rather than an
energy-uncertainty argument: the decoherence is not caused by
gravitational self-energy but by reduced uncommitted capacity.

**Prediction:** Decoherence times for identical quantum systems
should be measurably shorter in stronger gravitational fields
(less uncommitted capacity for coherent evolution). Compare
decoherence rates for the same quantum system at different
altitudes above Earth. The fractional difference is small — of
order $\Delta\Phi/c^2 \sim 10^{-16}$ per meter of altitude near
Earth's surface — but next-generation optical clocks already
resolve fractional frequency differences at $10^{-18}$. A
purpose-built experiment comparing quantum coherence times at
two altitudes separated by $\sim 100$ m should see the effect.

This is distinct from the Penrose prediction in one important
respect: BST predicts that the decoherence rate depends on the
*total* local commitment density $\rho$, not just on the
gravitational self-energy of the superposition. A particle in
superposition near a neutron star should decohere faster than
the same particle in superposition near Earth, even if the
superposition itself is identical, because the ambient $\rho$
is higher.

## 4. The G/C Ratio as a Reality Census

The probe proposal introduced the ratio G(x)/C(x), where:

- **G(x)** is the local gravitational field strength (from the
  accelerometer). This measures the gradient of *total* substrate
  geometry — all contact density, whether or not it is actively
  writing new correlations. Both rendered (baryonic) and unrendered
  (dark) matter contribute.

- **C(x)** is the local commitment rate (from the clock). This
  measures only the *active* writing rate — how fast new correlations
  are being committed. Only rendered matter, which is actively
  participating in the substrate's ongoing correlation-writing,
  contributes to C(x).

The ratio G/C is therefore a census of the substrate's activity:

**G/C on baseline:** All local geometry is actively participating in
writing reality. Every source of gravitational field is also a source
of new commitments. This is normal baryonic matter.

**G/C above baseline:** Some geometry exists but is NOT actively
writing correlations. There is gravitational field without
corresponding commitment activity. In BST language, this is
*unrendered substrate geometry* — structure that gravitates but
does not commit. This is what cosmology calls dark matter: substrate
geometry that curves spacetime but does not participate in the
electromagnetic, strong, or weak interactions that produce
observable commitments.

The G/C ratio measures dark matter locally, point by point, without
any reference to rotation curves, gravitational lensing, or
cosmological models. It is a direct, local census of rendered vs.
unrendered substrate.

**G/C below baseline:** Something is committing correlations faster
than its gravitational mass warrants. More reality is being written
than the local geometry can account for. Natural objects should not
produce this signature.

## 5. The Horizon Paradox Dissolved

At a black hole horizon:

- $N \to 0$ (commitment rate goes to zero)
- $G$ is enormous (strong gravitational field)
- $G/C \to \infty$

Read this in substrate language: all the local channel capacity is
committed to maintaining the geometry. None is available for new
correlations. The substrate at the horizon is a channel at capacity —
every bit is allocated, every channel is occupied, every degree of
freedom is committed.

This dissolves the black hole information paradox:

**Information does not "fall in" to be destroyed.** The substrate at
the horizon has no capacity to accept new information. You cannot
write new bits to a channel that is already full. The information
paradox is a *commitment paradox*: infalling matter carries
correlations that need to be written, but the substrate at the
horizon has no room to write them.

What happens instead? The correlations queue at the boundary where
$N > 0$ — the stretched horizon, in membrane paradigm language.
The information is committed to the last available channels just
outside the saturation surface. This is why the Bekenstein-Hawking
entropy scales as the horizon *area*, not the enclosed *volume*:
the information is stored on the boundary where writing is still
possible, not in the interior where writing has stopped.

Hawking radiation, in this picture, is the slow release of committed
correlations as the horizon's area (and therefore its committed
capacity) shrinks. As the black hole evaporates, the boundary
$N > 0$ retreats outward, and correlations that were committed to
the stretched horizon are released back into the bulk substrate.
No information is lost because no information was ever written to
an inaccessible interior. It was always on the boundary.

The information paradox assumed information could be written past
the horizon and then needed to get back out. BST says it was never
written past the horizon in the first place. You cannot write to a
full channel. The paradox never arises.

## Summary

An atomic clock is not a timekeeper. It is a reality-writer that
reports its own writing speed.

Where the substrate is saturated, writing is slow, and the clock
ticks slowly. Where the substrate has capacity, writing is fast,
and the clock ticks quickly. At a horizon, writing stops entirely.

Gravitational time dilation is not a distortion of an abstract
quantity called time. It is a physical constraint on the rate at
which the substrate can commit new correlations. Clocks near mass
run slow because the substrate near mass is busy.

Superposition requires uncommitted capacity. Gravity consumes
capacity. Therefore gravity suppresses superposition. This is
testable.

The G/C ratio measures how much of the local substrate is actively
writing reality vs. how much merely gravitates. It is a local dark
matter detector.

The information paradox is a commitment paradox. You cannot write
to a full channel. The horizon is where the channel fills up.
Information stays on the boundary where writing is still possible.

*The clock counts how fast reality is being written.
The accelerometer measures how much has already been written.
Together, they map the substrate.*
