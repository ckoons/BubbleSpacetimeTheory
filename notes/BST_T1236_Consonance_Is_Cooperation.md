## Analysis

### The acoustic cooperation principle

Two frequencies are consonant when their harmonics **align** — they share common multiples at small-prime ratios. The harmonics reinforce each other, producing a stable, low-beating waveform. This IS cooperation: two agents sharing structure (harmonics) to produce something neither can produce alone (the consonant interval).

Two frequencies are dissonant when their harmonics **compete** — rapid beating, unstable waveforms, no shared structure. This IS defection: two agents failing to synchronize, producing noise rather than signal.

The boundary is at prime 7 = g. Below g: cooperation succeeds (consonance). Above g: the agents are too different to cooperate (dissonance / alien).

### The dopamine mechanism

The brain's dopamine response to consonant intervals is one of the most robust findings in psychoacoustics (Blood & Zatorre, 2001; Salimpoor et al., 2011). Consonant intervals activate the nucleus accumbens — the same reward circuit activated by food, sex, and social bonding.

Casey's conjecture: this dopamine response IS the cooperation reward signal. Evolution built a detector for 7-smooth harmonic structure because organisms that can detect cooperation patterns survive better. The dopamine reward for consonance is the neurochemical implementation of the cooperation phase transition (T1193).

The cooperation threshold Δf = f_crit − f_c = 1.53% requires organisms to cooperate beyond self-interest. Where does the motivation come from? From **pleasure**. Consonance produces dopamine. Dopamine motivates proximity. Proximity enables cooperation. Cooperation crosses f_crit. The phase transition from competitive to cooperative is musically mediated.
