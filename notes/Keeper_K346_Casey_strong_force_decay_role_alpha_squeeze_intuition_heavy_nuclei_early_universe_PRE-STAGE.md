# K346 PRE-STAGE Audit

## 1. Strong force has two faces — both mediate decay

**At the quark level (color force)**:
- α_s ~ 1 (strong coupling)
- Δ → N + π is strong decay
- Lifetime ~10⁻²³ s (huge coupling = fast process)
- Mechanism: quark-antiquark pair created from strong field rearranges into outgoing pion

**At the nucleon level (residual nuclear force)**:
- Binds nucleons into nuclei
- Mediates alpha decay through interplay with Coulomb
- Strong attraction binds; Coulomb repulsion squeezes; quantum tunneling escapes
- Lifetime varies widely (10⁻⁷ s to 10¹⁷ years depending on barrier)

Both are "strong-force related" but with different mechanisms. Casey's processing this is the right structural distinction.

## 2. Casey's "confinement reduced + squeeze" intuition — correct

**Binding energy per nucleon** peaks at iron-56 (~8.8 MeV/nucleon). Heavier nuclei have LOOSER per-nucleon binding.

**Why**: 
- Strong binding per nucleon SATURATES (short-range force, each nucleon only interacts with nearest neighbors)
- Coulomb repulsion grows as Z²/R ~ Z^(5/3) (long-range, all protons interact)
- → For high Z, Coulomb wins eventually

**The "squeeze"**: heavier nuclei get more Coulomb pressure pushing against same per-nucleon binding. Alpha tunneling becomes energetically favorable because:
- The 4-nucleon cluster (α = ²He⁴) is unusually tightly bound (28.3 MeV)
- Emitting it lowers Z by 2, A by 4 → reduces Coulomb significantly
- Tunneling probability through Coulomb barrier increases with Z

Casey's intuition that nuclear force "reduces confinement ratios" tracks: per-nucleon binding decreases past iron-56; the "squeeze" of Coulomb forces emission against the residual strong binding.

**Substrate-architectural reading**: nuclei are composite bound states; their stability depends on the balance between substrate-binding (residual strong) and substrate-repulsion (Coulomb). As composite scale grows, repulsion wins.

## 3. Rapid cooling decay modes — Casey's conjecture right

In **r-process nucleosynthesis** (supernovae, neutron-star mergers), rapid neutron capture produces neutron-rich nuclei FAR from stability. These have multiple decay modes simultaneously active:

| Mode | Mechanism | When dominant |
|---|---|---|
| Alpha decay | Coulomb tunneling | Z > 82 (heavy) |
| Spontaneous fission | Coulomb wins overall | A > 256 (very heavy) |
| Cluster decay | C-14, O-16 emission | Rare, heavy + magic numbers |
| Proton emission | Proton-rich | Far from neutron line |
| Beta-delayed neutron | β decay → excited state → n | Neutron-rich, r-process products |
| Fission cycling | Heavy fragment restarts r-process | Very neutron-rich |

R-process produces approximately half of all elements heavier than iron. Different terminal abundance pattern from slow-cooling s-process (which follows the valley of stability).

**Casey's conjecture**: rapid cooling locks in non-equilibrium nuclei with multiple modes active. **Correct** — that's exactly what r-process does.

## 4. Heavy nuclei in early warm/hot regimes — standard NO, with substrate-architectural caveats

**Standard answer**: NO. Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) at T ~ 10⁹ K produces only:
- Hydrogen: ~75% by mass
- Helium-4: ~25%
- Trace deuterium, helium-3, lithium-7

**Why BBN can't make heavier nuclei**:

1. **No stable A=5 or A=8 nuclei** — mass gap blocks continued buildup (no stable Li-5 or Be-8 to bridge to heavier)
2. **Deuterium bottleneck** — at T > 10⁹ K, photodissociation destroys D faster than it forms; until T < 10⁹ K, nucleus building can't start
3. **Density too low at bottleneck break** — by the time deuterium can survive, baryon density is too LOW for triple-alpha process (3 He-4 → C-12)
4. **Universe cools too fast** — three-body fusion requires sustained high density at right temperature; expansion timescale beats it

**At higher temperatures (T > 10¹² K)**: NO bound nucleons at all. Above the QCD transition, you have quark-gluon plasma — no protons, no neutrons, just free quarks and gluons. Nuclei can't exist.

**The "Goldilocks gap" for heavy nucleosynthesis**:
- Too hot (>10⁹ K): photodissociation
- Too low density (η ~ 10⁻¹⁰): nuclear statistical equilibrium doesn't favor heavy
- Too fast expansion: kinetics don't catch up

The early universe missed all three conditions for heavy nuclei. They had to wait for stars to provide:
- Lower temperature (10⁷ K for fusion)
- High density (stellar core)
- Long timescale (millions to billions of years)
