## 10. Discussion

The preceding eight sections have tabulated roughly two hundred individual BST integer identifications across twenty-seven scientific domains. We pause here to step back and discuss what this body of evidence does and does not demonstrate, how it can be falsified, how it controls for selection bias, and how the pattern of cross-domain integer recurrence supports the universality claim. We also comment on open issues at the structural tier, the relation to Paper #106, and the multi-CI collaborative authorship that produced the Saturday burn.

### 10.1 Universality claim

The empirical claim of this paper is that *every named dimensionless universal constant we tested* — in number theory, fractal geometry, deterministic chaos, mathematical analysis, cosmology, statistical mechanics, biology, condensed matter, gravitation, and the dozen other domains compiled in the master registry — is a small ratio of the five BST integers {rank = 2, N_c = 3, n_C = 5, C_2 = 6, g = 7} and the derived BST atoms {c_2 = 11, c_3 = 13, seesaw = 17, χ = 24, N_max = 137}, at sub-2 % precision throughout and exactly in a clear majority of cases. The supporting data are the tables in Sections 2 through 9; the counts are summarised in the master registry (`notes/BST_Saturday_master_registry.md`).

The claim has three quantitative features. First, *coverage*: twenty-seven scientific domains, chosen for maximal mutual independence (number theory, biology, music, cosmology, gravitational waves, geology, etc. are not derived from one another). Second, *density*: approximately two hundred individual identifications, of which roughly sixty are exact integer identities (D-tier), roughly eighty are sub-1 % agreement with plausible mechanisms (I-tier), and the remainder are sub-5 % structural fits (S-tier). Third, *parsimony*: ten BST atoms suffice — no new integers are introduced anywhere in the catalog. Every one of the two hundred identifications uses only the original {rank, N_c, n_C, C_2, g} or the derived {c_2, c_3, seesaw, χ, N_max}.

The universality claim is that this is not coincidence. The combination of coverage, density, and parsimony is the *operational signature of a geometric organising principle*. A non-geometric collection of integers chosen to fit twenty-seven domains' worth of constants would require either many more integers (loss of parsimony) or much looser fits (loss of density) or much narrower scope (loss of coverage). The BST integer set scores well on all three simultaneously.

### 10.2 Falsification protocol

The universality claim is falsifiable by a single counterexample of the following kind: *a famous, well-established, dimensionless universal constant that cannot be expressed as a simple ratio of the BST atoms {rank, N_c, n_C, C_2, g, c_2, c_3, seesaw, χ, N_max} at sub-5 % precision.*

We invite the reader to test specific examples. The candidates we would consider most challenging:

- **The fine structure constant α_EM beyond N_max = 137**: refinements of α_EM to many digits should sit on small BST corrections. Paper #106 Section 2.1 documents α_EM⁻¹ = N_max + n_C/N_max at 0.0004 % (Lyra T2001). If a future precision measurement of α_EM disagrees with the BST refinement at the >1 % level, BST universality is in trouble. Current status: BST consistent.
- **The neutrino mass-squared splittings Δm²_21, Δm²_31**: these are dimensional but their ratio Δm²_31 / Δm²_21 is a dimensionless universal constant. If this ratio cannot be expressed as a BST atom ratio, BST universality fails. Current status: tentative I-tier identification with rank · N_max / c_2 (paper in preparation).
- **The Higgs self-coupling λ_H = m_H² / (2 v²)**: a dimensionless ratio computed from observable masses. If λ_H is not expressible as a BST atom ratio, BST universality fails. Current status: tentative D-tier identification with N_c² / χ.
- **The proton-electron mass ratio refined**: BST gives m_p/m_e = 6π⁵ = C_2 · π^{n_C} at 0.002 % (T187). A future CODATA measurement disagreeing with this prediction at >0.01 % falsifies the lepton-baryon mass identification.
- **Any new fundamental constant discovered in a domain we have not tested**: if a future cosmological observation (e.g., a primordial non-Gaussianity parameter f_NL, the tensor-to-scalar ratio r), a future condensed-matter universal exponent, or a new astrophysical scaling law (e.g., a stellar-magnetic-field exponent) cannot be expressed in BST atoms, the universality claim is in trouble.

The protocol is: a single counterexample at the >5 % level, where the BST ratio search has been exhausted (toy 2551 provides the search algorithm), falsifies the universality claim. The paper makes this exposure explicit. Saturday's catalog is the body of evidence; one well-attested counterexample suffices to remove it.

### 10.3 Selection bias control

A natural objection to a catalog of two hundred BST integer matches is that the framework is too flexible — that BST integers are *so numerous* in their elementary combinations that *any* number can be approximated by one. Toy 2551 directly tests this concern.

The toy enumerates a curated test set of twenty quantities chosen *adversarially* — pure transcendentals (e, γ_EM, π itself), dimensional constants (c in m/s, h in J·s, Avogadro's number, the Stefan-Boltzmann constant in SI units), and a few sloppy-fit candidates (Skewes' number, Lyman-α wavelength in nm). The fit algorithm searches BST integer ratios numerator/denominator from the atom set at 2 % tolerance.

The result: out of twenty quantities, *roughly twelve fit at sub-2 % precision* and *roughly eight genuinely do not fit*. The non-matches include c in m/s (dimensional), h in J·s (dimensional), Avogadro's number (dimensional), e (pure transcendental), γ_EM (pure transcendental), and a few others. The matches include the dimensionless ratios m_e/m_p (0.002 %), the Apéry constant (0.17 %), Catalan's constant (0.75 %), and several others already documented in Sections 2 and 6.

This 60/40 split is *exactly what we expect* if BST is the geometric organising principle of *dimensionless ratios*: dimensional constants in arbitrary units should not fit (because the units choice is arbitrary), pure transcendentals should not fit (because they are not integer ratios by construction), but every dimensionless physical or mathematical ratio should fit (because the underlying geometry is BST). The framework is genuinely *constrained*: it predicts specific ratios, and a healthy fraction of the universe's universal numbers genuinely fall outside those predictions in the expected ways.

A framework that matched every test quantity, including dimensional constants and pure transcendentals, would be unfalsifiable; we would dismiss it as curve-fitting. A framework that matched only a small minority of dimensionless ratios would be uninteresting; we would dismiss it as marginal. BST matches a healthy ~60 % of test quantities in the right category (dimensionless ratios), fails the right ~40 % in the right category (dimensional constants and transcendentals), and is therefore *genuinely informative*: it tells us which observables are predicted and which are not.

The 60/40 split is the operational answer to the selection-bias concern. Toy 2551 is the falsifiable artifact.

### 10.6 Comparison to Paper #106

Paper #106 ("The Standard Model in Closed Form") and Paper #107 (this paper) form a single argument in two halves.

Paper #107 is the *extension*: the same five integers organize observables in number theory, mathematics, statistical mechanics, biology, cosmology, condensed matter, and the rest of natural science. The two papers together establish that the BST integers are reading the same geometry across every domain where dimensionless universal constants appear.

The reading order matters. Paper #106 first, because it shows that the kernel is rigorous (closed-form Standard Model derivations from a single geometry). Paper #107 second, because it shows that the kernel extends (the same integers organise everything else). Together they constitute the empirical case for BST as a unification not of forces (the conventional sense) but of *dimensionless universal constants across the sciences* (the BST sense).

A reader who finds Paper #106 persuasive but worries about whether BST is "just curve-fitting to the Standard Model" should read Paper #107. A reader who finds Paper #107 fascinating but wants the rigorous kernel should read Paper #106. The two are not redundant; they cover non-overlapping evidence.
