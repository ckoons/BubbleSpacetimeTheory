# F200 — Casey: we are inside a singularity; the bounded domain avoids the infinities; the vacuum is bled boundary-heat

Casey's speculation on the vacuum-energy origin: *"I don't like the nested nature — there has to be a first. I suspect we are inside a singularity. There must be a way of avoiding infinities. It's possible we are bleeding the heat energy of the singularity."* Three instincts, and each lands on a structural fact about D_IV⁵ — a **bounded** symmetric domain.

## 1. "Inside a singularity" + "avoiding infinities" = a bounded symmetric domain, literally

Bounded symmetric domains are the standard mathematical realization of *exactly* this — a singularity with a finite interior:

- The **Bergman kernel blows up at the boundary**: `K(z,z) ~ 1/N(z,z)^{genus} → ∞` as `z →` the Shilov boundary. The boundary **is** a density singularity.
- But in the **Bergman metric the boundary is at infinite geodesic distance** — the domain is **geodesically complete**; from inside, the boundary is *unreachable*.
- The **interior curvature is finite everywhere**: `κ_Bergman = −n_C = −5` (constant holomorphic sectional curvature, K204). No blow-up inside.

So the bounded domain **is** the infinity-avoidance mechanism: it confines the infinity to an infinitely-distant, unreachable edge and leaves a complete, finite-curvature interior. We are inside the singularity; the singularity is its own boundary; and the interior stays finite *because* the bound pins the infinity to the edge.

## 2. "There has to be a first" / no nesting = geodesic completeness

A bounded complete domain is **self-contained** — geodesic completeness means there is *no reachable outside.* So there is no regress, no nesting: **one domain, one singular boundary, and the boundary is the "first,"** sitting at infinite distance. You never have to ask "what's outside the singularity," because the geometry has no reachable outside. Completeness terminates the nested structure Casey dislikes — the boundary is the single edge of everything.

## 3. "Bleeding the heat of the singularity" — and a lead on Λ

The dynamics is the heat semigroup `exp(−τH_B)`, which flows **boundary data inward** (the Hardy/holomorphic extension). The boundary is the high-density (hot) end; the bulk is cooler; heat flows inward. So the bulk's vacuum energy is **bled heat from the boundary singularity** — exactly the F174–176 cycle (vacuum = reservoir; the manifold *receives* energy = the absorb leg), now with the reservoir **identified**: it is the boundary singularity.

**The lead (tiered — a lead, not a derivation):** we already have `Λ = exp(−280)` with `280 = 2^{N_c}·n_C·g`, absurdly tiny. In a complete negatively-curved space, heat from a source decays **exponentially with distance**. If the source is the boundary (infinite distance) and you measure deep in the interior, what reaches you is `~ exp(−d)` for an effective bleed-action `d`. So an **exponentially small Λ is precisely the shape** of boundary-heat bled across a near-infinite Bergman distance into the deep interior — and `280` would be that effective action. This would reframe the cosmological-constant *smallness* problem ("why 10⁻¹²⁰?") into "of course it's exponentially small — it's residual boundary-heat at the bottom of an infinitely-deep well."
