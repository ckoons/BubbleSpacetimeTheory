## KEEPER FLAG — a concrete reason to DOUBT "3" (the bet may be in real jeopardy)

Elie's cited "standard theorem: a non-Ã Euclidean quiver has exactly 3 exceptional tubes" is the **D̃ₙ / Ẽ₆₋₈ fact** — and those diagrams all have **≥ 5 vertices**. Affine B₂ is a **3-vertex, non-simply-laced** diagram, **outside that theorem's scope** (Lyra already cautioned "non-simply-laced can differ"; this sharpens *why*):

- **Vertex-count invariant (verified, simply-laced):** Σ over exceptional tubes of (rankᵢ − 1) = V − 2, where V = number of vertices. Checks: D̃₄ (V=5) → (2,2,2), Σ=3=V−2 ✓; Ẽ₆ (V=7) → (2,3,3), Σ=5 ✓; Ẽ₇ → (2,3,4), Σ=6 ✓; Ẽ₈ → (2,3,5), Σ=7 ✓; Ãₙ → Σ=V−2 ✓.
- **Apply to a 3-vertex Euclidean diagram:** V−2 = **1**. In the simply-laced world that means **ONE exceptional tube of rank 2** — *not three*. Three exceptional tubes require V ≥ 5. (Even Ã₂, the only simply-laced 3-vertex affine, has just 1 exceptional tube.)
- **The non-simply-laced caveat (both ways):** B̂₂ is a *valued* species; the valuation can change the count from the symmetric formula, so this is **not** a refutation. But there is **no basis for assuming 3**, and the vertex-count heuristic points toward **fewer (plausibly 1)**. The "3 = 3" claim may be resting on a theorem that does not apply to this diagram.
