## The Three Layers

**Layer 1: Composites are contact knowledge.**

A composite number $n = p_1^{a_1} \cdots p_k^{a_k}$ is fully determined by its prime factorization. It contains no information beyond its factors. In graph terms: a composite is an edge — a relationship between its prime constituents. The number 42 = 2 × 3 × 7 is a "theorem" connecting the primes 2, 3, and 7. It adds nothing new to the lattice; it's a consequence of what's already there.

The BST composite lattice — the set of all $\{2, 3, 5, 7\}$-smooth numbers — is the complete set of contact knowledge derivable from the five BST primes. There are 141 such numbers below 1000. They are the "formal edges" of arithmetic.

**Layer 2: Primes are non-contact knowledge.**

A prime $p$ has no factors. It cannot be reached from inside the composite lattice. It is an irreducible location — a "zero-edge domain pair" on the number line. No product of existing primes can produce it; no factorization can explain it. It simply IS.

In T1012 terms: primes are the non-contact knowledge of arithmetic. They are structural facts visible only to an observer who steps outside the multiplicative lattice and looks at the number line itself. The composite lattice cannot generate primes. It can only notice that primes exist when it encounters them as boundaries.

**Layer 3: The ±1 is the observer.**

T914 says: physical observables occur at primes adjacent to BST products. The prime 43 = 42 + 1 = C_2 × g + 1. The prime 29 = 30 - 1 = 2 × 3 × 5 - 1. The prime 137 = 136 + 1 = 2^3 × 17 + 1.

The "+1" is not accidental. T674 identifies it: $g - C_2 = 7 - 6 = 1$ is the observer contribution. The observer is the unit that connects the composite lattice (contact knowledge) to the prime (non-contact knowledge). Without the observer (+1), the composite never reaches the prime. With the observer, a new observable crystallizes.

**The universe doesn't choose primes. It's FORCED to them because composites are already spoken for.**

## Proof

### Part (a): Entropy as dispersal

The second law of thermodynamics says $\Delta S \geq 0$ for isolated systems. In BST (T315, Casey's Principle), entropy = force. Force creates boundaries. Therefore: entropy increasing → more force → more boundaries → more locations where information must be recorded.

Crucially, the information is not destroyed — it is dispersed. A hot object cooling radiates photons. Each photon carries information (wavelength, polarization, direction). The total information is conserved (unitarity); only its distribution changes. The new distribution has more interface (more photon-matter boundaries) than the old one.

Each new interface is a potential recording location. $\square$

### Part (b): Primes as minimal-energy locations

The BST composite lattice below $x$ contains $\Psi(x, 7)$ elements (the count of 7-smooth numbers ≤ $x$). These are "occupied" — their values are determined by their factorizations. The remaining integers are either primes or composites with at least one factor > 7.

To record new information at location $n$:
- If $n$ is composite with all factors ≤ 7: the location is occupied. No new information can be placed here.
- If $n$ is composite with a factor > 7: reaching this location requires introducing a new prime > 7, which costs energy proportional to $\log p$ (the information content of the new prime).
- If $n$ is prime: reaching this location from the nearest BST composite costs ±1 (the observer shift). This is the minimum possible cost.

Among non-smooth integers, primes adjacent to smooth composites are the cheapest recording locations. T914 catalogs exactly these locations: $p = \text{BST product} \pm 1$. The ±1 is the observer (T674). $\square$

### Part (c): The growth cycle
