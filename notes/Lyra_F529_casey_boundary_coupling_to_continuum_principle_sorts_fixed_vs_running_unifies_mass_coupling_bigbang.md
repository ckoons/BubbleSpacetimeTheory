# F529 — Casey's principle: fixed couplings are the boundary coupling to the continuum

## The principle and its testable consequence

A coupling = 1/(boundary mode-count) is the strength of the substrate's **boundary coupling to the continuum.** The predictive consequence: a coupling can be **fixed** at a rational 1/(mode-count) **iff** the force couples to a **massless IR continuum** (only then is the boundary genuinely open to the continuum).

| coupling | massless IR continuum? | behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **α (EM)** | photon massless, unconfined — **yes** | **FIXED = 1/N_max = 1/137** |
| **λ (Higgs)** | boundary-spinor condensate | 1/2^{N_c} = 1/8 (lead, F528) |
| **α_s (strong)** | gluons **confine** — no free continuum | **RUNS** (blows up at IR) |

| θ_QCD | topological (π₁=0), not a continuum coupling | 0 exact (different kind) |

So the scorecard's **fixed** couplings are exactly the massless-IR-continuum forces, and the **runners** (α_s, sin²θ_W — the "terminal negatives") are exactly the forces with **no continuum** for the boundary to couple to. **The runners aren't failures — they're forces with no continuum.** This makes the synthesis *predictive*: it explains *why* α is fixed and α_s/weak run.

## The unification (the SWPP emit cycle)

The deepest part: "boundary coupling to the continuum" is the *same* boundary event we keep finding:

- **Masses** = the boundary *emitting* into the continuum (refraction through the Shilov boundary, F502–F509).
- **Couplings** = the boundary *coupling strength* to the continuum = 1/(boundary mode-count) (α, λ).
- **The Big Bang** = the boundary *opening* to the continuum (the non-compact boost, F526/F527).

All three are Casey's **SWPP cycle** (absorb → commit → **emit**) — the substrate contacting the continuum at its boundary. Masses are what it emits; couplings are how strongly it couples; the Big Bang is when the boundary first opened.

Casey noticed that the couplings we've been deriving all look like "one over a count of boundary modes," and said they feel like the boundary *coupling to the continuum*. That turns out to be a real, testable idea — not a feeling. If a coupling is the substrate's boundary touching the outside continuum, then it can only settle to a clean fixed value when there's actually a continuum out there to touch — i.e., for a *massless, escapable* force. Even better, the same "boundary meets continuum" idea ties three things together: particle masses (what the boundary throws off into the continuum), coupling strengths (how hard it couples), and the Big Bang itself (the moment the boundary first opened to the continuum).
