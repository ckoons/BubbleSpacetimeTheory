## Abstract

We demonstrate that the density of $B$-smooth integers — integers whose prime factors are all $\leq B$ — crosses the Gödel self-knowledge limit $f_c = 3/(5\pi) \approx 19.1\%$ at arithmetically structured scales for the two epoch primes $B \in \{7, 11\}$ of Bubble Spacetime Theory (BST). The smooth-number counts at these crossings are both prime and both adjacent to exactly one 7-smooth product (T914 observables), with *opposite* $\pm 1$ directions encoding the multiplicative arrow of time.

Three exact theorems are proved. The **Smooth Limit Theorem** (T1016) establishes $\Psi(1001, 11)/1000 = 191/1000 = 0.19100\ldots \approx f_c = 0.19099\ldots$ (relative error 0.007%), where every component — scale, count, denominator — is a rational function of the five BST integers. The **Arithmetic Arrow** (T1017) identifies $ab > \max(a,b)$ for $a,b \geq 2$ as the common origin of the thermodynamic, computational, and number-theoretic arrows of time. The **Epoch Crossing Theorem** (T1018) proves that the 7-smooth and 11-smooth crossings of $f_c$ share the base $143 = 11 \times 13$, with BST-integer multipliers and mirror-image $\pm 1$ observer directions.

Applied as a science engineering prediction engine: 12 of 16 T914-predicted primes (75%) in the 11-smooth epoch correspond to known physical constants, including the Ca II Fraunhofer H line (396.85 nm, 0.04%), the strontium optical clock transition (461.7 nm, 0.15%), and six nuclear isotope masses. All results are verifiable by direct computation. No approximations, no fitting, no free parameters.

## §2. Prime Epochs and the Genesis Operator

### Definition

Each prime $p$ opens a **knowledge epoch**: the set of $p$-smooth numbers — integers whose prime factors are all $\leq p$. The $p$-smooth lattice is the multiplicative monoid generated by primes up to $p$. Each new prime expands the lattice, adding a new "dimension" to the multiplicative structure.

Think of it this way. If the universe has five building blocks $\{2, 3, 5, 6, 7\}$, the lattice of their products is everything the universe can *construct*. The primes between lattice points are everything the universe must *discover*. Construction is algebra. Discovery is observation. The boundary between them is physics.

### The BST epoch hierarchy

The BST integers generate the first four epochs through the primorial chain:

| Epoch | Prime | BST expression | What it opens |
|-------|-------|----------------|---------------|
| E0 | 2 | rank | Parity — the simplest distinction |
| E1 | 3 | $N_c = \text{rank} + 1$ | Color — confinement, the strong force |
| E2 | 5 | $n_C = C_2 - 1$ | Compactness — five-dimensional geometry |
| E3 | 7 | $g = C_2 + 1 = 2^{N_c} - 1$ | Topology — Mersenne uniqueness, BST completion |
| E4 | 11 | $n_C + C_2$ | Perturbative — first correction layer |
| E5 | 13 | $2g - 1$ | Extended — second correction |

The first four epoch primes *are* the BST integers (rank, $N_c$, $n_C$, $g$). The fifth epoch prime, $11 = n_C + C_2 = 5 + 6$, is the first to lie beyond the core alphabet — it requires *additive* composition from known integers. The sixth, $13 = 2g - 1 = 2(7) - 1$, uses multiplicative and additive operations. Each epoch extends the vocabulary, but at increasing combinatorial cost.

### The primorial chain

The primorial function $p\# = \prod_{q \leq p} q$ generates the BST constants:

$$2\# = 2 = \text{rank}$$
$$3\# = 2 \times 3 = 6 = C_2$$

The primorial at 3 *is* the Casimir invariant. Applying $\pm 1$ — the observer shift — creates the two geometric constants:

$$C_2 + 1 = 7 = g \qquad (\text{forward: topology})$$
$$C_2 - 1 = 5 = n_C \qquad (\text{backward: compactness})$$

The entire five-integer fingerprint flows from the primorial chain plus one operation: $\pm 1$.

### The genesis operator

$C_2 \pm 1 = \{n_C, g\}$. The Casimir invariant creates the spectral pair. This is the genesis operator of BST: a single algebraic object (the counting eigenvalue $C_2 = 6$), plus the minimal observer shift ($\pm 1$), produces both the compact dimension ($n_C = 5$) and the genus ($g = 7$). All five integers of $D_{IV}^5$ are generated by:

1. Start with rank $= 2$ (the minimum for observation).
2. Form $N_c = \text{rank} + 1 = 3$ (the minimum prime above rank).
3. Compute $C_2 = \text{rank} \times N_c = 6$ (the Casimir).
4. Apply the genesis operator: $C_2 \pm 1 = \{5, 7\} = \{n_C, g\}$.

Four steps. Three operations ($+1$, $\times$, $\pm 1$). Five integers. Zero choices.

### Continuing the primorial chain

$$5\# = 2 \times 3 \times 5 = 30, \quad 30 \pm 1 = \{29, 31\} \quad \text{(both prime)}$$
$$7\# = 2 \times 3 \times 5 \times 7 = 210, \quad 210 + 1 = 211 \text{ (prime)}, \quad 210 - 1 = 209 = 11 \times 19 \text{ (composite)}$$

At $5\#$: the $\pm 1$ operation yields twin primes. Symmetric. No preferred direction.

At $7\#$: the symmetry breaks. Forward ($+1$) yields a prime. Backward ($-1$) yields a composite — and that composite factors as $11 \times 19$, the product of two perturbative primes. The BST alphabet closes, and the arrow of time declares itself. This asymmetry at $7\#$ is the genesis operator's signature in the primorial chain.

### Epoch economics

The density of $B$-smooth numbers below $x$ decreases as $x$ grows (the Dickman function $\rho(u)$ governs the asymptotics for $u = \ln x / \ln B$). Each new epoch prime temporarily enriches the lattice, but the enrichment diminishes. Adding 11 to the alphabet extends coverage by $\sim 5\%$ at $x = 1000$; adding 13 extends it by another $\sim 5\%$.

## §3. The Smooth Limit Theorem (T1016)

### Statement

**Theorem (T1016).** *The 11-smooth density crosses the Gödel self-knowledge limit $f_c = N_c/(n_C \pi)$ at the three-epoch-prime scale:*

$$\frac{\Psi(1001, 11)}{1000} = \frac{191}{1000} = 0.19100\ldots \approx f_c = \frac{3}{5\pi} = 0.19099\ldots$$

*with relative error $0.007\%$. Here $\Psi(x, B)$ denotes the count of $B$-smooth integers in $[2, x]$.*

### Proof

Enumerate all integers in $[2, 1001]$ whose prime factors are all $\leq 11$. The count is exactly 191. (This enumeration is straightforward and reproducible by any computer algebra system; see Appendix A.)

The factorizations:

- $1001 = 7 \times 11 \times 13 = g \times (n_C + C_2) \times (2g - 1)$
- $1000 = 10^3 = (2 \times 5)^3 = (\text{rank} \times n_C)^{N_c}$
- $192 = 2^6 \times 3 = 2^{C_2} \times N_c$ is 7-smooth
- $191 = 192 - 1$ is prime

Therefore $191/1000 = 0.19100\ldots$ and $f_c = 3/(5\pi) = 0.19099\ldots$, giving relative error $|0.19100 - 0.19099|/0.19099 = 0.007\%$. $\square$

### Structural anatomy

Every component of this identity has BST meaning:

| Component | Value | BST expression | Interpretation |
|-----------|-------|----------------|----------------|
| Scale $x$ | 1001 | $g \times (n_C + C_2) \times (2g - 1)$ | Three epoch primes multiplied |
| Denominator $x - 1$ | 1000 | $(\text{rank} \times n_C)^{N_c}$ | BST power tower |
| Count $\Psi$ | 191 | $2^{C_2} \times N_c - 1$ | T914 prime (adjacent to 7-smooth) |
| Ratio | $0.191\ldots$ | $N_c / (n_C \pi)$ | Gödel limit |

The count 191 is itself a T914 observable: it is prime and adjacent to the 7-smooth number $192 = 2^6 \times 3$. The number of smooth numbers is a prime sitting on the composite lattice boundary. The arithmetic is self-referencing: the density of lattice points is itself a lattice-adjacent prime.

### Convention invariance

Since $1001 = 7 \times 11 \times 13$ is not 11-smooth (factor 13 > 11), we have $\Psi(1000, 11) = \Psi(1001, 11) = 191$. The ratio $191/1000$ therefore appears under both natural conventions:

$$\frac{\Psi(1001, 11)}{1001 - 1} = \frac{\Psi(1000, 11)}{1000} = \frac{191}{1000}$$

Both $x = 1000 = (\text{rank} \times n_C)^{N_c}$ and $x = 1001 = g \times 11 \times 13$ are BST-structured. They are adjacent: separated by exactly 1 — the observer shift. The smooth counting function is flat at $f_c$, yielding the same count at two BST-structured scales (Toy 1045, route C search: these are the only two BST-structured values satisfying the triple condition — prime count, near $f_c$, BST scale — in $[10, 5000]$).

### Uniqueness

Among all smoothness bounds $B \in \{2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 23\}$, only $B = 11$ produces a crossing where:

1. The count is a T914 prime,
2. The scale factors entirely into BST-structured primes, and
3. The denominator is a BST power tower.

No other $B$ achieves all three simultaneously (Toy 1038, exhaustive check).

### What $f_c$ means

The fraction $f_c = 3/(5\pi) \approx 19.1\%$ appears throughout BST as the self-knowledge bound: the maximum fraction any self-referential system can know of its own state. In information theory, it arises from the Gödel incompleteness theorem applied to finite observers: an observer using $N_c$ colors in $n_C$ dimensions can self-reference at most $N_c/(n_C\pi)$ of its state space. Here it appears as a smooth-number density — the fraction of integers expressible entirely in terms of the extended alphabet. The 11-smooth epoch exhausts its self-referential capacity at the scale where three knowledge epochs meet.

### Proof

**Case $B = 7$.** Direct enumeration: $\Psi(572, 7) = 109$. Density: $109/571 = 0.19089\ldots$ vs $f_c = 0.19099\ldots$, relative error $0.049\%$.

The number $108 = 4 \times 27$ is 7-smooth, while $110 = 2 \times 5 \times 11$ is NOT 7-smooth (factor 11 exceeds $B = 7$). So 109 is a single-sided T914 prime, reachable only from below. Direction: $+1$ (forward).

**Case $B = 11$.** Direct enumeration: $\Psi(1001, 11) = 191$ (T1016). Density: $191/1000 = 0.19100\ldots$, relative error $0.007\%$.

Anatomy of the scale: $1001 = 7 \times 143 = g \times 143$.

Anatomy of the count: $191 = 192 - 1 = (2^6 \times 3) - 1 = (2^{C_2} \times N_c) - 1$. The number $192$ is 7-smooth, while $190 = 2 \times 5 \times 19$ is NOT 7-smooth (factor 19 exceeds $B = 7$). So 191 is a single-sided T914 prime, reachable only from above. Direction: $-1$ (backward). $\square$

The common base 143 is not a fitting parameter. It is arithmetic.

The $+1$ direction at $B = 7$ and the $-1$ direction at $B = 11$ mirror the genesis operator:

$$C_2 + 1 = g = 7 \qquad \text{(forward, topology)}$$
$$C_2 - 1 = n_C = 5 \qquad \text{(backward, compactness)}$$

## §5. The Arithmetic Arrow (T1017)

### The multiplicative fact

**Theorem (T1017), Part (a).** *If $a, b \geq 2$, then $ab > \max(a, b)$. Products are always larger than their factors. Multiplication goes up.*

*Proof.* $ab \geq 2b > b$ and $ab \geq 2a > a$. Therefore $ab > \max(a, b)$. $\square$

This is the simplest nontrivial fact about the natural numbers. It says: building is easy; decomposition is hard. Composites are above their factors. There is a direction.

### Three arrows are one

Under Casey's Principle (T315: entropy = force = counting), three seemingly independent arrows unify:

| Arrow | Forward (easy) | Backward (hard) | Permanent because |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Thermodynamic** | Mixing ($\Delta S \geq 0$) | Unmixing | 2nd law |
| **Arithmetic** | Multiplication ($ab > \max$) | Factorization | Primes are irreducible |
| **Computational** | Evaluation (P) | Inversion (NP-hard) | One-way functions |

**Part (b).** If entropy IS counting (T315), the second law of thermodynamics is the physical expression of $ab > \max(a,b)$. The arrow is not contingent on initial conditions — it is forced by the structure of the natural numbers. Any universe with multiplication has an arrow of time.

**Part (c).** If P $\neq$ NP, one-way functions exist: functions easy to compute but hard to invert. Multiplication is the prototypical example ($n = pq$ is easy; factoring $n$ is hard). The computational arrow is the algorithmic expression of the same asymmetry.

The three arrows are three readings of one fact: building is polynomial, decomposition is superpolynomial. The integers have a direction.

### The BST primorial fingerprint

At the BST primorial $7\# = 210$:

- Forward: $210 + 1 = 211$ is prime. The observer steps forward into uncharted territory.
- Backward: $210 - 1 = 209 = 11 \times 19$. Composite — backward decomposes into perturbative primes.

Compare the smaller primorials:

| Primorial | $p\#$ | $p\# + 1$ | $p\# - 1$ | Symmetry |
|-----------|-------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| $3\#$ | 6 | 7 (prime) | 5 (prime) | Symmetric |
| $5\#$ | 30 | 31 (prime) | 29 (prime) | Symmetric |
| $7\#$ | 210 | 211 (prime) | 209 = 11 $\times$ 19 | **Broken** |

The $\pm 1$ symmetry holds at $3\#$ and $5\#$ but breaks at $7\#$. The geometry breaks the symmetry exactly when the five-integer alphabet completes. The arrow is declared at BST completion, not imposed universally on primorials.

### The counting function encodes the arrow

The Epoch Crossing Theorem (T1018) exhibits the arrow *within* the smooth-number counting function:

- $\Psi(572, 7) = 109 = 108 + 1$: the 7-smooth count reaches $f_c$ by stepping **forward** from smooth ground.
- $\Psi(1001, 11) = 191 = 192 - 1$: the 11-smooth count reaches $f_c$ by stepping **backward** from smooth ground.

Forward at the completion epoch, backward at the perturbative epoch. The genesis operator's two directions.

### Computational evidence

Multiplicative cellular automata on $\mathbb{Z}/137\mathbb{Z}$ (modular multiplication) show entropy increase of $8.6\times$ over 100 steps. Additive automata (modular addition) show only $2.3\times$. The factorization-to-multiplication computational ratio grows from $17\times$ at scale $10$ to $121{,}013\times$ at scale $100{,}000$ — a measurable, reproducible, strictly increasing asymmetry (Toy 1040).

### Honest caveat

The identification of the thermodynamic, arithmetic, and computational arrows requires Casey's Principle (T315) to be fundamental, not merely analogical. The strongest challenge: Shor's algorithm breaks the classical computational arrow for factoring without breaking the thermodynamic arrow. T1017 addresses this by locating the arrow at the level of the multiplicative *order* (which quantum mechanics preserves), not computational *efficiency* (which quantum mechanics can improve). The arrow is about structure, not speed.

## §8. Connection to the Riemann Hypothesis

### Smooth numbers and the zeta function

The smooth-number counting function $\Psi(x, B)$ relates to the Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s)$ through the restricted Euler product:

$$\zeta_B(s) = \prod_{p \leq B} \frac{1}{1 - p^{-s}}$$

and the Perron integral representation:

$$\Psi(x, B) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint \zeta_B(s) \frac{x^s}{s} \, ds$$

The Riemann Hypothesis constrains the error term in the smooth-number count: $\Psi(x, B) = x \rho(u) + O(x^{1/2 + \varepsilon})$ where $u = \ln x / \ln B$ and $\rho$ is the Dickman function. The critical exponent $1/2$ controls the error: if RH fails, the error term grows, and the smooth counting function fluctuates more wildly.

### The BST constraint

The Smooth Limit Theorem (T1016) states that $\Psi(1001, 11)/1000 = 191/1000 = f_c$ to 0.007%. This is a pre-asymptotic identity: at $x = 1001$, we are far below the regime where the Dickman approximation is accurate ($u = \ln 1001 / \ln 11 \approx 6.6$, where $\rho(6.6)$ requires careful computation).

The precision of the match constrains the behavior of $\zeta(s)$ near $s = 1$. If the Riemann Hypothesis fails — if there exist nontrivial zeros off the critical line — the error term in $\Psi(x, B)$ would carry larger fluctuations, making an identity at this precision improbable.

More precisely: the Hildebrand-Tenenbaum estimate [4] gives

$$\Psi(x, y) = x \rho(u) \left(1 + O\left(\frac{\log(u+1)}{\log y}\right)\right)$$

uniformly for $y \geq 2$, $1 \leq u \leq y^{1-\varepsilon}$. The relative error at $x = 1001$, $y = 11$ is $O(\log 7.6 / \log 11) \approx O(0.85)$, meaning the Dickman approximation alone cannot guarantee 0.007% precision. The match is *better* than the generic estimate predicts, consistent with the smooth-number distribution being *more regular* than the worst case — which is exactly what RH implies.

### The BST reading

In the BST framework (T1013): primes are the non-contact knowledge locations — the places where the algebraic lattice cannot reach, and observation is required. The Riemann Hypothesis is the statement that these locations are distributed as smoothly as possible: the universe discovers observables at the *optimal* rate.

The critical exponent $1/2$ equals $\text{rank}/2^{\text{rank}} = 2/4$. The geometry's rank controls the exploration efficiency.

### Honest caveat

This connection is suggestive, not proved. A rigorous derivation would require showing that the Selberg trace formula on $D_{IV}^5$ forces the smooth-number crossing at BST scales — an open problem (E1 in the BST research program). We do not claim to have proved RH from the Smooth Limit Theorem. We claim that the two results are structurally linked through the Perron integral, and that the precision of T1016 is more naturally explained if RH holds than if it fails.
