Casey: is the neutrino bound to the neutron, and what's the difference between free and bound neutrinos? Answering math-first, with the standard-physics ground truth and the BST-specific content separated.

## 1. Not bound to the neutron — it's the emitted courier

The neutron is `udd` (three quarks); it contains no neutrino. In beta decay `n → p + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ` (quark-level `d → u + W⁻`, `W⁻ → e⁻ + ν̄ₑ`), the antineutrino is **created at the decay vertex**, not released from storage. So the neutrino is "tied to" the neutron only at the **instant of the T₃ transition vertex** — where the neutron, W, electron, and neutrino all meet — and then it flies free. Standard physics and the BST frame agree: **production, not binding.**

## 2. A neutrino can't be persistently bound at all — and the gap says why

A bound state requires a **normalizable, positive-norm** wavefunction (a state with a binding energy). The neutrino lives in the **non-unitary gap** (ν₁ at ν = 1/2): sub-unitary, no positive-definite norm, non-normalizable. **Binding is commitment, and a gap-state can't commit → it can't bind.** Neutrinos are *structurally* free-streaming.

This is the **reason** for "neutrinos barely interact," not a paraphrase of it: the only way a gap-state can touch the committed bulk is through the narrow **T₃ doorway** (the weak channel) — it can knock, never stick.

Two observable consequences:

- **Oscillation is the signature of the uncommitted gap-state.** Committed seats — electron, muon, tau, the quarks — have definite masses and **do not oscillate** (an electron stays an electron). Neutrinos oscillate *because* they never commit; if one could bind/commit it would acquire a definite mass and stop oscillating. So oscillation itself is evidence neutrinos are perpetually free.
- **Falsifier:** BST predicts **no persistent bound-neutrino state, ever** — no neutrino condensate, no neutrino-nucleus bound state. Find one and the gap-state picture is wrong.

*Honest refinement:* ν₁ is *exactly* in the gap (massless, can't commit at all); ν₂, ν₃ carry meV masses — *just over* the gap edge, the most infinitesimal commitments. All three are far too uncommitted to bind, and the whole flavor sector oscillates — the threshold-dwelling signature.

## 3. "Bound vs free" is a property of the NEUTRON, not the neutrino

The clean separation:

| object | bound vs free | consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **neutron** (committed bulk) | free → above T₃ threshold (m_n > m_p + m_e) | **fires** → emits the courier (~880 s) |
| **neutron** in a stable nucleus | bound → held *below* threshold by nuclear binding | **can't fire** → no decay, no courier |
| **neutrino** (gap-state) | always free (can't commit) | free-streams, oscillates |

So free-vs-bound decides whether the *launcher* fires; the *courier* is always free.

## 4. This closes Casey's energy round-trip (F196)

Last turn's picture — the vacuum gives energy to the neutrino, which transports it back inside — is exactly the **two T₃ doorways**:

- **Beta decay** `n → p + e⁻ + ν̄`: committed bulk matter **emits** the courier — energy out toward the threshold/vacuum.
- **Inverse beta decay** `ν̄ + p → n + e⁺`: a free neutrino, on one of its rare knocks, goes through the T₃ door and **deposits its energy back into the bulk** (`p → n`) — "transports energy back inside," literally.

The courier is free in transit and binds at **neither end** — it only passes transiently through the T₃ vertices: emit (β) → free-stream → absorb (inverse-β). Same doorway both directions, the **same T₃** that forces the CKM mixing. The cycle closes without the neutrino ever being bound.
