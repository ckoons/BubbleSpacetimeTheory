## The setup
Read the Higgs (emergent part, F85 hybrid) as a condensate — a color-singlet, SU(2)_L-doublet fermion bilinear ⟨ψ̄_L ψ_R⟩ forming on the Shilov boundary (the natural UV cutoff Λ = the substrate/boundary scale). This is BST's version of top condensation (Nambu; Bardeen–Hill–Lindner). The condensate that forms is the self-consistent fixed point of a gap equation; whichever channel condenses is, by construction, maximally coupled (y = 1) and *is* the top. The question OP-4 poses: does the substrate gap equation select a **unique** channel, and is it the **up-type gen-3**?

The selection factorizes into three independent choices — lepton vs quark, which generation, up vs down. The gap-equation view resolves two of the three and reframes the third.

## ✅ DERIVED — the condensate is a QUARK (not a lepton). [N_c color factor]
The gap equation's fermion loop carries a **color trace = N_c**. So quark channels reach criticality N_c = 3 times more easily than leptonic ones — standard NJL, with N_c a BST primary. **This is why the heaviest fermion is a quark (the top), ~100× the heaviest lepton (m_t/m_τ ≈ 97).** Non-circular, target-innocent (N_c from geometry, the color factor from standard NJL). Tier: **derived-framework.** This is the solid new content.

## ◐ SUPPORTED — it is GEN-3 (not gen-1/2). [boundary coupling]
The condensate lives on the Shilov boundary; the gen-3 modes sit at the outermost Korányi–Wolf stratum (the Shilov stratum), so they have the strongest coupling to the boundary condensate in the gap equation. This is the *dynamical, non-circular* version of the earlier circular "gen-3 is heaviest ⟺ at the boundary" — here it is "boundary modes couple strongest, so the gap equation selects the boundary stratum." Tier: **supported** (the coupling-strength claim needs the explicit mode overlaps).

## ★ THE ADVANCE — up vs down: Lyra's "factor-2 vs 41×" objection is DISSOLVED by exponential gap dynamics
Lyra's K763 objection: the up/down charge asymmetry is only a factor ~2 (|Y_{u_R}|/|Y_{d_R}| = (4/3)/(2/3) = 2 exactly), but y_t/y_b = 41× — a linear coupling→mass rule cannot bridge 2 → 41. **She is right that it cannot — *linearly*.** But condensation is a gap equation, and the gap is **exponential** in the coupling: Σ ~ Λ·exp(−1/G) (BCS/NJL). Near criticality the gap is exponentially sensitive, so a modest coupling asymmetry produces a large mass ratio. I checked the arithmetic: a **2× coupling asymmetry with an O(0.1) coupling (G_down ≈ 0.135) yields exactly the 41× mass ratio.** If the critical coupling sits between the two (G_up > G_c > G_down), the up-type **condenses** (y_t = 1, forms the Higgs) and the down-type does **not** — the bottom's mass is then a small feed-down (its Yukawa to the top-generated Higgs), which is *why* the gap is 41× and not 2×: it is condensed-vs-spectator, not a linear coupling ratio.

**So the factor-2 IS enough — because the gap equation is exponentially nonlinear.** This is the substantive payoff of Casey's "look at the gap equation": *precipitation = condensation = exponential sensitivity = large hierarchy from a small asymmetry.* The up/down driver is the hypercharge asymmetry of the two condensates (up-type ⟨Q̄_L u_R⟩ carries Y = +1 = Higgs; down-type Y = −1 = anti-Higgs), amplified exponentially.

**Discipline — what this is NOT.** It does **not derive 41 exactly** (that needs G_down, i.e. the substrate coupling, which we do not have). It makes the large hierarchy a **natural consequence** of a factor-2 charge asymmetry under exponential gap dynamics — upgrading up/down from "unexplained shortfall" to **supported mechanism**, not a derived number. And it does not by itself pin y_t = *exactly* 1 (pure top-condensation notoriously wants m_t ≈ 220 GeV and a heavy Higgs; BST's **hybrid** Higgs, F85, is the escape — the fundamental part sets the Higgs mass/structure, the emergent part carries the top alignment — but that is a claim to check, not assume). Tier: **supported mechanism / lead**, explicitly not derived.
