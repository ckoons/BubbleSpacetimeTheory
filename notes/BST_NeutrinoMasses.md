## 2. The BST Seesaw

### 2.1 The Seesaw Scale

The fundamental neutrino mass scale in BST is the **boundary seesaw**:

$$\frac{m_e^2}{m_p} = \frac{m_e}{6\pi^5} = 278.3 \text{ eV}$$

This is the BST analog of the standard seesaw m_D²/M_R:
- **m_e** ↔ m_D: the boundary (Dirac) mass — the electron is the lightest boundary excitation on Š = S⁴ × S¹
- **m_p** ↔ M_R: the bulk (Majorana) mass — the proton is the lightest bulk excitation in π₆

The seesaw ratio m_e/m_p = 1/(6π⁵) is geometrically determined by the Casimir eigenvalue C₂ = 6 and the Bergman volume factor π⁵.

### 2.2 The Electroweak Suppression

The neutrino has no S¹ winding (no electric charge). Its mass comes entirely from the weak sector — the Hopf fibration S³→S² on the Shilov boundary. This introduces an additional suppression of **α²**:

$$\alpha^2 = \frac{1}{137^2} = 5.325 \times 10^{-5}$$

**Physical interpretation:** The neutrino mass is generated by a process involving **two electroweak vertices** on the contact graph. Each vertex contributes one factor of α (the Bergman contact weight). Two vertices are required because:

1. **Vertex 1** (α): The neutrino couples to the Hopf fiber (weak interaction vertex)
2. **Vertex 2** (α): The Hopf fiber couples back to the boundary mass sector (mass generation vertex)

This is the BST analog of the standard 1-loop radiative neutrino mass mechanism.

### 2.3 The Combined Seesaw Scale

$$\alpha^2 \times \frac{m_e^2}{m_p} = \frac{m_e \alpha^2}{6\pi^5} = 0.01482 \text{ eV}$$

This is the fundamental neutrino mass unit in BST. Each neutrino mass is an integer-rational multiple of this scale.

## 3. The Geometric Factors

### 3.1 The Three Factors

Each neutrino mass eigenstate is characterized by a geometric factor f_i that encodes its coupling to the D_IV^5 geometry:

$$f_3 = \frac{2n_C}{N_c} = \frac{10}{3} = 3.333\ldots$$

$$f_2 = \frac{n_C + 2}{4N_c} = \frac{7}{12} = 0.5833\ldots$$

$$f_1 = 0$$

### 3.2 Interpretation of f₃ = 2n_C/N_c = 10/3

The heaviest neutrino (ν₃) has the largest geometric factor:

- **2n_C = 10**: the real dimension of D_IV^5 — the full non-color phase space available for weak interactions
- **N_c = 3**: the number of colors — the neutrino doesn't participate in color interactions, so the color directions dilute the coupling

The ratio dim_ℝ(D_IV^5)/N_c = 10/3 measures the **non-color phase space per color direction**: how much of the domain geometry the neutrino "sees" relative to the color sector it doesn't interact with.

### 3.3 Interpretation of f₂ = (n_C+2)/(4N_c) = 7/12

The middle neutrino (ν₂) has the factor:

- **n_C + 2 = 7**: the genus of D_IV^5 — the same topological invariant that appears in α_s = 7/20, c = 7/(10π), and β₀ = 7
- **4N_c = 12**: four Bergman contacts times the number of colors

Note the connection to the strong coupling: α_s = (n_C+2)/(4n_C) = 7/20. The ν₂ factor is:

$$f_2 = \frac{n_C + 2}{4N_c} = \alpha_s \times \frac{n_C}{N_c} = \frac{7}{20} \times \frac{5}{3} = \frac{7}{12}$$

The ν₂ mass is the strong coupling α_s rescaled by the dimension-to-color ratio n_C/N_c.

### 3.4 Interpretation of f₁ = 0

The lightest neutrino (ν₁) is **exactly massless**. This is a strong BST prediction.

**Why m₁ = 0:** In BST, the three neutrino flavors correspond to three geometric modes on the Shilov boundary. The lightest mode has a topological protection: it is a pure Goldstone mode of the broken Z₃ symmetry at the boundary. Its mass is forbidden by the residual Z₃ symmetry that protects the baryon number.

### 3.5 The Mass Ratio

$$\frac{m_3}{m_2} = \frac{f_3}{f_2} = \frac{2n_C/N_c}{(n_C+2)/(4N_c)} = \frac{8n_C}{n_C + 2} = \frac{40}{7} = 5.714$$

This is a pure geometric ratio — it depends only on n_C = 5.

**Observed:** m₃/m₂ ≈ √(Δm²₃₁/Δm²₂₁) = √(2.528/0.0753) ≈ 5.79. Deviation: −1.4%.

## 5. Falsifiable Predictions

### 5.1 m₁ = 0 Exactly

BST predicts the lightest neutrino is massless. This means:

- **Normal ordering only.** Inverted ordering (m₃ lightest, with m₃ ≈ 0.05 eV) is excluded.
- **Σm_ν = 0.058 eV.** Any cosmological measurement giving Σm_ν < 0.05 eV or Σm_ν > 0.07 eV would challenge BST.
- **Neutrinoless double-beta decay:** m_ββ = |U²_e2 m₂ + U²_e3 m₃| ≈ 2-4 meV (normal ordering, m₁=0). This is below current experimental sensitivity but within reach of next-generation experiments (nEXO, LEGEND-1000).

### 5.2 The Mass Ratio 40/7

$$\frac{m_3}{m_2} = \frac{40}{7} = 5.714\ldots$$

This is a clean rational number. Future precision measurements of Δm²₃₂ and Δm²₂₁ will test whether the ratio converges to 40/7 or to some other value.

### 5.3 Absolute Mass Scale

The absolute mass m₃ = 0.0494 eV is predicted, not just the splittings. Direct measurements from KATRIN (tritium beta decay endpoint) currently give m_ν < 0.45 eV (90% CL). Future experiments (Project 8) aim for sensitivity ~0.04 eV, which would directly test this prediction.

The neutrino mass formula m_ν = f × α² × m_e²/m_p and the baryon asymmetry η = 2α⁴/(3π) both involve powers of α times BST geometric factors. Their ratio:

$$\frac{m_{\nu_3}}{m_e \times \eta} = \frac{(10/3) \times \alpha^2 \times m_e/m_p}{2\alpha^4/(3\pi)} = \frac{(10/3) \times 3\pi}{2\alpha^2 \times 6\pi^5} = \frac{10\pi}{12\pi^5 \alpha^2} = \frac{5}{6\pi^4 \alpha^2}$$

This ratio connects the neutrino mass scale to the baryon asymmetry through purely geometric quantities.

Note that η × m_W = 6.018 × 10⁻¹⁰ × 80377 MeV = 0.0484 eV ≈ m₃. This "coincidence" follows because:

$$\eta \times m_W = \frac{2\alpha^4}{3\pi} \times m_W \approx \frac{10}{3} \times \alpha^2 \times \frac{m_e^2}{m_p}$$
