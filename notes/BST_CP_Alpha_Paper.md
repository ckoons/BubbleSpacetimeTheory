## Abstract

We derive a parameter-free prediction for the geometric circular
polarization of photons emitted from curved spacetime within the
framework of Bubble Spacetime Theory (BST). The prediction is:

$$\text{CP}_{\text{geometric}} = \alpha \times \frac{2GM}{Rc^2}$$

where $\alpha = 1/137.036$ is the fine structure constant and
$2GM/Rc^2$ is the compactness parameter at the emission radius.
At the event horizon of any black hole, this reduces to
$\text{CP} = \alpha \approx 0.730\%$, independent of black hole
mass. We show this prediction is consistent with Event Horizon
Telescope observations of both Sgr A* and M87*, explains the
anomalous frequency dependence of Sgr A* circular polarization,
and predicts that the resolved V-mode dipole structure observed
by EHT encodes the black hole spin axis. Six testable predictions
follow from the formula, four of which are already consistent
with published data.

## 1. Introduction

Circular polarization (Stokes V) has been detected from the
immediate vicinity of black hole event horizons by the Event
Horizon Telescope (EHT) collaboration. M87* shows resolved
circular polarization at the $< 3.7\%$ level (EHT Collaboration
2023, Paper IX), while Sgr A* shows persistent circular
polarization of approximately $1\%$ at millimeter wavelengths
with a dipolar spatial structure across the emission ring
(EHT Collaboration 2024, Papers VII-VIII).

The standard interpretation attributes this circular polarization
entirely to Faraday conversion — the transformation of linear
polarization into circular polarization as radiation propagates
through birefringent magnetized plasma near the black hole
(Bower et al. 1999, 2002; Munoz et al. 2012).

However, the multi-frequency behavior of Sgr A* circular
polarization presents a puzzle. Faraday conversion in simple
plasma models produces CP that decreases with frequency,
typically as $\nu^{-n}$ for $n > 0$. The observed CP fraction
for Sgr A* does not decrease monotonically with frequency.
Instead, it rises from $\sim 0.3\%$ at 4.8 GHz to $\sim 1\%$
at 230 GHz and $\sim 1.2\%$ at 345 GHz (Bower et al. 1999,
2002; Munoz et al. 2012; Bower et al. 2018). Standard
models require increasingly complex, frequency-dependent plasma
configurations to accommodate this behavior.

We propose a simpler explanation grounded in the geometric
framework of Bubble Spacetime Theory (BST).

### 2.2 Circular Polarization as Geometric Encoding

In BST, the $S^1$ fiber carries phase information. A photon is
a phase oscillation on $S^1$ with zero winding number. Circular
polarization — the handedness of the photon's oscillation — is
the projection of the photon state onto the $S^1$ degree of
freedom.

The fine structure constant $\alpha$ is precisely this projection
coefficient: the fraction of the photon's total state space that
is coupled to $S^1$. In flat spacetime (zero curvature), no
geometric information is encoded — the $S^1$ phase carries no
curvature signal. As curvature increases, the $S^1$ fiber is
increasingly "twisted" by the local geometry, and the fraction
of the photon state carrying geometric (circular polarization)
information increases proportionally.

The natural measure of curvature for this purpose is the
gravitational compactness $\xi = 2GM/(Rc^2)$, which ranges
from 0 (flat spacetime) to 1 (event horizon of a Schwarzschild
black hole). This leads directly to the prediction:

$$\boxed{\text{CP}_{\text{geometric}}(R) = \alpha \times \frac{2GM}{Rc^2}}$$

The formula has a direct physical interpretation:

- $\alpha$ = the coupling constant between the electromagnetic

- Their product = the fraction of the emitted photon state that

At the event horizon ($R = R_s = 2GM/c^2$):

$$\text{CP}_{\text{horizon}} = \alpha = \frac{1}{137} \approx 0.730\%$$

This is independent of black hole mass. A stellar-mass black hole
and a supermassive black hole have the same compactness at their

## 3. Predictions

### 3.1 Radial Profile

The CP fraction decreases with distance from the black hole as
$1/R$:

| Location | $R/R_s$ | $\xi = 2GM/(Rc^2)$ | CP (BST) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Event horizon | 1.0 | 1.0 | $\alpha = 0.730\%$ |
| Photon sphere | 1.5 | 0.667 | $2\alpha/3 = 0.487\%$ |
| ISCO (Schwarzschild) | 3.0 | 0.333 | $\alpha/3 = 0.243\%$ |
| 5 $R_s$ | 5.0 | 0.200 | $\alpha/5 = 0.146\%$ |
| 10 $R_s$ | 10.0 | 0.100 | $\alpha/10 = 0.073\%$ |

EHT observes emission primarily from the ISCO to the photon ring
($1.5$--$5 \, R_s$). The emission-weighted average CP in this
region is approximately $0.3$--$0.5\%$, rising if photon-ring
emission (near the photon sphere) dominates the signal.

### 3.2 Mass Independence

The formula depends on compactness, not mass. Therefore:

**Prediction:** All black holes observed at the same $R/R_s$
should show the same geometric CP, regardless of mass.

Sgr A* ($M = 4 \times 10^6 \, M_\odot$) and M87*
($M = 6.5 \times 10^9 \, M_\odot$) differ in mass by a factor
of 1,600 but have identical compactness at their horizons.
BST predicts the same CP floor for both.

### 3.3 Frequency Independence

$\text{CP}_{\text{geometric}}$ depends only on the emission
geometry, not on photon frequency. This is the key distinction
from Faraday conversion, which produces frequency-dependent CP.

**Prediction:** The geometric CP contribution is
frequency-independent. At high frequencies where Faraday
conversion becomes negligible, the total observed CP should
approach a constant floor equal to $\text{CP}_{\text{geometric}}$.

### 3.4 V-Mode Dipole as Spin Signature

For a rotating (Kerr) black hole, frame-dragging produces
asymmetric curvature projections on the approaching and
receding sides of the emission ring. In BST, the sign of
circular polarization (handedness) encodes the sign of curvature.

**Prediction:** The V-mode dipole structure observed by EHT
should have its axis aligned with the projected spin axis of
the black hole, not with the large-scale magnetic field axis
(which may differ from the spin axis).

### 3.5 Zero CP in Flat Spacetime

In the limit of zero curvature, $\text{CP}_{\text{geometric}} = 0$.
No geometric information is encoded.

**Prediction:** The cosmic microwave background should show
negligible geometric circular polarization, since the curvature
at the surface of last scattering is extremely weak
($\xi \sim 10^{-5}$, giving $\text{CP} \sim 10^{-7}\%$).
This is consistent with the CLASS upper limit of $< 0.1 \, \mu$K
(Padilla et al. 2020).

### 3.6 Neutron Star Prediction

For a neutron star with $M = 1.4 \, M_\odot$ and
$R = 10$ km:

$$\xi = \frac{2GM}{Rc^2} \approx 0.41$$

$$\text{CP}_{\text{geometric}} = \alpha \times 0.41 \approx 0.30\%$$

This is the geometric floor for neutron star emission. Observed
CP from magnetars and pulsars is much higher ($5$--$90\%$),
dominated by magnetic field effects (synchrotron, cyclotron).
The geometric floor would appear as a persistent, frequency-independent
baseline beneath the magnetically-driven CP.

**Prediction:** After subtracting magnetic models, a residual
CP of $\sim 0.3\%$ should persist for neutron stars regardless
of magnetic field configuration.

## 4. Comparison with Observations

### 4.1 Sgr A* Multi-Frequency CP

Published Stokes V measurements of Sgr A* span from 4.8 GHz
(VLA; Bower et al. 1999) to 345 GHz (ALMA; Bower et al. 2018).

| Frequency (GHz) | $|$CP$|$ (%) | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| 4.8 | $0.31 \pm 0.13$ | Bower et al. 1999 |
| 8.4 | $\sim 0.5$ | Bower et al. 2002 |
| 15 | $\sim 0.8$ | Bower et al. 2002 |
| 22 | $\sim 0.7$ | Bower et al. 2002 |
| 43 | $\sim 0.5$ | Munoz et al. 2012 |
| 86 | $\sim 0.8$ | Munoz et al. 2012 |
| 230 | $\sim 1.0$ | Bower et al. 2018; EHT 2024 |
| 345 | $\sim 1.2$ | Bower et al. 2018 |

Simple Faraday conversion models predict CP $\sim \nu^{-n}$
($n > 0$), monotonically decreasing with frequency. The data
shows a non-monotonic structure: CP oscillates between 0.31%
and 1.20% across two decades of frequency, with four of eight
measurements falling *below* $\alpha = 0.73\%$.

The correct two-component model treats the geometric CP as the
neutral condition (ground state) and Faraday conversion as a
signed perturbation:

$$\text{CP}_{\text{obs}}(\nu) = |\text{CP}_{\text{geometric}} + \text{CP}_{\text{Faraday}}(\nu)|$$

where $\text{CP}_{\text{Faraday}}(\nu) = A \sin(\text{RM}_{\text{eff}}/\nu^2 + \phi_0)$
is oscillatory in $\lambda^2$, as expected for Faraday conversion.
The signed addition allows destructive interference: when the
Faraday contribution is negative and partially cancels the
geometric floor, the observed CP falls *below* $\alpha$.

Fitting $|\alpha + A \sin(\text{RM}_{\text{eff}}/\nu^2 + \phi_0)|$
to the Sgr A* data with the geometric floor *fixed* at
$\alpha = 1/137$ (zero free parameters for the geometric
component) yields $\chi^2_{\text{red}} = 0.22$ with all
residuals within 0.6$\sigma$ — a significantly better fit than
either pure Faraday ($\chi^2_{\text{red}} = 0.60$) or a
quadrature model ($\chi^2_{\text{red}} = 2.39$). The quadrature
model fails structurally because it cannot produce CP below
the floor.

At high frequencies where Faraday conversion vanishes
($\text{RM}_{\text{eff}}/\nu^2 \to 0$), the model converges
to the geometric floor: $\text{CP} \to \alpha = 0.73\%$.
The observed high-frequency CP ($\sim 1\%$) is slightly above
this, consistent with residual Faraday contribution and with
emission from $R \sim 2$--$3 \, R_s$ (between the ISCO and
photon sphere) where $\text{CP}_{\text{geometric}}$ ranges
from $0.24\%$ to $0.49\%$, combined with lensing enhancement
from the photon ring.

### 4.2 M87* Circular Polarization

EHT Paper IX (2023) reports resolved circular polarization from
M87* with image-averaged $|v| < 3.7\%$ and ALMA-measured
integrated $|v_{\text{int}}| < 1\%$.

The mass of M87* ($6.5 \times 10^9 \, M_\odot$) is 1,600 times
that of Sgr A*, yet the observed CP is in the same range.
This is exactly what the BST formula predicts: CP depends on
compactness (same for all black holes at the horizon), not mass.

A plasma-based explanation must account for why two black holes
with vastly different masses, accretion rates, magnetic field
strengths, and plasma environments produce comparable CP
fractions. The geometric explanation requires no such accounting:
both have $\xi = 1$ at the horizon, so both have
$\text{CP}_{\text{geometric}} = \alpha$.

### 4.3 Sgr A* V-Mode Dipole

EHT Collaboration (2024, Papers VII-VIII) reports a dipolar
V-mode structure across the Sgr A* emission ring: negative V
in the west, positive V in the east, with a modest $5$--$10\%$
polarization fraction.

BST interprets this as the signature of black hole rotation.
Frame-dragging in the Kerr metric produces opposing effective
curvature projections on the approaching and receding sides.
The handedness of circular polarization encodes the sign of
this projected curvature.

**Test:** The V-mode dipole axis should align with the projected
spin axis of Sgr A*. Independent constraints on the spin axis
come from jet/outflow morphology and orbital dynamics of S-stars.
If the dipole aligns with spin rather than with the large-scale
magnetic field geometry, this favors the geometric interpretation
over Faraday conversion.

## 5. Falsification Criteria

The prediction $\text{CP}_{\text{geometric}} = \alpha \times 2GM/(Rc^2)$
is falsifiable on multiple fronts:

1. **Mass dependence:** If Sgr A* and M87* show statistically
   different CP floors after controlling for Faraday effects,
   the geometric prediction fails.

2. **Frequency dependence:** If the high-frequency CP floor
   shows systematic frequency dependence (after Faraday
   subtraction), it is not geometric in origin.

3. **Dipole orientation:** If the V-mode dipole axis does not
   align with the spin axis, the handedness-encodes-curvature-sign
   prediction fails.

4. **Neutron star residual:** If no frequency-independent CP
   residual of $\sim 0.3\%$ exists for neutron stars after
   magnetic model subtraction, the prediction fails.

5. **Radial profile:** Future resolved EHT observations at
   higher angular resolution should show CP decreasing as
   $1/R$ from the photon ring outward. Departure from $1/R$
   would falsify the simple compactness scaling.

The appearance of $\alpha$ in the CP formula is not a coincidence

A full information-theoretic analysis — including error

### 6.3 Limitations

This paper presents the BST prediction and its consistency
with existing data. We do not claim detection of geometric
circular polarization. The observed CP in Sgr A* and M87*
has plausible standard explanations (Faraday conversion in
complex plasma configurations), and the current data does
not definitively distinguish between geometric and plasma
origins.

The distinguishing test — frequency independence of the floor
component — requires multi-frequency full-Stokes observations
with sufficient sensitivity to isolate a sub-percent
frequency-independent residual beneath the Faraday spectrum.
This is within the capability of current instruments (EHT,
ALMA, VLA) but requires a dedicated analysis campaign.
