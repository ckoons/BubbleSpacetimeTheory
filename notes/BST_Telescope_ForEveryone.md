## 1. Light Can Spin

You probably know that light is a wave. But here's something you might
not know: light can *spin*.

Imagine throwing a football. A perfect spiral spins clockwise or
counterclockwise as it flies through the air. Light does the same
thing. A beam of light can corkscrew to the left or corkscrew to
the right as it travels toward you.

Scientists call this **circular polarization**. "Circular" because
the wave traces out a circle. "Polarization" because it tells you
which direction the wave is vibrating.

Most light you see — from the Sun, from a lightbulb, from your
phone — doesn't spin much. The waves point in random directions,
so the spins cancel out. But light from certain places in the
universe *does* spin. And how much it spins, and why, turns out
to be one of the most interesting questions in physics.

---

## 2. What Makes Light Spin?

There's one well-known reason light spins: **magnetic fields**.

When light passes through a hot, magnetized gas (called a plasma),
the magnetic field can twist the light. This is called **Faraday
conversion**, named after Michael Faraday, who discovered in the
1840s that magnets affect light.

Here's the important thing about Faraday twisting:

> **It depends on the pitch of the light.**

Light comes in different pitches (frequencies), just like sound.
Low pitch = low frequency (like bass notes). High pitch = high
frequency (like a whistle). Faraday twisting is strongest at low
frequencies and gets weaker at high frequencies.

Think of it like fog and sound. Low bass notes get scattered and
muffled by fog. High-pitched whistles cut right through. In the
same way, low-frequency light gets twisted a lot by magnetic
fields, and high-frequency light barely notices them.

So if you look at light from near a black hole and measure how much
it's spinning at different frequencies, you'd expect the spinning
to *decrease* as you go to higher frequencies. The magnetic twist
fades away.

That's what everyone expected.

That's not what they found.

## 3. The Black Hole at the Center of Our Galaxy

At the very center of our Milky Way galaxy, about 26,000 light-years
away, there's a supermassive black hole called **Sagittarius A***
(pronounced "Sagittarius A-star," or just "Sgr A*" for short).

It weighs about **4 million times as much as our Sun**, all
packed into a region smaller than Mercury's orbit. Space around it
is severely curved — warped by all that mass into a gravitational
funnel so deep that not even light can escape from inside the
boundary (called the event horizon).

We can't see the black hole itself, but we *can* see the hot gas
swirling around it, glowing in radio waves and microwaves. The
Event Horizon Telescope (EHT) — a network of radio dishes spanning
the entire Earth — took the first pictures of this glowing ring
in 2022.

And when scientists measured the spin of the light coming from
Sgr A* at different frequencies, they found something strange.

## 4. The Mystery: It Goes the Wrong Way

Here's what the measurements showed:

| Frequency | How much the light spins |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Low (1.4 GHz — like FM radio) | A little (0.15%) |
| Medium (15 GHz — like satellite TV) | More (0.80%) |
| High (230 GHz — microwaves) | Even more (1.00%) |
| Very high (345 GHz) | The most (1.20%) |

**The light spins MORE at higher frequencies.**

But Faraday twisting says it should spin *less*. The magnetic
effect fades at high frequency, like fog thinning out. The spin
should decrease, not increase.

Something else is happening.

## 5. The Floor

Imagine you're in a room with two kinds of sound. One is music
playing on a speaker — it's loud when you're close, quiet when
you're far away. The other is the hum of the building itself —
the air conditioner, the pipes in the walls, the electricity
in the wires. That hum is always there, everywhere in the
building, at the same level.

If you walk away from the speaker, the music fades. But the hum
doesn't. Eventually, all you hear is the hum. It's the **floor**
— the minimum amount of sound in the room.

We think the same thing is happening with light near black holes.

The magnetic twisting (Faraday) is like the music — it's loud at
low frequencies and fades at high frequencies. But there's also a
**geometric twist** — a spin that comes not from magnetic fields
but from the shape of space itself. And this geometric twist
doesn't care about frequency. It's always there, at the same
level, like the building's hum.

At low frequencies, the magnetic twist is strong and you can't
see the floor. At high frequencies, the magnetic twist fades
away, and the floor is revealed.

**That's why the spin increases at high frequency.** You're not
seeing *more* twisting. You're seeing *less* interference. The
magnetic fog clears, and the geometric floor stands alone.

## 7. The Formula

For those who like equations, the prediction fits in one line:

> **CP = α × (2GM / Rc²)**

- **CP** = how much the light spins (circular polarization), as a fraction
- **α = 1/137** = the fine structure constant (the universe's "coupling strength" between light and the geometry of space)
- **G** = Newton's gravitational constant (how strong gravity is)

- **R** = how far away from the center you are
- **c** = the speed of light

The quantity **2GM/Rc²** is called the **compactness** — it
measures how warped space is at distance R from a mass M. At the

> CP = α × 1 = α = 1/137 = 0.730%

## 9. Where Faraday Fails and Geometry Wins

Let's put this all together. Here's what we think is happening:

At **low frequencies**, the magnetic field twists the light a
lot. But the twist is in the *opposite direction* from the
geometric floor — it partially cancels it. So the total spin
is *less* than the floor. This is called **destructive
interference**, like two waves hitting each other and
canceling out.

At **medium frequencies**, the magnetic twist has weakened, so
the cancellation is less. The total spin is close to the floor.

At **high frequencies**, the magnetic twist has faded almost
completely. Nothing cancels the geometric floor. You see the
floor directly.

The formula for the total observed spin is:

> **CP_observed = | floor − Faraday(ν) |**

where the Faraday piece shrinks as the frequency goes up. At
very high frequencies, Faraday goes to zero and you're left
with just the floor.

When we fit this model to the Sgr A* data, it works. The
floor model gives a better fit (lower χ², the statistician's
measure of "how well does the model match the data") than
a model with only Faraday twisting and no floor.

## Glossary

**Black hole** — A region of space where gravity is so strong
that nothing, not even light, can escape. The boundary is called
the event horizon.

**Circular polarization (CP)** — The spin of a light wave.
Light can corkscrew clockwise (right-handed) or counterclockwise
(left-handed) as it travels.

**Compactness** — A measure of how warped space is near a massive
object. Calculated as 2GM/Rc². Equals 1 at a black hole's event
horizon, nearly 0 far from anything heavy.

**Event Horizon Telescope (EHT)** — A planet-sized array of radio
telescopes that work together to photograph black holes.

**Faraday conversion** — The twisting of light's polarization by
magnetic fields in hot gas. Gets weaker at higher frequencies.

**Fine structure constant (α)** — The number 1/137.036..., which
controls how strongly light interacts with matter. One of the
most fundamental numbers in physics.

**Frequency** — The pitch of a wave. High frequency = short
wavelength = high energy. Measured in GHz (gigahertz, billions
of cycles per second).

**Geometric floor** — The minimum circular polarization that comes
from the shape of space itself, not from magnetic fields. BST
predicts this is α × compactness.

**Stokes V** — The scientific measurement of circular polarization.
Named after George Gabriel Stokes, who invented the system in 1852.
V is the last of four Stokes parameters (I, Q, U, V).
