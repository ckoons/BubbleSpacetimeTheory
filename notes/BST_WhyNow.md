## 6. The Hubble Tension

### 6.1 BST's Position

BST predicts $H_0$ between 66.7 and 68.0 km/s/Mpc — firmly on the

### 6.2 Can BST Resolve the Tension?

The Hubble tension might arise from BST's time-varying Λ:

From the Reality Budget: $\Lambda \propto 1/N_{\text{total}} \propto
1/t$. This means Λ was slightly larger in the past than today. The
Friedmann equation $H^2 = \Lambda/3 + ...$ then gives a slightly
larger $H$ at earlier times.

Local $H_0$ measurements (SH0ES) use supernovae at $z \lesssim 0.1$,
which probe the universe's expansion rate averaged over the recent
past. If Λ was slightly larger recently than the CMB-inferred value,
local measurements would find a higher $H_0$.

The fractional change in Λ over one Hubble time:

$$\frac{\Delta\Lambda}{\Lambda} \sim -\frac{\Delta t}{t_0} \sim -1$$

This is a large change (of order 100%) over the Hubble time. Over the
SH0ES lookback time ($\sim 0.5$ Gyr), the change is:

$$\frac{\Delta\Lambda}{\Lambda} \sim -\frac{0.5}{13.6} \sim -4\%$$

A 4% increase in Λ gives a $\sim 2\%$ increase in $H$, which shifts
$H_0$ from 68.0 to $\sim 69.4$. This reduces the tension but doesn't
fully resolve it.

**BST reduces but does not eliminate the Hubble tension.** The residual
tension may point to physics beyond simple ΛCDM that BST's full field
equation would capture.
