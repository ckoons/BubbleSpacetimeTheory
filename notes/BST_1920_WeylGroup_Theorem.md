## Abstract

The proton-to-electron mass ratio in BST is $m_p/m_e = 6\pi^5 = 1836.118$ (0.002% precision). The intermediate factor 1920 appears in both the Hua volume formula $\text{Vol}(D_{IV}^5) = \pi^5/1920$ and the baryon orbit count on the Shilov boundary, and cancels identically. Previous work (BST_BaryonCircuit_ContactIntegral.md, BST_MissingLemma_ClebschGordan.md) proved this cancellation algebraically. This note answers the deeper question: **why does the same group appear in both places?**

The answer is that 1920 = |W(D_5)|, the order of the Weyl group of the root system $D_5$. The Weyl group $W(D_n)$ is the symmetry group of the quadratic form $z \cdot z = \sum z_j^2$ that defines $D_{IV}^n$. It appears in the volume formula as a **symmetry reduction** and in the baryon orbit as the **orbit of a free action** on boundary configurations. The cancellation is therefore not a numerical coincidence but a theorem about the $D_n$ symmetry of the domain.

We prove this as a general result for all $D_{IV}^n$, showing that the Weyl group $W(D_n)$ cancels identically in the mass ratio for any $n \geq 1$.

## 1. The Question

The BST mass gap proof rests on five pillars:

1. $H_{YM} = (7/10\pi) \cdot \Delta_B$ (Kahler-Einstein + Uhlenbeck-Yau)
2. Vacuum: $C_2 = 0$, $E = 0$ (flat connection)
3. Baryon $\in \pi_6 \subset \text{Sym}^3(\pi_6)$ (triple Bergman projection; $C_2(\pi_6) = 6$)

5. Gap $= C_2 \times \pi^{n_C} \times m_e = 6\pi^5 \times m_e$ (**the 1920 cancellation**)

Step 5 is where the factor 1920 enters and cancels. The algebraic fact is:

$$6\pi^5 = 6 \times 1920 \times \frac{\pi^5}{1920}$$

The left factor 1920 is the baryon orbit size. The right factor $\pi^5/1920$ is $\text{Vol}(D_{IV}^5)$. They cancel, leaving $6\pi^5$.

This was proved in BST_MissingLemma_ClebschGordan.md as a combinatorial identity. But the proof is unsatisfying: it does not explain **why** the same group $\Gamma = S_5 \times (\mathbb{Z}_2)^4$ appears in two seemingly unrelated contexts (analytic geometry and particle physics). This paper provides the explanation.

## 2. Identification: $\Gamma = W(D_5)$

### 2.1 The Weyl Group of $D_n$

The root system $D_n$ consists of vectors $\pm e_i \pm e_j$ for $1 \leq i < j \leq n$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Its Weyl group $W(D_n)$ is generated by:

- **$S_n$**: permutations of coordinates $(z_1, \ldots, z_n) \mapsto (z_{\sigma(1)}, \ldots, z_{\sigma(n)})$
- **$(\mathbb{Z}_2)^{n-1}$**: even numbers of sign changes $(z_1, \ldots, z_n) \mapsto (\pm z_1, \ldots, \pm z_n)$ with an even number of minus signs

The constraint to **even** sign changes distinguishes $D_n$ from $B_n$: $|W(B_n)| = n! \cdot 2^n$ while $|W(D_n)| = n! \cdot 2^{n-1}$.

For $n = 5$:

$$|W(D_5)| = 5! \times 2^4 = 120 \times 16 = 1920$$

### 2.2 The BST Group $\Gamma$

In BST, the group $\Gamma = S_5 \times (\mathbb{Z}_2)^4$ acts on $D_{IV}^5$ as:

- **$S_5$**: permutations of the 5 complex dimensions of $D_{IV}^5$
- **$(\mathbb{Z}_2)^4$**: relative $S^1$ phase signs between pairs (even parity constraint from the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ identification on the Shilov boundary $\check{S} = S^4 \times S^1$)

The even-parity constraint on sign changes arises because $D_{IV}^n$ is defined by the quadratic form $z \cdot z = \sum_{j=1}^n z_j^2$, which is invariant under $z_j \mapsto -z_j$ only in pairs (preserving $z \cdot z$ since each $z_j^2$ is individually invariant, but the boundary identification $\check{S} \cong (S^{n-1} \times S^1)/\mathbb{Z}_2$ forces even parity on the global sign choice).

### 2.3 The Identification

**Proposition 2.1.** *The BST group $\Gamma = S_5 \times (\mathbb{Z}_2)^4$ is isomorphic to $W(D_5)$, the Weyl group of the root system $D_5$.*

*Proof.* Both groups act on $\mathbb{R}^5$ (or $\mathbb{C}^5$) by coordinate permutations and even sign changes. The generators are identical:
- $S_5$ permutes the basis vectors $e_1, \ldots, e_5$
- $(\mathbb{Z}_2)^4 \cong \{(\epsilon_1, \ldots, \epsilon_5) \in \{\pm 1\}^5 : \prod \epsilon_j = +1\}$ flips even subsets of signs

The isomorphism is the identity map on the natural representation. $\square$

This is not a coincidence: $D_{IV}^n$ **is** the Hermitian symmetric space whose restricted root system is of type $D_n$ (or more precisely, $BC_n$ reduces to $D_n$ for the quadratic form symmetry). The quadratic form $z \cdot z = \sum z_j^2$ that defines the domain type IV has $W(D_n)$ as its symmetry group by construction.

## 3. Why $W(D_n)$ Appears in the Volume Formula

### 3.1 Hua's Volume Formula

**Theorem 3.1 (Hua, 1963).** *The Bergman-metric volume of $D_{IV}^n$ is:*

$$\text{Vol}(D_{IV}^n) = \frac{\pi^n}{n! \cdot 2^{n-1}} = \frac{\pi^n}{|W(D_n)|}$$

### 3.2 The Symmetry Reduction

The volume computation proceeds by integrating the Bergman kernel determinant $|K(z,z)|$ over $D_{IV}^n$. The key structure:

1. **The integrand.** The Bergman kernel is $K(z,w) = c_n \cdot N(z,w)^{-(n+2)}$ where $N(z,w) = 1 - 2z \cdot \bar{w} + (z \cdot z)(\bar{w} \cdot \bar{w})$ is the norm function. The quadratic form $z \cdot z = \sum z_j^2$ appears explicitly.

2. **The symmetry.** The norm function $N(z,w)$ is invariant under $W(D_n)$:
   - $S_n$: permuting $z_j$ preserves $z \cdot z = \sum z_j^2$ and $z \cdot \bar{w} = \sum z_j \bar{w}_j$
   - $(\mathbb{Z}_2)^{n-1}$: even sign flips preserve $z \cdot z$ (each $z_j^2$ is sign-invariant) and preserve $z \cdot \bar{w}$ (sign flips applied simultaneously to $z$ and $w$)

3. **The reduction.** The integral over $D_{IV}^n$ decomposes into an integral over a fundamental domain $\mathcal{F}$ of the $W(D_n)$-action times the group order:

$$\text{Vol}(D_{IV}^n) = \frac{1}{|W(D_n)|} \int_{D_{IV}^n} dV_B$$

The "raw" integral $\int dV_B$ evaluates to $\pi^n$ from angular integrations (this is a standard computation in tube domain coordinates). Dividing by the symmetry group:

$$\text{Vol}(D_{IV}^n) = \frac{\pi^n}{|W(D_n)|}$$

**The factor $1/|W(D_n)|$ in the volume formula is a symmetry reduction by the Weyl group of the domain's defining quadratic form.** It is not a mysterious combinatorial number -- it is the order of a symmetry group.

## 4. Why $W(D_n)$ Appears in the Baryon Orbit

### 4.1 The Baryon Configuration Space

A baryon is a $\mathbb{Z}_3$ color circuit visiting three points on the Shilov boundary $\check{S} = S^{n-1} \times S^1$. A baryon configuration consists of:

- Three quark positions $\xi_1, \xi_2, \xi_3 \in S^{n-1}$
- Three $S^1$ phase angles $\phi_1, \phi_2, \phi_3 \in S^1$
- A quark-dimension assignment $\sigma: \{1,2,3\} \to \{1, \ldots, n\}$ (injective)
- Relative $S^1$ orientation signs $(\epsilon_1, \ldots, \epsilon_{n-1}) \in (\mathbb{Z}_2)^{n-1}$

### 4.2 The $W(D_n)$ Action

The Weyl group $W(D_n) = S_n \times (\mathbb{Z}_2)^{n-1}$ acts on baryon configurations by:

- **$S_n$**: permuting which winding mode (complex dimension) each quark occupies. This is the "dimension assignment" -- which of the $n$ causal winding modes participates in the color circuit.
- **$(\mathbb{Z}_2)^{n-1}$**: flipping the relative $S^1$ phases between quarks. The even-parity constraint arises because the overall $\mathbb{Z}_3$ phase is fixed by the color-singlet condition.

### 4.3 Freeness

**Proposition 4.1 (BST_MissingLemma_ClebschGordan.md, Proposition 2.2).** *The action of $W(D_n)$ on generic baryon configurations is free: for any generic configuration $B$, $\text{Stab}_{W(D_n)}(B) = \{e\}$.*

*Proof.* A non-trivial element $g \in W(D_n)$ either permutes dimensions non-trivially (moving at least one quark to a different winding mode) or flips signs (reversing at least one relative phase). For generic configurations -- where quark positions are mutually distinct and not on any proper invariant sub-variety -- neither operation is compatible with fixing the configuration pointwise. $\square$

### 4.4 The Orbit Size

By the orbit-stabilizer theorem:

$$|\text{Orbit}(B)| = \frac{|W(D_n)|}{|\text{Stab}(B)|} = \frac{|W(D_n)|}{1} = |W(D_n)| = n! \cdot 2^{n-1}$$

**The baryon orbit has size $|W(D_n)|$ because the baryon inherits the $D_n$ symmetry of the domain.** The same quadratic form $z \cdot z$ that defines $D_{IV}^n$ also governs the baryon circuit topology on the Shilov boundary.

## 5. The Cancellation as a Theorem

We can now state the cancellation not as a numerical identity but as a structural theorem.

**Theorem 5.1 (Weyl Cancellation).** *For any type IV bounded symmetric domain $D_{IV}^n$ with $n \geq 1$, the proton-to-electron mass ratio is:*

$$\frac{m_p}{m_e} = C_2(\pi_{n+1}) \times |\text{Orbit}(B)| \times \text{Vol}(D_{IV}^n) = C_2(\pi_{n+1}) \times |W(D_n)| \times \frac{\pi^n}{|W(D_n)|} = C_2(\pi_{n+1}) \times \pi^n$$

*The Weyl group $W(D_n)$ appears in both the baryon orbit (numerator) and the volume formula (denominator), and cancels identically.*

*Proof.*
1. $\text{Vol}(D_{IV}^n) = \pi^n / |W(D_n)|$ (Hua, 1963; Theorem 3.1)
2. Baryon orbit size $= |W(D_n)|$ (Proposition 4.1 + orbit-stabilizer)
3. $m_p/m_e = C_2 \times \text{orbit} \times \text{Vol} = C_2 \times |W(D_n)| \times \pi^n/|W(D_n)| = C_2 \times \pi^n$. $\square$

**Corollary 5.2.** *The mass ratio depends ONLY on:*
- *$C_2(\pi_{n+1})$: the Casimir eigenvalue of the Bergman space (a spectral quantity)*
- *$n$: the complex dimension of the domain (a topological quantity)*

*It does NOT depend on $|W(D_n)|$. The Weyl group order is an intermediate quantity that cancels by the symmetry of the calculation.*

**Corollary 5.3.** *For $n = n_C = 5$: $C_2(\pi_6) = 6$ and $\pi^5 \approx 306.020$, giving $m_p/m_e = 6\pi^5 = 1836.118$.*

### 5.1 Interpretation

The cancellation has a clean geometric interpretation:

- The **volume formula** counts how many fundamental domains of $W(D_n)$ tile the domain. The volume of each tile is $\pi^n / |W(D_n)|$.
- The **baryon orbit** counts how many $W(D_n)$-equivalent baryon configurations exist. Each configuration contributes one unit to the boundary integral.
- The **mass ratio** is the total boundary integral: (number of configurations) $\times$ (volume per configuration) $= |W(D_n)| \times \pi^n / |W(D_n)| = \pi^n$.

The 1920 is a **gauge redundancy** of the calculation, not a physical quantity. The physical content is $C_2 \times \pi^n$.

## 8. Generalization to All $D_{IV}^n$

### 8.1 The General Theorem

**Theorem 8.1 (General Weyl Cancellation).** *For any type IV bounded symmetric domain $D_{IV}^n$ with $n \geq 1$, the three-point boundary integral satisfies:*

$$C_3(n) = C_2(\pi_{n+1}) \times \pi^n$$

*independent of $|W(D_n)|$. Equivalently:*

$$(\text{baryon orbit size}) \times \text{Vol}(D_{IV}^n) = \pi^n$$

*which is the statement that the $W(D_n)$ symmetry cancels identically between the orbit count and the volume normalization.*

*Proof.*

(i) **Volume:** $\text{Vol}(D_{IV}^n) = \pi^n / |W(D_n)|$ (Hua, 1963).

(ii) **Orbit:** The baryon orbit under $W(D_n)$ has size $|W(D_n)| = n! \cdot 2^{n-1}$ (Proposition 4.1, generalized to arbitrary $n$ by the same freeness argument: the action of $W(D_n)$ on generic configurations of $\check{S}^n = S^{n-1} \times S^1$ is free because neither a non-trivial permutation nor a non-trivial even sign flip can fix a generic triple).

(iii) **Cancellation:**
$$C_3 = C_2 \times \text{orbit} \times \text{Vol} = C_2 \times |W(D_n)| \times \frac{\pi^n}{|W(D_n)|} = C_2 \times \pi^n$$

$\square$

### 8.2 Consistency Checks

| $n$ | $|W(D_n)|$ | $\text{Vol}(D_{IV}^n)$ | $C_2(\pi_{n+1})$ | $C_3(n) = C_2 \cdot \pi^n$ |
|-----|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| 1 | 1 | $\pi$ | 2 | $2\pi$ |
| 2 | 4 | $\pi^2/4$ | 3 | $3\pi^2$ |
| 3 | 24 | $\pi^3/24$ | 4 | $4\pi^3$ |
| 4 | 192 | $\pi^4/192$ | 5 | $5\pi^4$ |
| **5** | **1920** | $\pi^5/1920$ | **6** | $6\pi^5 = 1836.118$ |
| 6 | 23040 | $\pi^6/23040$ | 7 | $7\pi^6$ |

In every case, $|W(D_n)|$ cancels. The mass ratio $C_3(n) = (n+1)\pi^n$ depends only on $n$.

The $n = 1$ case was verified exactly in BST_BaryonCircuit_ContactIntegral.md (Section 5): $D_{IV}^1$ is the unit disk, $\text{Vol} = \pi$, the baryon orbit is trivial (size 1), and $C_3(1) = 2\pi$.

### 8.3 The Pattern $C_2(\pi_{n+1}) = n + 1$

The Casimir eigenvalue $C_2(\pi_k) = k(k - n)$ for the holomorphic discrete series $\pi_k$ of $\text{SO}_0(n,2)$. The Bergman space is $\pi_{n+1}$ (weight $k = n + 1$), giving:

$$C_2(\pi_{n+1}) = (n+1)(n+1-n) = (n+1) \cdot 1 = n + 1$$

This is a general fact: the Bergman space always has $C_2 = n + 1$, independent of the Weyl group. Combined with the Weyl cancellation:

$$C_3(n) = (n+1) \cdot \pi^n$$

This is the **universal mass formula** for type IV domains.

## 9. Summary

The 1920 cancellation in BST is a theorem, not a coincidence:

1. **$1920 = |W(D_5)|$**, the order of the Weyl group of the root system $D_5$.

2. $W(D_5)$ is the symmetry group of the quadratic form $z \cdot z = \sum z_j^2$ that **defines** $D_{IV}^5$.

3. It appears in the **volume formula** as a symmetry reduction: $\text{Vol} = \pi^5 / |W(D_5)|$.

4. It appears in the **baryon orbit** as the orbit size of a free group action: $|\text{Orbit}| = |W(D_5)|$.

5. These are **two manifestations of the same symmetry**, so they cancel: $|W(D_5)| \times \pi^5/|W(D_5)| = \pi^5$.

6. The cancellation **generalizes** to all $D_{IV}^n$: the Weyl group $W(D_n)$ always cancels, leaving $C_3(n) = (n+1)\pi^n$.

7. The root system $D_5$ is also the root system of $\text{SO}(10)$, connecting the 1920 cancellation to grand unification -- not as a high-energy gauge symmetry, but as the Weyl symmetry of the domain geometry.

The physical mass ratio $m_p/m_e = 6\pi^5$ contains only two pieces of information: the Casimir eigenvalue $C_2 = n_C + 1 = 6$ (spectral) and the dimension $\pi^{n_C} = \pi^5$ (geometric). The Weyl group order 1920 is a gauge artifact of the intermediate calculation.
