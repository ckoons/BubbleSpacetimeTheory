# SP-29: Casimir Mechanism Investigation

## Casey directive

> "Common 'vacuum fluctuations' and 'van der Waals counterargument' sound like 'we really don't know' what the source of Casimir energy is. Perhaps (and likely) it's either 'commitment energy' or energy from other substrate processes."

The Casimir effect is "settled" in the measurement layer — nobody contests the measured force. The MECHANISM is openly debated: Schwinger-camp QED vacuum fluctuations vs van-der-Waals-camp non-vacuum explanations (Jaffe 2005, Milonni). BST's substrate ontology offers a third reading: commitment dynamics on D_IV⁵.

This program tests Casey's five candidate mechanisms as falsifiable predictions, then identifies which existing precision Casimir data discriminates between them and which new experiments would be most decisive.

## Five hypotheses (Casey, May 18)

### H1: Underage prevents light absorption/emission

Between the plates the "commitment circle" cannot match light frequencies or phases properly. The plates physically suppress what the substrate can commit to in the gap.

**BST framing**: connects to W-30 surface tension ontology (substrate has structure between plates), W-36 Casimir/Hawking/Schwinger unification (boundary mechanism). The substrate's commitment activity is suppressed by the boundary geometry.

**Falsifiable prediction**: spectroscopy of atoms placed between Casimir plates shows shifted or weakened absorption/emission lines vs same atoms outside plates. Magnitude of shift should scale as BST primary factor of plate-gap-to-atomic-radius ratio.

### H2: Angle of incidence of commitment circle matters

The commitment circle has angular structure; only certain angles can absorb or emit.

**BST framing**: connects to Möbius cohomology Z/2 = H¹ (CP-charge of operators, Lyra T2329). The commitment circle's orientation relative to the boundary affects which substrate transitions are allowed.

**Falsifiable prediction**: rotating plates relative to source produces angle-dependent decay or transmission anomalies. If H2 holds, signal varies with rotation; if H2 doesn't hold, signal is rotation-invariant.

### H3: Commitment circle bigger than gap

The substrate's commitment object has a characteristic physical size that may exceed the plate gap at small separations (Casey doubts but worth naming).

**Falsifiable prediction**: vary plate gap continuously through a critical scale. If commitment circle has characteristic size L_c (likely BST primary multiple of compton wavelength), expect a phase-transition signature in Casimir force at L_c.

### H4: Rate of commitment slows (THE BIG ONE)

Between plates, substrate commits less frequently. Because radioactive decay is itself a commitment event, decay rates should be measurably different inside Casimir plates vs outside.

**BST framing**: connects to W-39 Cs-137 modulation experiment design (already filed). If commitment rate slows by structural BST factor, decay rate ratio outside/inside = BST primary product.

**Falsifiable prediction**: place Cs-137 source between Casimir-style plates vs in free space. Measure decay rates. If H4 holds, ratio is structural; if H4 doesn't hold, ratio is unity within experimental error.

**Why this is the strongest single test**: it's $25-50K experimental scope, not $100M LHC; weeks of data collection; paper-publishable result either way; directly tests BST's substrate ontology against the standard QED-vacuum explanation.

### H5: Substrate can't release virtual particles

Between plates, the substrate is blocked from emitting virtual particles in the standard sense.

**BST framing**: connects to W-37 beacon model formalize. The substrate's virtual-particle release channel is suppressed by boundary geometry.

**Falsifiable prediction**: ultra-low-noise vacuum fluctuation measurements (Riek 2015-style direct vacuum sampling) inside vs outside Casimir geometry show different spectral content. If H5 holds, sampled vacuum spectrum is gap-dependent.
