## 1. The Ring

The Big Bang in BST is a phase transition: the pre-spatial state (fully saturated channel, all 137 slots occupied everywhere, no emergent geometry) nucleated into the spatial state (available channel capacity, circuit propagation, emergent 3D geometry). This transition released energy as the system fell from the high-energy saturated configuration to the low-energy spatial configuration.

The transition rang the substrate like a struck bell. The energy propagated across the Koons substrate as gravitational waves — ripples in contact density spreading outward from the nucleation point. These primordial gravitational waves carry information about the geometry of the transition: the shape of the nucleation event, the symmetry of the initial break, and the critical exponents of the phase transition on $D_{IV}^5$.

The electromagnetic echo of this ring is the cosmic microwave background — thermal radiation from the hot plasma that formed as the spatial phase cooled. The gravitational wave echo is different. Gravitational waves couple to the contact density directly, not through electromagnetic circuits. They propagate through the substrate itself. They are the substrate’s own vibration.

## 2. The Echo

The substrate is $S^2$ — a closed surface. If the phase transition nucleated at a single point, the transition wavefront propagated outward across the sphere in all directions. But $S^2$ is finite and has no boundary. The wavefront eventually reaches the antipodal point and converges — focused by the topology of the sphere into a concentrated echo.

The echo is a second ring, arriving at the antipode after one half-crossing time of the substrate. It is not a repetition of the original signal. It is the original signal focused and inverted by the spherical topology — amplified at the convergence point, carrying the signature of having traversed the full half-sphere.

The wavefront does not stop at the antipode. It passes through the convergence point, re-diverges, propagates back across $S^2$, and converges again at the original nucleation point. Then to the antipode again. Then back. The ring echoes back and forth across the closed substrate, each traversal fainter than the last as cosmic expansion dilutes the energy and the growing substrate increases the path length.

The result is a series of echoes:

|Echo        |Path                            |Relative amplitude  |Information carried                        |
|------------|--------------------------------|--------------------|-------------------------------------------|
|Primary ring|Nucleation → observer           |1 (reference)       |Phase transition energy, critical exponents|
|First echo  |Nucleation → antipode → observer|Reduced by expansion|Substrate diameter, topology               |
|Second echo |Full circuit of $S^2$           |Further reduced     |Substrate curvature, damping rate          |
|$n$-th echo |$n$ half-crossings              |Exponentially damped|Expansion history over $n$ crossings       |

The spacing between echoes gives the diameter of $S^2$ — the size of the Koons substrate at the time of the transition. The damping rate gives the expansion history — how much the substrate grew between echoes. The spectral shape of each echo gives the critical exponents of the phase transition on $D_{IV}^5$.

## 3. More Than One Ring

If the phase transition nucleated at multiple points on $S^2$ simultaneously, each nucleation produces its own ring. The gravitational wave background becomes a superposition of rings from different nucleation events.

The number of nucleation points depends on the thermodynamics of the transition:

**Single nucleation (strongly first-order transition).** One bubble of spatial phase forms and expands to fill the substrate. One primary ring. The universe began at one point on $S^2$. The gravitational wave background is a single spectral template with antipodal echoes.

**Double nucleation.** Two bubbles form at different points on $S^2$ and expand toward each other. Two primary rings with different timings and directions. The bubbles collide when their expanding wavefronts meet, producing a collision boundary — a ring-shaped topological defect on $S^2$ where the two spatial-phase regions merged. This defect may be observable as a specific pattern in the CMB or as a localized gravitational wave feature.

**Multiple nucleation (weakly first-order transition).** Many bubbles form simultaneously across $S^2$, expand, and merge. Many primary rings. The gravitational wave background is a complex superposition with a characteristic statistical structure. The boundaries where bubbles collided form a network of topological defects — domain walls, cosmic strings — distributed across the substrate. These defects are an additional source of dark matter in BST (see Working Paper Section 27.1).

**Continuous nucleation (second-order transition).** The transition occurs everywhere simultaneously with no distinct nucleation events. No discrete rings. The gravitational wave background is smooth with a spectrum determined entirely by the critical exponents. No topological defects from bubble collisions.

The distinction between these scenarios is encoded in the gravitational wave spectrum:

|Scenario             |Spectral signature               |Defect content    |
|---------------------|---------------------------------|------------------|
|Single nucleation    |Single spectral template + echoes|None              |
|Double nucleation    |Two templates, different phases  |One collision ring|
|Multiple nucleation  |Statistical superposition        |Network of defects|
|Continuous transition|Smooth, featureless spectrum     |None              |

## 4. Resonant Modes of the Substrate

The closed $S^2$ substrate has resonant modes — specific angular frequencies at which gravitational waves constructively interfere with their own echoes. These are the normal modes of the sphere, characterized by the spherical harmonic degree $l$:

$$f_l = \frac{c}{R_{S^2}} \sqrt{l(l+1)}$$

where $R_{S^2}$ is the radius of the substrate at the time of the transition. The lowest mode ($l = 1$) is the dipole — one side of the substrate compressed while the other is rarefied. The quadrupole ($l = 2$) is the first mode that produces gravitational waves in the standard sense.

If the phase transition excited specific modes preferentially — which depends on the nucleation geometry — the primordial gravitational wave spectrum would show peaks at the corresponding frequencies, redshifted by the subsequent expansion. These peaks encode the topology of $S^2$ the way the resonant frequencies of a drum encode its shape.

**BST predicts spectral features; inflation predicts a featureless spectrum.** Standard slow-roll inflation produces a nearly scale-invariant primordial gravitational wave spectrum — no preferred frequencies, no peaks, no features. BST’s phase transition on a closed substrate produces a spectrum with features determined by the substrate resonant modes and the nucleation geometry.

This is a clean observational discriminant between BST and inflation.

## 5. The Physical Temperature Scale and the NANOGrav Prediction

### 5.1 $T_c$ from BST Bubble Geometry

The phase transition temperature is set by the scale of a single channel bubble. In BST, the fundamental bubble has radius $R_s = 137\,\lambda_e$ (Working Paper Section 10.2), where $\lambda_e = \hbar/(m_e c)$ is the electron Compton wavelength. The critical temperature at which the substrate transitions from the fully-saturated pre-spatial phase to the spatial phase is:

$$T_c = \frac{130.5\,\hbar c}{k_B R_s}$$

The prefactor 130.5 comes from the critical heat capacity at the transition (Working Paper Section 15.1). Substituting $R_s = 137\,\lambda_e$:

| Quantity | Value |
|---|---|
| $R_s = 137\,\lambda_e$ | $3.743 \times 10^{-12}$ m |
| $T_c$ | $0.487$ MeV $= 5.65 \times 10^9$ K |
| Cosmic time at $T_c$ | $3.1$ seconds after the Big Bang |
| Epoch | Start of Big Bang Nucleosynthesis |

**The BST phase transition — the moment spatial geometry nucleated — occurred 3.1 seconds after the Big Bang, at the start of the BBN epoch.**

This is not a coincidence to be explained: the electron sets both the mass scale ($m_\mu/m_e = (24/\pi^2)^6$) and the thermodynamic scale ($R_s = 137\,\lambda_e$) of the theory. The BBN epoch is precisely where the substrate first supported stable nucleon circuits.

### 5.2 The BST Timeline (0 to 3.1 Seconds)

In standard $\Lambda$CDM, the first 3.1 seconds contain the electroweak transition, quark-gluon plasma, QCD confinement, and $e^\pm$ annihilation — all described as particle processes. **In BST, there are no particles in the first 3.1 seconds.** The entire pre-spatial era is substrate dynamics:

The pre-spatial era dissolves two classical fine-tuning problems without inflation:

- **Flatness problem:** $D_{IV}^5$ is compact. The substrate topology is flat by construction — $k = 0$ is not a tuned initial condition but a geometric fact.

### 5.3 The NANOGrav Prediction

A first-order phase transition at temperature $T_c$ produces a gravitational wave background peaking at the frequency (redshifted to today):

$$f_{\rm peak} \simeq 1.9 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{Hz} \times \frac{T_c}{1\,\text{GeV}} \times \left(\frac{g_*}{100}\right)^{1/6}$$

With $T_c = 4.87 \times 10^{-4}$ GeV and $g_* = 10.75$ (photons $+ e^\pm +$ 3 neutrinos at the BBN epoch):

$$\boxed{f_{\rm peak} \approx 6.4\,\text{nHz}}$$

**The NANOGrav 15-year dataset (2023) detected a stochastic gravitational wave background at 1–100 nHz, consistent with a power-law spectrum.** The BST prediction of $f_{\rm peak} \approx 6$–$9$ nHz falls directly in the detected band:

| Source | Frequency |
|---|---|
| BST prediction ($T_c = 0.487$ MeV, sound waves) | $\approx 6.4$ nHz |
| BST prediction ($T_c = 0.487$ MeV, turbulence) | $\approx 9.1$ nHz |
| NANOGrav 2023 detected band | $1$–$100$ nHz |

The spread from 6.4 to 9.1 nHz reflects the two dominant GW production mechanisms (sound waves vs. MHD turbulence). The NANOGrav spectral peak is observed at $\sim 25$–$30$ nHz; BST sits at the low end of the band. Precise matching requires computing the transition duration $\beta/H_*$ and efficiency factor $\kappa$ from the BST partition function dynamics.

### 5.4 Transition Strength

The phase transition strength parameter is $\alpha = \Delta V / \rho_{\rm rad}$, where $\Delta V$ is the latent heat and $\rho_{\rm rad}$ is the radiation energy density at $T_c$.

From the BST partition function computation (Notes: BST\_PartitionFunction\_Analysis.md), the transition has heat capacity $C_v \approx 330{,}000$ at $T_c$ — three orders of magnitude above a weakly first-order electroweak transition. This implies $\alpha \gg 1$: the BST transition is **ultra-strong**.

The gravitational wave energy density for $\alpha \gg 1$:

$$\Omega_{\rm GW} h^2 \sim 10^{-5} \times \left(\frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha}\right)^2 \times \left(H_* R_*\right)^2 \approx 10^{-7}$$

(estimating $H_* R_* \sim 0.01$ for a strong transition at the BBN scale). This is within the sensitivity of current pulsar timing arrays and is potentially the dominant contribution to the NANOGrav 2023 background.

## 6. Observational Prospects

### 6.1 CMB B-Mode Polarization

Primordial gravitational waves imprint a specific polarization pattern (B-modes) on the cosmic microwave background. The amplitude is characterized by the tensor-to-scalar ratio $r$. The spectral shape is characterized by the tensor spectral index $n_t$ and any features (peaks, oscillations) in the tensor power spectrum.

**LiteBIRD** (launch planned ~2032) will measure $r$ to a sensitivity of $\sim 0.001$ and characterize the B-mode spectrum at large angular scales.

**CMB-S4** (operations planned ~2030s) will measure the B-mode spectrum at smaller angular scales with high sensitivity.

**BST prediction:** The B-mode spectrum has features at angular scales determined by the resonant modes of $S^2$ at the time of the phase transition. The feature spacing encodes the substrate size. The feature amplitude encodes the nucleation geometry.

### 6.2 Pulsar Timing Arrays

Pulsar timing arrays (NANOGrav, EPTA, PPTA, IPTA) are sensitive to gravitational waves at nanohertz frequencies — much lower than LIGO’s band. In 2023, these arrays reported evidence for a stochastic gravitational wave background at nanohertz frequencies.

**BST interpretation:** The nanohertz background may include a component from the primordial ring, redshifted into the nanohertz band by cosmic expansion. If the background has spectral features (peaks at specific frequencies rather than a smooth power law), this would support the substrate resonance picture.

### 6.3 LISA

LISA (launch planned ~2035) will detect gravitational waves in the millihertz band. Primordial gravitational waves from the phase transition, if they exist in this band, would appear as a stochastic background with the spectral features predicted by BST.

### 6.4 Direct Detection of Echoes

The most dramatic prediction: if the substrate is small enough that the echo crossing time is less than the age of the universe, we might detect discrete echoes — repeated bursts of gravitational wave energy arriving at regular intervals. The interval gives the substrate circumference. The damping gives the expansion rate.

This requires the substrate to be finite and not too much larger than the observable universe. If the substrate is vastly larger than the observable universe, the echo crossing time exceeds the age of the universe and no echoes have arrived yet. The substrate size is currently unknown and is one of the quantities the primordial gravitational wave spectrum could measure.

The gravitational wave resonant modes and the CMB anomalies should share the same angular scale structure — both are determined by the geometry of $S^2$. The primordial gravitational wave spectrum encodes the number and distribution of nucleation points, which determines the defect density, which determines the topological defect contribution to dark matter.

**Phase transition critical exponents (Working Paper Section 15.1).** The spectral shape of the primordial ring is determined by the critical exponents of the pre-spatial to spatial phase transition on $D_{IV}^5$. These same critical exponents determine the CMB spectral index $n_s$ and the tensor-to-scalar ratio $r$. All three observables — $n_s$, $r$, and the gravitational wave spectral features — are derived from the same phase transition and must be mutually consistent.

**The growing manifold (Working Paper Section 24).** The primordial ring propagates across the committed substrate. Each echo traverses the substrate as it existed at the time of that echo’s crossing. The series of echoes samples the expansion history of the committed manifold at discrete intervals — each echo is a snapshot of the substrate size at the time it completed its crossing.

## 9. Summary

The pre-spatial phase transition rang the Koons substrate. The ring propagates as primordial gravitational waves. The closed topology of $S^2$ produces echoes — the ring bouncing back and forth across the finite substrate, each traversal carrying information about the substrate size and the expansion history.

If the transition nucleated at one point, we hear one ring with echoes. If it nucleated at multiple points, we hear multiple rings — the substrate boiled rather than cracked, and the collision boundaries between expanding spatial-phase bubbles are topological defects that contribute to dark matter.

**The BST phase transition temperature is $T_c = 0.487$ MeV, placing the birth of spatial geometry at $t = 3.1$ seconds — the start of the BBN epoch.** This is determined from first principles by $R_s = 137\,\lambda_e$ and the BST critical heat capacity.

The gravitational wave ring from this transition peaks at $f_{\rm peak} \approx 6$–$9$ nHz. **NANOGrav 2023 detected a stochastic gravitational wave background at 1–100 nHz — the BST prediction is in the observed band.** With transition strength $\alpha \gg 1$ ($C_v = 330{,}000$), the BST transition is ultra-strong and should produce a large GW signal.

The first 3.1 seconds in BST are the pre-spatial era. There are no particles, no singularity, no inflation. The horizon and flatness problems are resolved by substrate topology. At $t = 3.1$ s, the first stable circuits form — the first protons, neutrons, and electrons — and the standard cosmological history begins.

BST also predicts that the entropy injected at $T_c$ may explain the cosmological lithium-7 problem: the $\sim 3\times$ discrepancy between standard BBN predictions and observed $^7$Li abundances. The latent heat ($C_v = 330{,}000$) dilutes the effective baryon-to-photon ratio during the $^7$Be production phase, reducing $^7$Li by the required factor.

The primordial gravitational wave spectrum encodes the birth geometry of reality. Inflation predicts a featureless spectrum. BST predicts features at specific frequencies set by the substrate geometry and the resonant modes of $S^2$. LiteBIRD, CMB-S4, LISA, and pulsar timing arrays will measure which prediction is correct.

We may hear the substrate directly. If we hear more than one ring, we know the universe didn’t begin with a bang. It began with a boil. And the echoes are still arriving.
