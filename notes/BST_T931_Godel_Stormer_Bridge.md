# T931 — Gödel-Størmer Bridge: Finiteness of Dual Primes and the 137 Orphan

## Statement

**T931 (Gödel-Størmer Bridge)**: For the smooth set $S = \{2, 3, 5, 7\}$ determined by $D_{IV}^5$:

1. **Størmer-T914 Equivalence**: The Størmer dual primes for $S$ are EXACTLY the T914 dual-membership primes. A prime $p$ has dual membership if and only if both $p - 1$ and $p + 1$ are $S$-smooth.

2. **Finiteness**: There are exactly **17** such primes:

$$\mathcal{D}_S = \{2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 29, 31, 41, 71, 97, 127, 251, 449, 4801\}$$

3. **The 137 Orphan**: $N_{\max} = 137$ is NOT a dual prime. It is not even a single-membership prime: neither $136 = 2^3 \times 17$ nor $138 = 2 \times 3 \times 23$ is $S$-smooth. The spectral cap of $D_{IV}^5$ sits in the arithmetic desert — unreachable from the smooth lattice in either direction.

4. **BST Partition**: Of the 17 dual primes, exactly 14 lie below $N_{\max}$. The geometry provides a second finiteness (spectral truncation at 137) on top of the arithmetic finiteness (Størmer's theorem). These two finiteness mechanisms are independent but reinforce: the Størmer bound gives $|\mathcal{D}_S| = 17$; the spectral cap restricts to $|\mathcal{D}_S \cap [2, N_{\max}]| = 14$.

**The arithmetic says "finitely many." The geometry says "and no more than 14 matter." Both are right.**

## Proof

### Step 1: BST composites = 7-smooth numbers

By T926 (Spectral-Arithmetic Closure), the BST composite lattice is generated by $\{2, 3, 5, 6, 7\}$. Since $6 = 2 \times 3$, this equals the multiplicative monoid of $\{2, 3, 5, 7\}$-smooth numbers. The two sets are identical.

Therefore: T914's dual-membership condition ("$p$ is reachable from both $+1$ and $-1$ of BST composites") is equivalent to "$p - 1$ and $p + 1$ are both 7-smooth."

### Step 2: Størmer's theorem (1897) gives finiteness

**Størmer's Theorem**: For any finite set $S$ of primes, there are only finitely many primes $p$ such that both $p - 1$ and $p + 1$ are $S$-smooth.

The proof uses the theory of Pell equations. For each prime $p$ with both $p - 1 = m$ and $p + 1 = n$ being $S$-smooth, we have $n - m = 2$, so $n \times m = p^2 - 1$. The equation $x^2 - Dy^2 = 1$ (Pell) with $D = p$ has solutions parametrized by the fundamental solution, and the requirement that $x \pm 1$ are both $S$-smooth constrains the solutions to finitely many. Each prime $p$ with dual $S$-smooth neighbors corresponds to a solution of a specific Pell equation with $S$-smooth constraints.

This is the genuine depth-1 step: Størmer's proof requires the Pell equation theory, which is not a simple counting argument. The finiteness is a theorem of algebraic number theory, not combinatorics.

### Step 3: Explicit enumeration for $S = \{2, 3, 5, 7\}$

Computational verification (Toy 975):

- Search all primes $p \leq 100{,}000$
- Test whether both $p - 1$ and $p + 1$ factor into $\{2, 3, 5, 7\}$
- Result: exactly 17 primes satisfy the condition
- Largest: $p = 4801$ (with $4800 = 2^6 \times 3 \times 5^2$ and $4802 = 2 \times 7^4$)
- No duals found between 4801 and 100,000
- Størmer's theorem guarantees this list is complete

### Step 4: The 137 orphan proof

**Claim**: $N_{\max} = 137$ is not adjacent to any 7-smooth number.

**Proof**:
$$136 = 2^3 \times 17$$

Since $17 \notin \{2, 3, 5, 7\}$, the number 136 is NOT 7-smooth.

$$138 = 2 \times 3 \times 23$$

Since $23 \notin \{2, 3, 5, 7\}$, the number 138 is NOT 7-smooth.

Therefore 137 has no 7-smooth neighbor in either direction. It is an **orphan** — a prime completely isolated from the $S$-smooth lattice. $\square$

**Structural significance**: The spectral cap $N_{\max} = 137$ is the representation-theoretic bound of $D_{IV}^5$ (determined by $N_{\max} = \sum_{k=0}^{n_C - 1}\binom{n_C}{k} - 1$). The fact that this bound falls on a smooth-adjacency orphan is the coincidence of two independent finiteness mechanisms:

- **Arithmetic finiteness** (Størmer): The dual primes terminate at 4801. Single-membership primes thin out by Dickman's theorem.
- **Geometric finiteness** (spectral cap): The representation theory truncates at 137.

The two mechanisms meet at 137: the geometry truncates WHERE the arithmetic isolates. This is not coincidence — it is the content of T926 Corollary 2.

### Step 5: The two-finiteness structure

The dual primes partition into two sets relative to $N_{\max}$:

$$\mathcal{D}_S^{\text{in}} = \{2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 29, 31, 41, 71, 97, 127\} \quad (|\mathcal{D}_S^{\text{in}}| = 14)$$

$$\mathcal{D}_S^{\text{out}} = \{251, 449, 4801\} \quad (|\mathcal{D}_S^{\text{out}}| = 3)$$

The 14 interior duals are the primes that participate in BST physics at leading order. The 3 exterior duals ($251, 449, 4801$) exist arithmetically but are beyond the spectral cap — they correspond to representation-theoretic levels that $D_{IV}^5$ does not reach.

**Remark**: The ratio $14/17 = 82.4\%$ of duals lying below $N_{\max}$ says that the geometry captures most — but not all — of the arithmetic finiteness. The three exterior duals are a prediction: if BST is correct, these primes should NOT appear as leading-order physical constants. $\square$

## Corollaries

### Corollary 1: g = 7 is the structural bridge

$g = 7$ is the UNIQUE BST integer that is simultaneously:
- A Størmer dual prime ($6 = 2 \times 3$ and $8 = 2^3$ are both smooth)
- A Mersenne prime ($7 = 2^3 - 1 = 2^{N_c} - 1$)
- An observer-shifted prime ($7 = C_2 + 1 = 6 + 1$)

The genus bridges the Casimir ($C_2 = 6$) downward to the power-of-two lattice ($2^{N_c} = 8$) upward. This triple membership is unique among all 17 Størmer duals and reflects the central role of $g$ in $D_{IV}^5$.

### Corollary 2: The Mersenne duals

Three of the 17 Størmer duals are Mersenne primes:
- $7 = 2^3 - 1 = M_3$ (dual #4)
- $31 = 2^5 - 1 = M_5$ (dual #10)
- $127 = 2^7 - 1 = M_7$ (dual #14)

The Mersenne exponents are $\{3, 5, 7\} = \{N_c, n_C, g\}$ — exactly the odd BST primes. By T891 (Mersenne-Genus Bridge), this chain $M_{N_c} \to M_{n_C} \to M_g$ connects the three Mersenne primes through the BST integer hierarchy.

### Corollary 3: Consecutive smooth pairs vs dual primes

Toy 975 finds 23 consecutive 7-smooth pairs $(n, n+1)$ up to 100,000, but only 7 of these contain a prime. The other 16 pairs are $(n, n+1)$ where both are composite smooth numbers. The Størmer dual primes are the subset of consecutive smooth pairs where at least one element is prime.

The largest consecutive smooth pair is $(4374, 4375)$ where $4374 = 2 \times 3^7$ and $4375 = 5^4 \times 7$. Neither is prime, so this pair does not contribute a dual prime.

### Corollary 4: The density crossover

At scale $x$, the fraction of primes $\leq x$ that are adjacent to a 7-smooth number:

The density decreases monotonically (by Dickman's theorem, $\psi(x, 7)/x \to 0$). The Gödel fraction $f = N_c/(n_C\pi) = 19.1\%$ is crossed near $x \approx 5{,}000$. At the BST scale $N_{\max} = 137$, the density is far higher (87.9%) — almost every small prime has a smooth neighbor. The spectral cap is the mechanism that restricts the physically relevant primes to a finite catalog, not the thinning of smooth numbers.
