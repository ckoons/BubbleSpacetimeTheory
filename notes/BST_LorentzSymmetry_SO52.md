$$K_1 = M_{15}, \quad K_2 = M_{25}, \quad K_3 = M_{35}$$

$$[K_i, K_j] = -\epsilon_{ijk}\,J_k \qquad \text{(boost composition gives rotation)}$$

This is the standard Lorentz algebra $\mathfrak{so}(3,1)$, confirming the embedding.

## 3. The Speed of Light

### 3.1 Substrate derivation

In the BST contact graph:
- **Spatial distance** is measured in contacts: the number of bubble contacts between two points on $S^2$
- **Temporal distance** is measured in commitment steps: the number of irreversible state changes along the $S^1$ fiber

One commitment step advances the phase by one unit on $S^1$. The phase disturbance propagates through one contact on $S^2$ per commitment step. Therefore:

$$c = \frac{\text{1 contact}}{\text{1 commitment step}} = 1 \quad \text{(in natural units)}$$

The speed of light is the substrate propagation speed. It is not a "speed limit" imposed from outside — it is the rate at which commitment propagates through the contact graph. Nothing can travel faster because the contact graph IS the only medium, and one step per step is the maximum throughput of a single channel.

### 3.2 Why $c$ is constant

The speed of light is the same in all inertial frames because the contact graph has no preferred frame. The $\mathrm{SO}_0(5,2)$ isometry group acts transitively on $D_{IV}^5$: every point is equivalent to every other point. There is no special "rest frame" for the substrate.

The Maxwell wave equation (derived in BST_Maxwell.md) gives $c = 1$ directly:

$$\Box A^\mu = \left(\frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2} - \nabla^2\right) A^\mu = 0$$

where $c = 1$ because the $S^1$ phase oscillation and the $S^2$ spatial propagation use the same substrate clock.

### 3.3 Massless vs. massive propagation

- **Massless particles** (photon, gluon): propagate at $c$. These are phase oscillations on $S^1$ without net winding — no topological commitment, pure wave.
- **Massive particles** (electron, proton): propagate at $v < c$. These have topological winding (charge) or color closure ($Z_3$), requiring commitment expenditure that slows propagation.

$$E = \frac{mc^2}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} = \gamma\,mc^2$$

## 4. Lorentz Transformations

### 4.1 Boost along $x^1$

The boost generator $K_1 = M_{15}$ exponentiates to:

$$\Lambda(\phi) = e^{\phi\,K_1} = \begin{pmatrix} \cosh\phi & \sinh\phi \\ \sinh\phi & \cosh\phi \end{pmatrix}$$

acting on the $(x^1, x^5)$ = $(x, t)$ plane, with rapidity $\phi = \mathrm{arctanh}(v/c)$.

In coordinates:

$$t' = \gamma(t - vx/c^2), \qquad x' = \gamma(x - vt)$$

where $\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2} = \cosh\phi$.

### 4.2 Time dilation

$$\Delta t' = \gamma\,\Delta\tau$$

**BST interpretation:** A moving soliton expends commitment steps in both the spatial and temporal directions. The faster it moves spatially, the fewer commitment steps it can devote to temporal evolution. Time dilation is commitment budget reallocation: the total commitment rate is fixed at $c$, and motion through space costs commitment that would otherwise go to motion through time.

### 4.3 Length contraction

$$L' = L/\gamma$$

**BST interpretation:** The number of contacts along the moving soliton's spatial extent, as measured by the lab frame, is contracted because the soliton's commitment pattern is Lorentz-boosted. The contact graph itself doesn't shrink — but the projection of the soliton's extent onto the lab frame's spatial slice is shortened.

### 4.4 Relativity of simultaneity

Two events that are simultaneous in one frame ($\Delta t = 0$) are separated in time in a boosted frame ($\Delta t' = -\gamma v \Delta x/c^2$).

**BST interpretation:** Simultaneity is frame-dependent because commitment ordering on the contact graph is only well-defined along a single worldline. Two spatially separated events have no unique temporal ordering until a signal (commitment chain) connects them. This is the causal structure of the contact graph: only events connected by commitment chains have a definite ordering.

## 5. The Energy-Momentum Relation

### 5.1 Derivation from the geodesic equation

The 4-momentum of a soliton is $p^\mu = m\,dx^\mu/d\tau$. The norm of the 4-momentum:

$$g_{\mu\nu}\,p^\mu\,p^\nu = m^2\,c^2$$

$$E^2/c^2 - |\mathbf{p}|^2 = m^2\,c^2$$

or equivalently:

$$\boxed{E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2}$$

- $mc^2$ is the rest energy: the Casimir eigenvalue of the soliton's Bergman representation, in energy units. For the proton: $m_p c^2 = C_2\,\pi^{n_C}\,m_e c^2 = 6\pi^5\,m_e c^2 = 938.272$ MeV.

- The total energy $E$ is the Pythagorean combination: the mass-shell condition.

The two Casimir invariants of the Poincare group:
