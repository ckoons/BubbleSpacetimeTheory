## 3. Why semi-stability truncates at ONE excitation: m_π threshold

The strong-decay channel **Δ → N + π** opens when the excitation energy exceeds m_π ≈ 140 MeV.

| Decay | Q-value | Channel | Lifetime |
|---|---|---|---|
| n → p + e⁻ + ν̄ | ~0.78 MeV | Weak only | 14.6 min (semi-stable) |
| Δ → N + π | ~150 MeV | Strong open | 10⁻²³ s (resonance) |

The 0.78 MeV n-p mass split is BELOW m_π by a factor ~180, so the neutron has only the weak channel available → semi-stable. Any excitation above m_π threshold gets the strong channel → resonance.

**So the truncation isn't trajectory-truncation. It's strong-decay-threshold truncation.** The trajectory may continue indefinitely; semi-stability stops at the m_π cutoff.

## 4. Why lepton trajectory has THREE semi-stable points but nucleon has TWO

| | Strong interaction | Decay channels | Trajectory semi-stable count |
|---|---|---|---|
| Leptons | NONE | Weak only | 3 (e stable, μ 2.2μs, τ 0.3ps — all weak) |
| Nucleons | YES | Weak below m_π, strong above | 2 (p stable, n semi-stable; above m_π → resonances) |

The substrate-architectural trajectory may have the same number of distinguished points in both cases — but for nucleons, semi-stability is cut off by the strong-decay threshold while the trajectory continues.

**Honest structural answer to Casey**: the m_π threshold is the cutoff, not the trajectory length.
