# F74 — PMNS angles are combinatorial: the substrate counts mixing

## 0. The placement (Casey's PMNS assignment, on Grace's trichotomy)

Grace's trichotomy (F73) sorts substrate observables by π-content into three operations: VOLUME (π^{n_C}, measure a volume), SPECTRAL (π^{rank}, measure per-mode), COMBINATORIAL (π⁰, count). The PMNS mixing angles place cleanly and predictably:

> **PMNS mixing angles are COMBINATORIAL (π⁰).** A mixing angle is a *dimensionless overlap* between basis states — a ratio of K-type overlaps — so it carries no measure and no π. The substrate **counts** the mixing; it does not measure it.

This extends the trichotomy from **masses** to **mixing**, and sharpens what the trichotomy means: masses are *geometric-measure* objects (a volume π^{n_C} for composites, a per-mode measure π^{rank} for point modes), while mixing angles are *combinatorial-overlap* objects (π⁰). The π-content distinguishes "measure" observables (masses) from "overlap" observables (mixing).

## 3. Why combinatorial (the geometric reading)

A mixing angle is the overlap ⟨a|b⟩ between two K-type basis states (mass basis vs flavor basis). An overlap is a **projection coefficient** — a discrete, dimensionless count of shared structure, not an integral over a volume or a spectral density. So it carries no π (no measure). The PMNS angles all come from **one** substrate-K-type cross-K-type primitive (Cal #36 cluster) — i.e., one combinatorial overlap structure generating the three angles. That is the substrate "counting" the flavor/mass overlap. Contrast: a mass is "how much substrate volume is wound" (π^{n_C}) or "the per-mode spectral measure" (π^{rank}) — a measure, hence π. Mixing is counting; mass is measuring.
