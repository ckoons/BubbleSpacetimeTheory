## The shared idea (why condensed matter can test BST)
BST's substrate is a bounded symmetric domain whose **boundary conditions select the eigenvalue spectrum** (the same mechanism that gives the SM mode counts). If that is physical rather than numerological, then engineering a boundary tuned to the BST integers (N_max=137, g=7, N_c=3, n_C=5) should produce a **predicted spectral feature** where a generic structure would not. The Casimir effect is the existing proof-of-concept that boundary geometry sets the vacuum mode spectrum; the other two are cheaper, sharper bets.

## Package 1 — Photonic crystal (CHEAPEST clean falsification, ~$10K)
- **BST prediction:** a 1D/2D photonic crystal whose layer count / mode structure is tuned to the BST integers exhibits a **mode-count / band-edge feature at the predicted integer** (e.g. a 137-fold or g=7-periodic resonance) that a detuned control crystal lacks.
- **Protocol:** fabricate two photonic crystals — one BST-tuned, one a detuned control — and measure the transmission/reflection spectrum. Look for the predicted feature in the tuned sample and its absence in the control.
- **Cost:** ~$10K (standard photonic-crystal fabrication + optical spectroscopy; the cheapest clean test in the program).
- **What refutes BST:** the BST-tuned crystal shows **no** spectral feature at the predicted integer that the control lacks — i.e. the tuning does nothing. A null result here is a clean, cheap refutation of the spectral-engineering claim.

## Package 2 — BaTiO₃ 137-plane (the KILLER test, ~$25K)
- **BST prediction:** a BaTiO₃ (or comparable ferroelectric) heterostructure built with a **137-plane** boundary condition shows a distinctive response (dielectric/phonon-mode/polarization feature) tied to N_max=137 — the sharpest single-integer test, because 137 is BST's most specific integer.
- **Protocol:** grow the 137-plane structure (MBE / pulsed-laser deposition), measure the dielectric/phonon spectrum, compare to controls at 136 and 138 planes. The prediction is a feature that peaks at exactly 137, not its neighbors.
- **Cost:** ~$25K (thin-film growth + characterization).
- **What refutes BST:** the 137-plane structure behaves **identically** to its 136/138 neighbors — no special feature at 137. Since 137 is BST's signature integer, a null here is the strongest single-experiment falsifier.

## Package 3 — Casimir spectral engineering (proof-of-concept, ~$60–90K)
- **BST prediction:** the Casimir force between plates with a **BST-tuned boundary** (surface structure keyed to the substrate integers) deviates from the standard smooth-plate Casimir prediction in a **predicted, integer-keyed** way — because the boundary selects which vacuum modes contribute.
- **Protocol:** precision Casimir-force measurement (AFM/MEMS cantilever) between a BST-structured surface and a control smooth surface; compare the measured force-vs-distance to the standard Casimir curve. The prediction is a specific deviation at the tuned scale.
- **Cost:** ~$60–90K (precision Casimir apparatus; more expensive, but it is the existing proof-of-concept that boundaries set the spectrum).
- **What refutes BST:** the BST-structured surface gives the **standard** Casimir force with no integer-keyed deviation — the boundary tuning has no spectral effect beyond ordinary geometry.
